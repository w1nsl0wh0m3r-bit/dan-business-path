1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A local service company selling alteration / wardrobe repair membership to households, clubs, property managers, or affluent consumers. The investable version has repeat demand, documented customer lists, and a non-owner production team.
  • Who are the customers? Mix varies by category: B2C households/parents/affluent consumers plus B2B clubs, property managers, HOAs, schools, restaurants, or small commercial accounts.
  • Demand drivers: recurring maintenance, emergency need, compliance, youth-sports/college pressure, home comfort, or wardrobe-preservation convenience depending on category.
  • Geographic relevance: Strong in NE/NY/SFL where density, affluent suburbs, old housing stock, humidity/pests, and service fragmentation support local route economics.
  • Market size: Use public industry reports as directional only; local market underwriting should be built bottom-up from target territory households/businesses and seller financials.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Revenue range: $250K-$900K; avoid subscale owner-job unless it has uniquely dense customers.
  • SDE range: $200K-$500K preferred; below $150K must be a tuck-in or founder-led startup wedge.
  • Years in business: 5+ years with transferable reputation and repeat customers.
  • Route density / customer count: enough density that one crew/tech/editor/tailor can fill days without long windshield time.
  • Key assets to verify: customer list, recurring contracts/memberships, staff retention, licenses/certifications, equipment, reviews, CRM/job history.
  • Red flags to avoid: owner is the only producer, cash revenue not in tax returns, no customer data, weak reviews, deferred payroll/equipment, heavy one-customer concentration.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Top relevant brands/benchmarks: Local tailors, dry cleaners, Nordstrom/local department-store alterations, The Seam, Hemster, Wishi/closet stylists, clothing repair apps.
  • FDD highlight: request Item 19, initial investment, royalty/brand fund, territory, renewal, and transfer language; do not rely on marketing averages.
  • Unit economics from franchise disclosure: use only disclosed Item 19 or franchisee calls; otherwise label as franchisor estimate.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Top competitors or roll-ups active in this space: Local tailors, dry cleaners, Nordstrom/local department-store alterations, The Seam, Hemster, Wishi/closet stylists, clothing repair apps.
  • PE activity: highest in pest, HVAC, plumbing and broader home services; lower but emerging in niche wardrobe and sports-content services.
  • Fragmentation level: generally fragmented locally, with national brands setting price umbrellas in home services and software/platform brands in youth sports.
  • Moat: local reviews, recurring customer base, response speed, technicians/operators, licenses, club/property-manager relationships, and documented workflows.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Typical deal structure: asset purchase with 10%-20% seller note/earnout tied to customer retention; stock purchase only if licenses/contracts require and liabilities are diligence-clean.
  • SBA eligibility and typical loan terms: SBA 7(a) can finance operating businesses with cash flow; underwrite 10-year amortization, ~11% rate, 10% equity injection.
  • Down payment required at Dan's scale: roughly $75K-$225K for $750K-$2.25M purchase prices.
  • Payback period at base case: 2-4 years if seller note, manager salary, and maintenance capex are honestly included.
  • Honest multiple arbitrage: buy a clean small business around 3.5-4.0x SDE, professionalize systems, grow to $500K+ SDE, and potentially exit around ~4x to a strategic/local consolidator.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Market mapping, broker outreach, owner calls, franchise FDD requests, price scrape, and 10 operator/customer interviews in Boston/NY/SFL.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Operator partner search, financial model, route density map, tech stack review, and site/ride-along visits with 3-5 targets.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit one LOI or kill; if franchise/greenfield, complete FDD review and unit-level reference calls before signing.

8. Kill Criteria

What would make this a NO-GO?

  • Seller cannot tie P&L to tax returns, bank deposits, and job/customer history.
  • Owner is the only sales channel and production bottleneck.
  • No path to recruit/retain an operator or licensed qualifier where required.
  • Revenue is mostly one-time, discount-driven, or from one large customer.
  • Base-case SBA debt service leaves less than 1.25x DSCR after manager salary and capex.
  • Local competition shows price compression with no differentiated service wedge.
