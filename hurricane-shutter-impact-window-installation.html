1. Category Definition & Demand Map

Hurricane shutter and impact window installation provides code-compliant storm protection for residential and commercial properties in coastal hurricane zones. Products include accordion shutters, roll-down shutters, impact-resistant windows, storm panels, and hurricane fabric. Revenue comes from new installation ($2,000-25,000 per project), replacement of aging systems, and repair after storm damage. The business requires contractor licensing, building code knowledge, and relationships with insurance companies and building departments.

Demand drivers and recurrence: Florida building code (the strictest in the nation) requires hurricane-rated protection on all new construction and major renovations in wind-borne debris regions. Insurance companies mandate or strongly incentivize hurricane protection with premium discounts. Post-hurricane repair and replacement creates demand spikes. Aging housing stock requires system replacement every 15-20 years. Climate change is expanding hurricane zones northward, creating new markets in the Carolinas and mid-Atlantic.

Geographic fit: Florida is the primary market (by far), followed by Gulf Coast (Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama), coastal Carolinas, and to a lesser extent the mid-Atlantic coast. Miami-Dade and Broward County have the strictest codes and highest demand. Secondary Florida markets (Tampa, Jacksonville, Panhandle) are growing rapidly.

Core buyer
Homeowners in coastal hurricane zones, typically triggered by insurance requirement, new home purchase, or post-storm repair. Secondary: commercial property owners and HOAs managing multi-unit buildings. Real estate agents refer buyers who need compliance for closing.
Revenue engine
New construction compliance (builders must install hurricane protection). Insurance-mandated upgrades (homeowners installing to get coverage or discounts). Post-storm repair and replacement (insurance claim-driven). Aging system replacement (15-20 year product lifecycle). Renovation and addition triggers (building permit requires compliance on the entire structure).
Hard part
Navigating the building code and permit process is the hardest operational challenge. Each jurisdiction has different requirements, and the Miami-Dade County product approval process (NOA - Notice of Acceptance) is the gold standard that most other jurisdictions reference. Installers who understand the code and can handle permitting efficiently have a genuine competitive advantage.

Why this path (Acquisition) for this category: Acquisition is preferred because the contractor licensing, building code expertise, insurance company relationships, and permit navigation capabilities take years to develop. An established installer with 5+ years of history and strong local relationships is worth the acquisition premium. Starting from scratch requires 1-2 years of licensing, relationship building, and code education before meaningful revenue.

2. Economics to Underwrite

Hurricane protection installation generates 35-45% gross margins. A typical residential impact window installation of $15,000 has $6,000-8,000 in materials (impact windows, hardware), $2,000-3,000 in labor (1-2 days for a 2-person crew), leaving $4,000-7,000 in gross profit. Hurricane shutters have slightly lower average project values ($4,000-8,000) but similar margin profiles.

Key insight: The key metric is revenue per installation crew per month. A two-person crew completing 8-12 installations per month at $10,000 average project value generates $80-120K in monthly revenue. During peak season (May-July), crews can run at 120% capacity. Off-season (Nov-Feb) drops to 50-60%.

5. Competitive Landscape

Competition comes from general contractors offering hurricane protection as add-on, dedicated hurricane shutter and window companies, window replacement companies (Window World, Renewal by Andersen) adding impact products, and DIY hurricane panels from big-box stores. The advantage of a dedicated specialist is code expertise, efficient permitting, insurance company trust, and product range.

Where the whitespace is: Whitespace in insurance company direct partnerships (preferred installer programs), smart/automated hurricane protection (motorized roll-downs with app control), and expanding markets (Carolinas, mid-Atlantic) where hurricane codes are being adopted.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

Valuation frame
3-4x SDE for established installers. A company at $1.2M revenue with $178K SDE acquires for $534-712K. Working capital (inventory) adds $50-100K. Strong insurance referral relationships can justify premium multiples.
Financing frame
SBA 7(a) for acquisition. Material inventory financing or supplier terms. SBA 504 for warehouse/office real estate. Seller note for 15-25% alignment.

Capital discipline rules: Never pay more than 4x SDE. Verify insurance referral relationships transfer post-sale. Budget for material inventory on top of purchase price. Require 6-month transition with owner maintaining insurance and building department relationships. Model at 85% of trailing revenue for post-acquisition adjustment.

For a small business path, the danger is not missing a huge market; it is buying or building a job. The model should include a downside case with 15% lower revenue, 10% higher labor, slower ramp, and real replacement management. If that case cannot survive, the category belongs in watchlist, not pursue.

11. Operator Reality

These are not hypothetical. They come from operator interviews, Reddit threads, YouTube walkthroughs, and industry forums. Take them seriously.

Where operators win

  • Non-discretionary demand driven by code and insurance
  • Insurance premium discounts pay for installations over time, making the sale easier
  • Building code expertise is a genuine competitive moat
  • Growing market as codes expand geographically
  • Product replacement cycle creates recurring demand
  • Post-hurricane repair provides demand spikes with insurance funding

Where owners lose money

  • Over-relying on seasonal peak revenue without planning for off-season
  • Underpricing to win volume when margins are the key to sustainability
  • Not building insurance company referral partnerships
  • Ignoring permit process efficiency (slow permitting kills conversion)
  • Failing to maintain crew quality and safety standards
  • Not diversifying beyond shutters into the higher-value impact window segment

The one thing operators consistently say: The installers who win are the ones who make the insurance and permit process invisible for the homeowner. If the customer has to navigate code compliance themselves, you have failed at your core value proposition.

13. Dashboard Score Reality Check

The hurricane protection score holds only in coastal code-and-insurance markets where compliance is unavoidable. It is not a generic window contractor thesis; the edge is permitting, product approvals, fast quoting, and reliable crews in a market where PGT, ES Windows/Tecnoglass, CWS, and local dealers shape pricing.

What holds up

  • The demand score holds because Florida code, insurance discounts, lender requirements, and storm memory create non-discretionary retrofit demand.
  • Code expertise is a real moat: Miami-Dade NOA approvals, HVHZ rules, permit packages, and inspection discipline separate serious installers from handymen.
  • Ticket size can be excellent on whole-home impact window and door projects, especially where homeowners bundle protection, energy efficiency, and resale value.
  • Insurance-agent and HOA channels are underrated if they produce warm leads before storm season rather than commodity post-storm bidding.
  • The score should move up if the target has diversified supplier access and can switch between PGT, ES Windows, CWS, and shutter lines without margin collapse.

What is overstated

  • This is overrated if it assumes all storm demand is profitable. Post-storm surges can bring labor chaos, angry customers, permitting delays, and margin leakage.
  • The score should move down if revenue depends on one manufacturer, one permit runner, or one licensed qualifier.
  • Material volatility is not abstract: glass, aluminum, freight, tariff exposure, and manufacturer lead times can turn quoted jobs into margin traps.
  • The honest downside risk is geographic concentration plus regulatory exposure — a license issue, failed inspection pattern, or supplier backlog can stop revenue fast.
  • Replacement-cycle recurring revenue is weaker than the current score implies; most revenue is project-based unless service, screens, shutters, and warranty work are actively sold.
