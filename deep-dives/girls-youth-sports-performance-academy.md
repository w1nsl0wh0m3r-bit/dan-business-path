# Girls / Youth Sports Performance Academy Deep Dive
*Generated: 2026-05-25 | Confidence: High for D1 franchise data; Medium for girls-specific niche sizing.*

## 1. Executive Verdict — CONDITIONAL GO
Youth sports performance is compelling because parents spend aggressively, youth sports participation has rebounded, and D1 Training-type economics can be strong once mature. But the category is crowded with local coaches, club teams, and gyms; acquisition targets may be personality-led. A girls-specific positioning could differentiate, especially around injury prevention, strength/confidence, volleyball/soccer/lacrosse, and recruiting prep — but must not shrink TAM too far. Best route: buy/partner with an operator-coach and build a girls-forward academy inside a proven performance model.

## 2. Market Sizing
- Aspen Institute Project Play / SFIA data indicate youth sports participation has recovered post-COVID; search summary cites 65% of ages 6-17 trying a sport at least once and parent spending over **$40B annually**. Treat the $40B as Aspen-estimated parent spend, not facility/operator revenue.
- Aspen’s 2024/2025 reporting highlights average family spending around **$1,016 per child for a main sport**, up materially vs. 2019, with higher-income families spending much more.
- The addressable market for a New England academy is local: affluent suburbs with soccer, lacrosse, volleyball, basketball, softball, and hockey participation.

## 3. Unit Economics
D1 Training 2023 FDD data via Franchise Chatter:
- Initial investment: **$164,250-$666,219**.
- Franchise fee: **$59,500**; opening support fee **$29,500**.
- Royalty: greater of **7% of gross sales** or minimum monthly royalty.
- Marketing fund: **$250/month**; local marketing requirement then-currently **$100/day**; co-op up to 1.5%.
- Mature >2-year franchised locations (13 units): average gross revenue **$870,884**; median **$853,513**; average revenue less disclosed expenses **$379,867**. Note this excludes rent and other undisclosed expenses in the displayed metric.
- 1-2-year franchised locations (22 units): average gross revenue **$530,036**; median **$486,995**; average revenue less disclosed expenses **$236,662**.
Source: Franchise Chatter D1 Training FDD review ([Franchise Chatter](https://www.franchisechatter.com/2024/02/17/fdd-talk-d1-training-franchise-costs-fees-average-revenues-and-or-profits-2024-review/)).

**Manager-run estimate:**
- Stabilized revenue: $500K-$900K.
- Fully loaded EBITDA after rent/utilities/insurance not fully disclosed: likely 10-20% for competent operators.
- After GM/head coach at $70K-$110K, cash flow can range from breakeven to $150K+.
- Payback: 3-6 years depending capex and ramp.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

## 6. Operator Economics
This is operator-led. A credible head coach must manage programming, parent sales, athlete retention, school/club relationships, and coach quality. Budget $70K-$110K plus performance bonus/equity kicker. A unit doing $550K revenue may not support Dan + GM meaningfully after rent and marketing. The underwriting target should be $800K+ revenue potential or acquisition at low basis.

## 7. Acquisition Targets
Look for:
- Sports performance gyms with $400K-$1M revenue and 100+ recurring members/athletes.
- Volleyball/soccer/lacrosse training businesses with girls participation and club relationships.
- Physical therapy / injury-prevention adjacency, but avoid medical licensing complexity unless partner-led.
- Owner-coach willing to stay as minority partner/operator.
Multiples: 2.0-3.0x SDE for founder-led gyms; 3.0-4.0x if manager-run, recurring contracts, clean books, and diversified coaches.

## 8. Dan Fit — 3.9 / 5
Good fit with Dan’s sports background and consumer diligence. The girls/youth angle is personally intuitive and locally brandable. The issue is execution: if the head coach leaves, the asset can evaporate. Operator partnership is mandatory.

## 9. 90-Day Roadmap
1. Obtain D1, Redline, Parisi FDDs; compare Item 19, closures, territories, required marketing.
2. Interview 10 parents of girls in club soccer/lacrosse/volleyball in New England; test willingness to pay $150-$300/month.
3. Call 5 club directors; ask what training gaps exist and whether they would refer.
4. Identify 3 operator candidates: former college S&C coaches, club directors, PT/performance coaches.
5. Underwrite one pop-up pilot: 8-week girls speed/strength/injury-prevention clinic with prepayment.

## 10. Kill Criteria
- No credible operator/head coach.
- CAC relies on paid ads instead of club/school referrals.
- Revenue per athlete below $150/month with high facility rent.
- Churn >10% monthly outside seasonal cycles.
- Franchise territory/fee rules prevent local partnership flexibility.
- Girls-specific positioning reduces demand instead of improving conversion.
