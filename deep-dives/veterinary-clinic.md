# Veterinary Clinic — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL GO
Veterinary services is a $50B+ recession-resilient market with sticky recurring revenue (pets need annual care). The thesis: acquire a small-animal general practice in a suburban market, hire a vet to operate, and collect steady cash flow. The risk: vet shortages are acute, and finding a vet willing to work for your practice (vs. corporate) is the binding constraint. This works only if the practice has an established vet who will stay post-sale.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- **What is this business?** A veterinary clinic provides medical care for companion animals — wellness exams, vaccinations, dental cleanings, surgery, diagnostics, and emergency care. Small-animal general practice (dogs, cats) is the target. Exotic, equine, and large-animal are separate categories with different economics.
- **Who are the customers?** 100% B2C — pet owners. US pet ownership: ~66% of households (87M+ homes). Millennials are the largest pet-owning cohort and spend more per pet than prior generations.
- **Demand drivers:** Pet humanization trend (owners spend more per animal), aging pet population (more chronic care), pet insurance adoption (reduces price sensitivity), post-COVID pet boom (sustained higher ownership rates). Veterinary care is non-discretionary for responsible pet owners.
- **Geographic relevance:** Suburban markets with high household income and pet ownership rates are ideal. NE/NY/SFL all strong. Florida is particularly attractive (retirees with pets, year-round parasite prevention needs). Boston suburbs (Metrowest, North Shore) and Westchester/Fairfield County are prime territory.
- **Market size:** US veterinary services market ~$55-60B (AVMA 2024 data, estimate). ~30,000 small-animal practices in the US. Growing 6-8% annually (above GDP) driven by pricing power and service expansion.

## 3. Unit Economics Model (Three-Case)

Model assumes a well-established suburban small-animal practice, 2-3 vets on staff, 4-5 support staff.

| Case | Revenue | SDE | SDE Margin | Entry Price | Multiple | Cash-on-Cash Year 1 |
|------|---------|-----|-----------|-------------|----------|---------------------|
| Bear | $800K | $100K | 12.5% | $350K | 3.5x | $5K |
| Base | $1.3M | $210K | 16% | $735K | 3.5x | $45K |
| Bull | $2.0M | $380K | 19% | $1.52M | 4.0x | $85K |

**Key assumptions:**
- Entry multiples: 3-4.5x SDE for independent practices (well below corporate multiples of 6-10x EBITDA)
- SBA 7(a): 10-year term, ~11% rate (2026), 10% down
- Cash-on-cash = (SDE - debt service) / down payment
- Operator partner: hire an experienced practice manager + retain selling vet on 2-3 year employment agreement
- Revenue includes a realistic mix of wellness (55%), surgery/procedures (25%), and ancillary (pharmacy, boarding, grooming = 20%)

**SBA debt service on base case:** $735K loan at 11%, 10yr = ~$10,100/mo or $121K/yr. Base SDE of $210K minus $121K = $89K free cash. On $73.5K down payment = 121% cash-on-cash by Year 2.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox

**What makes a GOOD acquisition target:**
- Revenue range: $800K-$2M
- SDE range: $120K-$350K
- Years in business: 10+ (established client base, goodwill)
- Active client count: 3,000+ active patients (visited in last 18 months)
- Vet staff: 2+ associate vets willing to stay post-sale (binding constraint)
- Key assets: veterinary licenses (transferable), medical equipment (digital X-ray, in-house lab), client database/PIM software, lease or real estate
- Real estate: owning the building is a significant plus — adds $150-300K in value and eliminates lease risk
- Red flags: single vet (owner) practice, no associate vet; aging equipment needing $100K+ replacement; declining active patient count; pending state board complaints

**What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate:**
- Vet franchises are limited. Most growth is via corporate consolidation (VCA, Banfield, National Veterinary Associates).
- **Banfield Pet Hospital** (Mars Inc.) — franchise model, ~1,000 hospitals (mostly inside PetSmart). Not independently acquirable.
- **VCA Animal Hospitals** (Mars Inc.) — ~1,200 hospitals, corporate-owned only.
- **National Veterinary Associates (NVA)** — PE-backed (JAB Holding), ~1,000 practices, acquirer not franchisor.
- **PetVet Care Centers** — PE-backed, acquirer.
- **Takeaway:** Franchise route is essentially unavailable for independent vet practices. The path is acquisition of an independent practice or joining a corporate network (which defeats the purpose).

## 5. Competitive Landscape

**Fragmentation level:** Moderate and declining rapidly. ~30,000 practices, but corporate consolidation has accelerated since 2017. Top 10 corporate groups now own ~25-30% of all US practices.

**Key players:**
- **Mars Inc. (VCA + Banfield + BluePearl)** — largest vet company globally, ~2,200+ US hospitals. Deep pockets, premium acquisitions.
- **National Veterinary Associates (NVA)** — PE-backed (JAB Holding), ~1,000 practices. Aggressive acquirer of independents.
- **PetVet Care Centers** — PE-backed, ~400 practices.
- **Thrifty Pet / VetCor** — PE-backed rollups targeting smaller practices.
- **Independents** — still majority of market (~70%) but declining share.

**PE activity:** Intense. JAB Holding, KKR (previously invested in NVA), Ontario Teachers', and others have poured billions into vet consolidation. This has inflated multiples for quality practices (corporate buyers pay 6-10x EBITDA) but also created a bifurcated market — lower-revenue practices that corporates ignore trade at 3-4x SDE.

**Moat:** Client relationships (pet owners are very sticky — average client stays 7+ years with same vet), location/convenience, vet talent. The moat is the DVM — if they leave, clients follow.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

- **Typical deal structure:** Asset sale (equipment, client lists, goodwill, assumed lease). Real estate may be separate. Seller employment agreement is standard (selling vet stays 1-3 years). Seller notes common (15-25%).
- **SBA eligibility:** Yes, veterinary practices are SBA 7(a) eligible. Good practice brokers specialize in vet transitions. SBA-friendly lenders include Live Oak Bank, First Financial (both active in vet lending).
- **Down payment at Dan's scale:** $35K-$75K on a $350K-$750K acquisition.
- **Payback period at base case:** ~3.5 years.
- **Multiple arbitrage:** Buy independent at 3-3.5x → improve operations → sell to corporate consolidator at 5-7x EBITDA (if practice qualifies). This is the classic vet PE arbitrage, though the window is narrowing as corporates get pickier.

**Capital discipline rules:**
- Never pay >4x SDE for a single-practice independent
- Require seller vet to sign 2-year employment agreement with non-compete
- Verify active patient count independently (PIM software report, not seller's word)
- Walk away if practice has only one vet and no associate candidate lined up
- Budget $50-100K for equipment upgrades in first 12 months

## 8. Kill Criteria

- Practice has only one vet (the seller) and no associate candidate
- Seller vet won't sign a minimum 18-month employment agreement
- Active patient count declining >10% year-over-year for 2+ years
- State requires DVM ownership and Dan can't find a vet partner
- Corporate consolidators have swept the market — nothing available below 5x SDE
- Equipment is outdated and needs $150K+ investment
- Lease expiring within 18 months with landlord pushing for 30%+ rent increase
- Yelp/Google reviews consistently mention long wait times, rude staff, or misdiagnosis
