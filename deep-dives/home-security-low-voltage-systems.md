# Home Security / Low-Voltage Systems — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: GO
One of the cleaner operator-partner theses: fragmented, local, recurring monitoring/service revenue, and affluent-market demand for cameras, access control, Wi-Fi, AV and smart-home maintenance.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? Low-voltage contractors install and service security alarms, cameras, access control, structured wiring, networking, smart-home controls, lighting controls and sometimes AV systems. The acquisition target is not a pure one-time installer; it should have monitoring, inspection, service-plan or managed-network recurrence.
- Who are the customers? B2C affluent homeowners, second-home owners and small multifamily; B2B/light commercial offices, restaurants, medical offices, schools and property managers.
- Demand drivers: Aging security systems, package theft, remote-work network needs, insurance/compliance expectations, camera upgrades, access-control migration and wealthy households willing to pay for responsive local service.
- Geographic relevance: Excellent NE/NY/SFL relevance: dense affluent housing stock in Boston suburbs, Westchester/CT/NJ, Long Island, Manhattan/Brooklyn brownstones and South Florida second-home/condo markets.
- Market size: U.S. security and alarm services are tracked by IBISWorld and the Security Industry Association; exact local low-voltage TAM should be built from households/businesses, monitoring accounts and install tickets. Grand View Research and Fortune Business Insights report large and growing smart-home/security markets, but those are broad hardware/service proxies.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue range: $1M-$3M with 300+ recurring accounts or defensible service contracts.
- SDE range: $200K-$600K after replacing owner with sales/ops lead.
- 7+ years in business; clean licensing and low-voltage permits.
- Dense account base by affluent ZIP/property manager; no builder >25% of revenue.
- Verify RMR schedule, attrition, monitoring contracts, licenses, truck/equipment, tech certifications, manufacturer relationships and backlog.
- Avoid new-construction cyclicality, one master technician, obsolete panels, unpaid permit issues, weak documentation or truck-roll chaos.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Relevant brands: Alarm 360, Signal Security/security-adjacent, HelloTech/smart-home support, Screenmobile-style home-service benchmarks; many strong independents are better than franchises.
- FDD focus: franchise fee/royalty, required vendors, territory, license responsibility and whether monitoring accounts are owned by franchisee.
- Prefer independents or dealer programs where Dan owns customer/RMR; avoid deals where the monitoring company captures all enterprise value.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- ADT, Vivint, Brinks Home, Alarm.com dealer networks, Johnson Controls/Tyco, Convergint in commercial, local AV/security installers and electrical contractors. PE is active in security integration and fire/life-safety platforms; local residential/small-commercial remains fragmented. Moat is RMR ownership, licenses, trusted techs, fast service and embedded home/business systems.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Asset purchase with contract assignment and seller note for RMR retention. SBA is attractive because goodwill is supported by recurring accounts; lenders will focus on DSCR after technician/manager comp. Down payment for a $1.2M base deal is ~$120K plus working capital. Honest arbitrage: 3.5-4x SDE in; 4-5x possible if recurring revenue and manager bench are proven.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Build NE/NY/SFL target list from Google Maps, Alarm.com dealers, low-voltage license lists and broker listings; request RMR by account cohort.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Operator-partner search among security/AV ops managers; diligence licenses, contracts, attrition and truck-level productivity.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): LOI only on a target with transferable RMR and a second-in-command; otherwise keep mapping.

## 8. Kill Criteria
- Recurring monitoring/service revenue below 20% with no path to build it.
- Customer contracts not assignable or RMR owned by third-party dealer.
- License/permit issues or uninsurable installation history.
- One technician holds all customer knowledge.
- Builder/new-construction concentration masks weak recurring economics.
