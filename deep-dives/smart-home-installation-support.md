# Smart Home Installation / Support — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Growing tailwind from IoT adoption and energy management, but the category is fragmented between low-voltage electricians, AV integrators, and general handymen. Best path: acquire a low-voltage / security integration shop with smart-home add-on revenue, not a pure-play smart-home installer. Recurring monitoring/service contracts are the prize — pure installation is project-based with lumpy revenue.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? A smart home installation and support company designs, installs, configures, and maintains connected home systems — security cameras, smart locks, thermostats, lighting control, voice assistants, motorized shades, whole-home Wi-Fi, and integrated control platforms (Control4, Savant, Crestron, or consumer-grade like Ring/Nest). Revenue comes from installation projects, ongoing service/maintenance contracts, monitoring subscriptions, and equipment markup.
- Who are the customers? B2C — affluent homeowners building or renovating ($500K+ homes). Secondary B2B — small businesses needing security/access control. Builder/contractor channel for new construction pre-wire. 80/20 split: 80% residential, 20% light commercial.
- Demand drivers: US smart home market growing 15-20% annually (Statista estimates ~$40B US market by 2026). New construction increasingly includes pre-wire for smart systems. Aging-in-place demand drives automated lighting, locks, and voice control. Energy costs drive thermostat and solar-battery integration. Post-COVID home technology investment persists. AI integration (voice, predictive automation) creating upgrade cycles.
- Geographic relevance: All Dan markets work, but density matters. Boston/NE suburbs (high-income renovation market), NY/CT/NJ (luxury home + new construction), SFL (new construction + condo market, security focus). Markets with $500K+ median home values are ideal.
- Market size: US smart home market estimated at $35-45B in 2025 (Statista). Installation and integration services represent roughly 15-25% of that, or $5-10B. IBISWorld estimates Low-Voltage Electrical Contractors (NAICS 23821) at ~$25B including security, AV, and smart home. Estimate smart-home-specific installation at $3-7B as a subset.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue range: $600K-$2.0M with 30%+ recurring service/monitoring revenue.
- SDE range: $150K-$400K; verify by separating installation project revenue from recurring contracts.
- Years in business: 5+ years with established builder/contractor referral network.
- Route density: service clients within 45-minute radius; installation projects can be wider.
- Key assets to verify: dealer certifications (Control4, Savant, Lutron, Ring Alarm Pro), builder/contractor relationships, service contract list with renewal rates, low-voltage licenses, van/equipment inventory, CRM/project management system, Google reviews.
- Red flags to avoid: 90%+ project-based revenue with no recurring base; owner is the only certified technician; depends on a single custom-home builder for 40%+ revenue; equipment inventory is obsolete consumer-grade (not upgradeable to pro platforms); no service contracts or maintenance agreements.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Franchise is uncommon in smart home integration — most operators are independent or dealer-based (not franchise). Relevant brands/systems: Crestron, Control4, Savant are dealer networks (not franchises, but similar exclusivity). Security franchise brands: ADT Authorized Dealer, Vivint (dealer model), Vector Security (franchise).
- For independent acquisition, focus on: dealer certifications that transfer, builder referral network strength, and recurring monitoring revenue.
- Home technology integration franchises exist at small scale: OnTech Smart Services (Google Nest Pro installer network, not franchise), SmartRent (multifamily, not relevant). The space is too fragmented for dominant franchises.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Top competitors: Local/regional AV integrators and low-voltage contractors (vast majority), ADT (security + smart home, public), Vivint (security + smart home, PE-backed), Best Buy Geek Squad (consumer smart home installation), local electricians adding smart home as a side service, Amazon Smart Home Services (contractor marketplace).
- PE activity: Some at scale — Vivint was taken private by Blackstone (2012), later went public, then acquired by Nortek Security & Control / Melrose Industries. ADT owned by Apollo. Below $5M EBITDA, minimal PE interest.
- Fragmentation level: Very high. CEDIA (industry association) reports median integrator revenue of ~$500K. Most markets have 10-30 small integrators plus national brands (ADT, Best Buy).
- Moat: dealer certifications and manufacturer relationships (Control4/Lutron/Savant restrict dealer counts), builder/architect referral networks, recurring monitoring/service contracts, technical expertise barrier, local reputation and reviews.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Typical deal structure: asset purchase — customer list, service contracts, dealer certifications, equipment, vehicles. Seller note 15-20% tied to service contract retention. Transition agreement 60-120 days (longer than typical because of technical knowledge transfer and builder relationship introductions).
- SBA eligibility: good if recurring monitoring/service contracts are transferable. Dealer certifications may require manufacturer approval for transfer — verify pre-LOI.
- Down payment required: 10% of purchase price plus closing costs. On $750K base case, roughly $75K-$110K cash.
- Payback period at base case: 3-4 years. Longer than pure route businesses because some revenue is project-based and lumpy.
- Honest multiple arbitrage: buy at 3-3.5x SDE, grow recurring revenue base, add energy/solar integration services, potentially exit at 4-5x to a regional consolidator if you reach $1M+ EBITDA.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map 20 local integrators in target metro. Contact 3-5 brokers. Call CEDIA for member directory. Interview 3 integrators. Research dealer certification transfer requirements (Control4, Lutron, etc.). Check state low-voltage licensing requirements.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Identify operator-partner (low-voltage technician or experienced integrator). Collect 3 P&Ls. Build financial model separating recurring vs project revenue. Visit 2-3 targets. Evaluate builder/contractor referral pipeline strength.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on best-fit target. Prioritize 30%+ recurring revenue, established dealer certifications, and builder referral network. If no target clears the bar, consider launching with operator-partner using consumer-grade platform (Ring/Nest ecosystem) targeting the mid-market gap between ADT and custom integrators.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Manager-adjusted SDE below $150K after paying a lead integrator $70K-$90K.
- Less than 25% recurring revenue — business is project-based and cyclical.
- Dealer certifications (Control4, Savant, Lutron) cannot transfer to new owner — manufacturers may reject buyers without industry experience.
- State licensing requirements create 6-12 month delays or require Dan/partner to hold the license personally.
- Target is dependent on a single custom-home builder for 40%+ revenue — builder switches = instant revenue collapse.
- Technology obsolescence risk: business is built around a platform being discontinued or displaced (e.g., proprietary system losing market share to open/consumer platforms).
- Customer acquisition cost exceeds $500 with average ticket under $3K — unit economics don't support growth investment.
