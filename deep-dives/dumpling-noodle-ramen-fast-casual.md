# Dumpling / Noodle / Ramen Fast Casual — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL GO only for proven local unit economics
The cuisine has real consumer demand and strong urban fit, but restaurant execution risk is unforgiving. Dan should pursue only a profitable existing unit or small group with simple menu, strong throughput, and operator-partner—not a build-from-scratch food dream.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map

- Fast-casual Asian noodle/dumpling/ramen concepts sell bowls, dumplings, buns, sides, beverages, delivery, catering, and sometimes frozen retail packs.
- Customers are urban/suburban consumers, office lunch, students, families, delivery users, and catering accounts; B2B is modest but possible via offices/events.
- Demand is driven by fast-casual Asian food adoption, delivery habits, Gen Z/millennial preference, and affordable indulgence. NE/NY/NJ is highly relevant; SFL works in dense affluent/young pockets but seasonality and rents matter.
- Market size claims for ramen/dumpling are noisy; use restaurant-level trade-area underwriting rather than top-down TAM.

**Key assumptions:**
- Entry multiples: 3-4.5x SDE at Dan’s scale ($200K-$500K SDE), not platform multiples.
- SBA 7(a): 10-year term, ~11% rate, 10% down.
- Cash-on-cash = (SDE - annual debt service) / down payment; figures are directional and before taxes.
- Operator partner assumption: Dan can hire/partner with an operator; model includes manager/clinical/GM reality where relevant.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox

- Good target: $1M-$2.5M revenue, $180K-$500K SDE, 3+ years, strong reviews, simple menu, repeat lunch/dinner traffic, delivery profit discipline, documented recipes, trained kitchen lead, and transferable lease.
- Verify prime cost, labor scheduling, delivery channel margin, rent % of sales, health inspections, lease assignment, recipe/process dependence on owner, and food safety controls.
- Franchise candidates/benchmarks: AFURI Ramen + Dumpling, JINYA Ramen Bar, Teriyaki Madness, Pei Wei, Wow Bao, and local multi-unit noodle/dumpling concepts. Pull FDDs for initial investment, royalty, ad fund, AUV/Item 19, and supply-chain terms.

## 5. Competitive Landscape

- Competition includes independents, ramen groups, Korean/Japanese/Chinese fast casual, poke/bowl shops, delivery-only brands, and mall/food-hall concepts.
- PE interest is generally in scaled restaurant platforms, not single stores; small-unit exit multiple discipline matters.
- Moat is location, lease, consistent food, throughput, labor systems, delivery economics, reviews, and brand memorability. Recipes alone are not a moat.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

- Use asset purchase with lease contingency, seller training period, health/permit diligence, and seller note. Keep personal guarantee/rent exposure tightly modeled.
- At $790K base price, 10% down is ~$79K plus working capital and refresh capex; 11% SBA debt service leaves little room for margin drift.
- Restaurant multiple arbitrage is thin: buy 3.0-3.5x SDE and exit 3.5-4.0x only if multi-unit systems develop.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

- Days 1-30: map Boston/NY/NJ/CT ramen/dumpling operators, collect menus/prices/reviews, and identify operator-chef/GM candidates.
- Days 31-60: review P&Ls, POS data, delivery margins, lease terms, health inspections, and food/labor controls for 3-5 targets.
- Days 61-90: LOI on one profitable existing unit or kill; do not sign a franchise/startup lease without operator partner.

## 8. Kill Criteria

- Owner-chef is the business and cannot transfer.
- Prime cost consistently above 65%-68%.
- Rent above 8%-10% of sales without extraordinary volume.
- Delivery is high mix but unprofitable after fees.
- No operator partner or kitchen leader.
