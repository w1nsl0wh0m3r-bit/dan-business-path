# Towing / Roadside Assistance — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
A real urgency-and-dispatch business with recurring municipal, motor-club, insurance, dealer and fleet demand; attractive only if acquired with trucks, rotation slots, dispatcher discipline, and non-AAA revenue mix.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? Towing and roadside assistance operators recover disabled vehicles, provide jump starts, lockout, tire, winch-out and accident tow service, and often monetize storage/impound lots. Customers split across B2C emergency calls, motor clubs/insurers, municipalities/police rotations, auto dealers, repair shops, apartments, and fleet accounts. Demand is recurring at the market level because cars break, crashes happen, and fleets need uptime, but individual retail customers are episodic. NE/NY/SFL all work; NY/NJ/CT density helps, Boston suburbs need police/dealer relationships, SFL adds accident volume and tourism. IBISWorld classifies U.S. automobile towing as a multi-billion-dollar fragmented industry; use local call volume and contracted account lists rather than national TAM precision.
- Who are the customers? B2B/B2C split varies by account mix; underwrite each target from actual invoices, not seller narrative.
- Demand drivers: recurring account relationships, vehicle parc, compliance/uptime, weather, accidents, owner convenience, and local density depending on category.
- Geographic relevance: Boston/NE primary, NY/CT/NJ secondary, SFL tertiary; see category-specific notes above.
- Market size: use cited industry pages and public-company/FDD context as directional only; local revenue density matters more than fake TAM precision.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue $800K-$2.5M; SDE $180K-$500K; 7+ years in business; 4-12 trucks with maintenance records; police rotations, dealer accounts, motor-club volume by margin, storage lot permits, driver roster, dispatch logs. Avoid one-owner/one-driver shops, predatory impound complaints, uninsured drivers, aged trucks, or 70%+ low-margin AAA volume. Franchise candidates include HONK-partner networks (not traditional franchise), Allstate Roadside contractors, Agero provider networks, and local brands; true FDD-driven towing franchises are limited, so acquisition beats franchise.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Prefer franchise only where FDD economics are transparent, territory is protected, royalty/ad fund leaves 15%+ manager-run SDE, and franchisee validation confirms owner-absentee feasibility.
- Validate Item 19, royalty, ad fund, initial investment, supplier restrictions, required owner involvement, and resale history.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
Competitors include AAA/Agero/Allstate provider networks, local towers, Copart/IAA ecosystem adjacencies, dealership preferred vendors, and police rotation lists. PE consolidation is limited at Dan scale but accident management and roadside platforms are institutional. Fragmentation is high. Moats are rotation lists, dispatch speed, storage location, driver availability, insurance compliance, and account relationships.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Typical deal structure: asset purchase with seller note/earnout tied to customer retention, working-capital peg, equipment inspection, lease assignment, and 30-90 day seller transition.
- SBA eligibility and typical loan terms: generally SBA 7(a)-financeable when books are clean, licenses/contracts transfer, collateral/equipment is documented, and no unresolved legal/environmental issue exists.
- Down payment required at Dan's scale: roughly 10% of purchase price plus closing costs and working capital; keep total cash at risk below what a downside case can repay in 3-4 years.
- Payback period at base case: generally 2-4 years on equity if base-case SDE holds after debt service.
- Honest multiple arbitrage: assume ~3x in → 4x out at this scale only after systems, recurring revenue, documented management depth, and clean add-on pipeline are proven.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map local competitors in Boston/NE, NY/CT/NJ, and SFL; pull broker listings; request franchise FDDs where relevant; interview 3 operators or former employees; build target list.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Identify operator partner; normalize 3 years of financials; verify licenses, lease, equipment, reviews, customer concentration and recurring revenue; conduct site visits.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit one disciplined LOI or reject the category; if franchise path survives, compare FDD economics with acquisition model and require franchisee validation.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- No transferable police rotations or commercial accounts; trucks require major capex; driver turnover >40%; more than half gross profit from low-margin motor-club calls; unresolved predatory towing complaints; owner is main dispatcher and driver; storage lot zoning or lien-sale practices are sloppy.
