# Medical Courier / Lab Specimen Courier — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Strong recurring B2B route logic with healthcare tailwinds, but compliance burden (HIPAA, chain-of-custody, bloodborne pathogen, temperature control) and contract-pricing pressure from large lab systems are real risks. Best as acquisition of a contracted regional courier with documented SOPs and low customer concentration — not a startup chasing hospital procurement.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? A specialized logistics company that transports medical specimens (blood, tissue, cultures), pharmaceuticals, lab results, medical equipment, and organ/tissue donations between hospitals, clinics, labs, pharmacies, nursing homes, and patients. Routes are scheduled (daily/weekly pickup and delivery runs) and on-demand (STAT/emergency calls). The business requires HIPAA compliance, chain-of-custody documentation, temperature-controlled transport, and trained, background-checked drivers.
- Who are the customers? Hospitals, reference labs (Quest, Labcorp), independent labs, physician offices, clinics, nursing homes/SNFs, pharmacies, blood banks, and organ procurement organizations. 100% B2B. Lab pickup/delivery routes and hospital-system contracts are the core revenue engine.
- Demand drivers: Aging population drives lab work volume (3-4 billion lab tests performed annually in the US per CDC). Decentralization of healthcare (urgent cares, outpatient surgery, home health) increases specimen transport needs. Same-day and STAT delivery demand is growing. Global medical courier market projected to reach $7.2B by 2025 and $8B+ by 2030 (WorldMetrics 2026 report).
- Geographic relevance: All three target markets are strong — Boston/NE has dense hospital/lab networks and biotech corridors; NY/CT/NJ has massive healthcare volume; SFL has large elderly population driving lab/specimen demand. Proximity to major reference labs (Quest/Labcorp regional hubs) is key.
- Market size: North America leads the global medical courier market (WorldMetrics). Local/regional courier operations range from $500K to $5M+ in revenue. No clean public TAM for the small-regional segment; label local estimates as estimate.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue: $1M-$4M, SDE: $200K-$600K
- 5-25+ drivers/contractors with clean background checks and medical-courier training
- Documented route schedules and stop-level revenue data
- Multi-year contracts with labs, hospitals, or clinics (not month-to-month)
- HIPAA-compliant SOPs, chain-of-custody forms, temperature-monitoring logs
- Proper insurance (commercial auto, professional liability, cyber/HIPAA breach)
- Dispatch/scheduling software (not paper-based routing)
- Low customer concentration (no single hospital/lab >30% of revenue)
- Red flags: drivers classified as 1099 but functioning as W2 (misclassification risk), no written contracts, poor insurance history, owner holds all hospital relationships personally, HIPAA violations or breaches

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- This category has limited franchise activity. Companies like Dropoff (technology platform/brand for medical delivery), Medical Couriers Inc., and Lab Logistics operate more as networks or contractor systems. If evaluating any franchise/network affiliation, focus on: contract ownership (do you own the client relationships?), territory exclusivity, insurance requirements, technology fees, driver classification guidance, and whether the brand meaningfully improves hospital/lab acquisition vs. independent operations.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Major players: Quest Diagnostics and Labcorp have massive internal logistics networks — they are both customers and competitors. AdaptHealth, Optum (UnitedHealth), and large health systems have in-house courier fleets.
- Regional/niche competitors: Dropoff (Austin-based medical delivery platform), Medical Couriers Inc., Lab Logistics, Stat Experts, and dozens of local/regional independent medical couriers in every metro.
- General couriers: FedEx, UPS, and OnTrac handle some medical packages but cannot do STAT/emergency or chain-of-custody specimen transport reliably.
- PE/consolidation: Limited at the small-regional level. Larger healthcare logistics platforms (World Courier, Marken/UPS, FedEx Custom Critical) operate nationally but do not serve the local scheduled-route market.
- Fragmentation: Highly fragmented below the national lab networks. Most metros have 5-15 independent medical couriers.
- Moat: HIPAA compliance and chain-of-custody discipline create switching costs (hospitals don't change couriers casually). Temperature-control capability (cold chain) for specimens is a barrier. Route density and STAT response time are operational moats. Background-checked, trained driver pool is hard to replicate quickly.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Deal structure: Asset purchase or membership-interest purchase depending on contract-assignment terms. Critical diligence item: can contracts be assigned to new owner, or do hospitals/labs require consent? Seller note (15-25%) tied to contract retention. Consider earnout based on first-year revenue from top 5 accounts.
- SBA eligibility: Strong if recurring contract revenue is documented. Lender will scrutinize: contract terms (auto-renewal vs. month-to-month), insurance history, any HIPAA incidents, driver classification (W2 vs. 1099), and customer concentration. Vehicle fleet provides collateral.
- Down payment at base case ($1.2M price): ~$120K at 10% down.
- Payback period: ~1.0-1.3 years at base case if contract retention holds above 85%.
- Multiple arbitrage: Buy a fragmented 3.5x SDE regional courier, consolidate contracts and professionalize dispatch/compliance, build to 4.0-4.5x on exit to a larger healthcare logistics platform. Modest but achievable.

Speak with 2-3 SBA lenders. Interview operator-partner candidates.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on top target with seller note/retention earnout. Include diligence gates: contract-assignment verification (get hospital/lab consent), driver background-check audit, insurance binder confirmation, HIPAA compliance review, and customer-reference calls with top accounts. If no quality target, kill or park.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Contracts are month-to-month with no renewal history — retention is unprovable
- Owner personally holds all hospital/lab relationships and they will not transfer
- Driver misclassification (1099 vs. W2) exposure is material
- HIPAA violations or breaches in the company's history
- Customer concentration: single hospital/lab system exceeds 40% of revenue
- Insurance costs are rising faster than contract pricing allows
- Base-case DSCR fails after adding dispatcher, ops manager, and proper insurance
