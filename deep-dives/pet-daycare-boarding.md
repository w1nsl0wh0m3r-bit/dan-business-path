# Pet Daycare / Boarding Deep Dive
*Generated: 2026-05-25 | Confidence: Medium (market data and FDD summaries public; margins location-dependent)*

## 1. Executive Verdict — **CONDITIONAL GO**
The category has real demand, emotional customer stickiness, and strong recurring usage, but the operating model is harder than the “cute dogs” surface suggests: labor, noise/zoning, incident risk, cleaning, capacity utilization, and manager quality drive everything. **Best path:** buy a profitable independent kennel/daycare with expansion upside or acquire a mature franchise resale. De novo franchise builds at $600K-$1.3M investment are too risky unless the location is a layup.

## 2. Market Sizing
- IBISWorld data cited in 2024 industry analysis puts U.S. **pet grooming and boarding** at approximately **$15B revenue in 2023**.
- Global Market Insights cited U.S. pet daycare around **$1.7B in 2024**; Grand View Research cited U.S. pet boarding around **$8.62B in 2024**. Scope differs, so use these as directional not additive.
- Pet spend is structurally supported by high pet ownership and “pet humanization,” but local capacity/zoning is the real constraint.

## 6. Operator Economics
A $1.1M unit at 18% SDE margin produces ~$198K. Pay a capable GM $85K fully loaded and Dan is left with ~$110K before debt service. That works only if purchase price is reasonable and the facility does not need major capex. New-build franchise economics are tougher: debt service on $800K+ all-in investment can wipe out early cash flow.

## 7. Acquisition Targets
Look for:
- Independent boarding/daycare facilities with owner retirement and dated marketing.
- Capacity expansion: underused grooming rooms, pricing below market, no membership model.
- Clean incident history, strong Google reviews, documented vaccination controls.
- Real estate optionality: lease assignability, outdoor space, zoning compliance.
Avoid:
- Facilities with chronic barking/noise complaints.
- Unclear capacity math or inflated add-backs from owner labor.
- High staff turnover and weak SOPs.

## 8. Dan Fit — **3.7 / 5**
Good consumer-services pattern recognition and recurring revenue. But it is operationally fussy and liability-prone. Dan should like the business only with a strong animal-care GM/operator and a facility that already works.

- No verified zoning/lease protection for animal boarding/daycare use.
- Manager-run cash flow below $100K after debt service in base case.
- Incident history, poor reviews, or insurance issues.
