# Durable Medical Equipment Delivery / Service — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Attractive demographic tailwind (aging-in-place, mobility/accessibility demand), but the category bifurcates sharply: private-pay mobility/accessibility retail (stairlifts, ramps, lifts) is a strong consumer business with 35%–50% gross margins; Medicare-reimbursement DME is a regulatory nightmare with compressed margins. Only pursue the private-pay path — ideally through 101 Mobility franchise acquisition or an independent accessibility shop.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- **What is this business?** A retail and service business that sells, delivers, installs, and services durable medical equipment (DME) — stairlifts, wheelchair ramps, vertical platform lifts, patient lifts, bathroom safety equipment, mobility scooters, and walk-in tubs. Some operators also rent stairlifts and sell refurbished equipment. The business requires certified installers and is heavily referral-driven (hospitals, PT/OT, discharge planners, elder-law attorneys, VA, senior living communities).
- **Who are the customers?** Seniors aging in place, people with mobility disabilities, family caregivers, VA patients, and rehabilitation patients. Primarily B2C (private-pay consumers) with a B2B referral channel (healthcare professionals, senior living, VA). The private-pay segment is the attractive one — Medicare-reimbursed DME involves coding, audits, and reimbursement compression.
- **Demand drivers:** 10,000 Americans turn 65 every day through 2030 (US Census); aging-in-place preference (AARP: ~77% of adults 50+ want to remain in their homes); home accessibility retrofit demand; stairlift market growing 6–8% CAGR; VA and state grant programs funding ramps and lifts for veterans; post-surgery rehab equipment demand.
- **Geographic relevance:** All three target markets work. Boston/NE has older housing stock (more stairlift/ramp demand) and dense senior population. NY/CT/NJ has high-income seniors who can afford private-pay solutions. SFL has massive retiree population and condo/single-level demand (fewer stairlifts, more bathroom safety and scooter demand).
- **Market size:** US home medical equipment market ~$40B+ (CMS/IBISWorld estimates); the private-pay accessibility/mobility segment is a subset. 101 Mobility's 2024 FDD reports average per-territory revenue of ~$706K across 172 franchised outlets (Franchise Chatter). A well-run independent or franchise territory can generate $500K–$1.5M.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue: $600K–$1.3M, SDE: $150K–$400K
- High private-pay mix (>70% of revenue); minimal Medicare/reimbursement dependency
- Certified installer(s) on staff (not just the owner)
- Strong Google reviews (4.5+ stars) and referral pipeline from healthcare professionals
- Established supplier relationships (Acorn, Handicare, Bruno, Savaria, Harmar)
- Rental fleet generating recurring revenue
- Used/refurbished equipment resale capability
- Clean territory or service area with growth potential
- Red flags: heavy Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement dependency, owner is sole installer and salesperson, supplier concentration with single vendor, no documented referral sources, pending warranty claims or product liability issues

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- **101 Mobility:** 177 franchised + 16 company-owned outlets (2023); initial investment $182K–$259K; franchise fee $44K–$74K; tiered royalty (7% below $500K, 6% $500K–$1M, 5% above $1M); marketing fee 2% (can rise to 4%); Item 19 disclosed — average revenue per territory ~$706K (2024 FDD via Franchise Chatter). On SBA Franchise Registry.
- **Mobility Plus:** Verify current FDD for fees and Item 19.
- **Next Day Access:** Verify current FDD for fees and Item 19.
- **Key franchise diligence:** territory size and exclusivity, installer training and certification support, supplier pricing advantages through franchise network, lead generation from national brand/marketing, and whether the royalty burden is justified by acquisition and operational support.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- **101 Mobility** — largest franchise system in the accessibility/mobility retail space; national footprint with local franchise owners.
- **National Seating & Mobility / Numotion** — complex rehab, more reimbursement-driven; different segment.
- **MobilityWorks / United Access** — vehicle mobility and adaptive equipment; adjacent but different product line.
- **Local DME stores** — walkers, scooters, lift chairs, bathroom aids; often reimbursement-heavy.
- **Contractors/handymen + online stairlift sellers** — compete on installation and price; quality/credential advantage for established operators.
- **Big-box medical supply** — limited accessibility installation capability; mostly retail product sales.
- **PE activity:** Limited at the small-territory level. Some regional platform building by accessibility companies. National Seating & Mobility was acquired by VGM Group / PE.
- **Fragmentation:** High — most markets have 5–15 independent DME/accessibility operators. 101 Mobility is the only national franchise platform at scale.
- **Moat:** Certified installation capability (not easy to replicate), referral relationships with healthcare professionals, supplier pricing and product access, Google/local SEO for "stairlift near me" searches, trust/reputation with seniors and families, and VA contract eligibility.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- **Deal structure:** Asset purchase or franchise transfer — customer lists, referral relationships, inventory, installation equipment, vehicle fleet, lease assignment, franchise territory rights. Seller note (15–25%) tied to revenue retention. Consider earnout based on first-year revenue performance.
- **SBA eligibility:** Strong — 101 Mobility is on the SBA Franchise Registry. Lender will verify franchise agreement terms, territory rights, installer certifications, and insurance coverage. Independent acquisitions similarly financeable if revenue is documented.
- **Down payment at base case ($2.0M price):** ~$200K at 10% down.
- **Payback period:** ~2.0–3.0 years at base case. Slower than route businesses because revenue is project-based (installations) rather than recurring subscriptions.
- **Multiple arbitrage:** Buy a single underperforming territory at 3.0–3.5x SDE, professionalize marketing and referral pipeline, expand to multi-territory, sell at 4.0–4.5x per territory to a platform buyer or back to franchisor. Modest but achievable.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Revenue relies materially on Medicare/insurance reimbursement — this creates audit risk and margin compression
- Franchisees confirm that owner sales involvement is mandatory (Dan can't be the salesperson)
- Median territory cannot clear $150K manager-adjusted EBITDA at realistic install volumes
- Lead cost is rising with weak conversion — indicates market saturation or poor brand differentiation
- Install technician hiring is a persistent bottleneck in target market
- Supplier concentration with single vendor creates pricing risk
- Product liability or warranty claim exposure is material
- Customer reviews are poor — trust is everything in this business
