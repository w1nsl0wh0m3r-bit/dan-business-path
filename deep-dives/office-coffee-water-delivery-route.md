# Office Coffee / Water Delivery Route — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: GO
One of the cleaner Dan-scale route-business fits: recurring B2B consumption, density economics, simple acquisitions, and obvious digital/route-optimization upside. Watch post-COVID office occupancy and customer concentration. Buy an established route with 80+ accounts and a reliable driver, then layer technology.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? An office coffee service (OCS) and/or water delivery route provides coffee equipment, supplies, and/or bottled/filtered water to offices, healthcare facilities, gyms, salons, and small businesses on a scheduled recurring basis. Revenue comes from equipment placement (often free or subsidized), consumable resupply (coffee, cups, sweeteners, water bottles, filters), and maintenance/repair.
- Who are the customers? B2B — small-to-midsize offices, medical/dental practices, auto dealerships, salons, gyms, schools, and co-working spaces. Typical account has 10-100 employees. B2C water delivery to homes is a secondary market.
- Demand drivers: workplace coffee is near-universal; convenience outsourcing; recurring consumption (coffee is a daily habit). Water delivery benefits from health/wellness trends and avoidance of tap water in some markets. Hybrid work is a headwind for downtown office towers but helps suburban/flex office and home-delivery segments.
- Geographic relevance: Strong in all Dan-target markets. NE/Boston and NY/CT/NJ have dense office parks and older buildings with limited plumbing for plumbed solutions. South Florida has large medical/dental and professional-services office density.
- Market size: Research and Markets pegs the global office coffee service market at ~$9.8B in 2025, projected to reach ~$20.6B by 2034 at 8.6% CAGR. US is roughly 35-40% of global, implying ~$3.5B-$4B US market. Add water delivery and the addressable service-market is larger.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue range: $600K-$1.8M with 80-200+ active accounts. Look for 60%+ accounts on auto-delivery schedules.
- SDE range: $150K-$400K; verify margins aren't propped up by owner deferring maintenance or driving routes themselves.
- Years in business: 5+ years with established route geography and customer relationships.
- Route density: accounts clustered in a 30-45 minute radius; tight enough for 1-2 drivers to cover efficiently.
- Key assets to verify: customer list with delivery history, equipment inventory (brewers, coolers, dispensers), vehicle(s), supplier contracts (coffee roasters, water bottlers), phone number/website, route management software.
- Red flags to avoid: top 10 accounts exceed 50% of revenue; equipment is old/lease-encumbered; owner drives the route personally with no backup; coffee commodity costs have squeezed margins without pricing power; declining account count over last 3 years.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Franchise is less common in OCS/water (most operators are independent). Relevant brands/systems: Canteen/Compass Group (corporate, not franchise), Aramark Refreshments (corporate), Quench/Waterlogic (bottle-free water, dealer model), Farmer Brothers (distributor, not franchise), Keurig Dr Pepper-away-from-home programs.
- For independent acquisition, focus on seller terms, customer retention guarantees, and transition support.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Top competitors: Canteen (Compass Group subsidiary, largest US OCS), Aramark Refreshments, Farmer Brothers, Royal Cup Coffee, First Choice Coffee Services, local/regional OCS operators, Amazon/Office Depot for commodity supplies (indirect competition), Keurig Dr Pepper direct programs.
- PE activity: Compass Group and Aramark are public/mega-cap. Below them, the market is fragmented among regional and local operators. PE roll-ups exist at $10M+ EBITDA scale (e.g., Waterlogic/Quench consolidation). Dan-scale ($200K-$500K SDE) is below PE radar.
- Fragmentation level: Very high below the top-10 national operators. Most metros have 5-15 local OCS/water delivery operators.
- Moat: route density and driver relationships, equipment lock-in (proprietary brewers/coolers), habitual reordering, and service responsiveness. Amazon can deliver commodity coffee pods but can't fix a broken brewer same-day.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Typical deal structure: asset purchase — customer list, equipment, vehicles, goodwill. Seller note of 10%-20% tied to customer retention (accounts active after 6-12 months). Transition period of 30-90 days with seller introducing new owner to key accounts.
- SBA eligibility: strong. Route businesses with transferable customer lists and recurring revenue are among the most financeable small-business categories.
- Down payment required: ~10% of purchase price. On a $875K deal, roughly $87K-$120K cash including closing costs.
- Payback period at base case: 2-3 years if retention holds above 85% and route density supports margin.
- Honest multiple arbitrage: buy at 3.5x SDE, optimize routing/digital ordering, maybe tuck in a smaller adjacent route, exit at 4.0-4.5x.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map local OCS/water operators in target metro. Contact 3-5 business brokers specializing in route/distribution businesses. Interview 3 operators. Build account-density map from public data.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Identify operator-partner (experienced route manager). Collect 3 P&Ls. Build valuation model. Test SBA pre-qualification. Evaluate tuck-in acquisition candidates.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on best-fit target. Prioritize accounts with auto-delivery schedules, low concentration, and growth whitespace (medical, co-working, suburban flex).

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Account churn exceeds 20% annually — indicates service quality or pricing problems.
- Top 5 accounts exceed 40% of revenue.
- Owner is the sole driver with no route documentation or customer-contact history.
- Equipment requires $50K+ immediate replacement.
- Local market has been picked over by Canteen/Aramark with no whitespace for independent operators.
- Hybrid work has permanently reduced the addressable office-base in target geography by 30%+ with no offset from medical/home/retail segments.
