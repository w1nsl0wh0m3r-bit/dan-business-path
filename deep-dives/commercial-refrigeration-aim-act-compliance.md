# Commercial Refrigeration / AIM Act Compliance — Deep Dive
*Generated: 2026-05-25 | Category rank: 25 | Blended score: 3.50*

## 1. Executive Verdict — CONDITIONAL GO, HIGHER COMPLEXITY
Commercial refrigeration is a real, durable, regulation-supported service market. The AIM Act/HFC phasedown creates retrofit, leak-repair, reclaim, and equipment-transition demand. But this is a harder operating business than garage doors/backflow: skilled technicians are scarce, emergency service is 24/7, customers are demanding, and bad project execution burns margin.

**Dan-specific take:** Worth pursuing only if buying a shop with experienced techs, service contracts, and a retained operator. Do not buy a two-tech owner-operated business and assume it can be manager-run on day one.

## 2. Market Sizing
- EPA states the AIM Act authorizes EPA to phase down HFC production/consumption, manage HFCs/substitutes, and facilitate transition to next-generation technologies; HFCs are used in refrigeration and air conditioning ([EPA AIM Act background](https://www.epa.gov/climate-hfcs-reduction/background-hfcs-and-aim-act)).
- Commercial refrigeration demand comes from grocery, convenience stores, restaurants, cold storage, foodservice, pharmacies, and institutional kitchens.
- Exact U.S. service-market revenue should be sourced from paid industry reports (IBISWorld/HVACR/refrigeration equipment repair). Bottom-up, a mid-sized metro with hundreds of food retail and restaurant sites can support multiple multi-million-dollar service contractors.

## 5. Franchise Comp Table
This is not a clean franchise category. The better path is independent acquisition. HVAC franchises exist, but commercial refrigeration/AIM compliance is more technical and B2B than consumer HVAC. Any franchise FDD should be treated as adjacent, not directly comparable.

## 6. Operator Economics
This needs a serious operator: service manager/GM with refrigeration background. Budget **$110-150K + performance bonus**. A dispatcher alone is not enough. Key KPIs: first-time fix rate, callback %, tech utilization, after-hours response time, PM contract gross margin, refrigerant inventory/reclaim compliance, AR aging.

## 7. Acquisition Targets
**Look for:** 5+ experienced techs, grocery/C-store/restaurant contract base, PM revenue >25%, documented refrigerant handling compliance, after-hours process, supplier accounts, job-costing by work order, low callback rate.

**Avoid:** owner as only senior refrigeration tech, no EPA Section 608 certification records, project revenue with poor gross margins, customer concentration >25%, unpaid emergency receivables, no inventory controls for refrigerants.

**Multiple:** 3-4x SDE for a clean service-heavy shop; 2.5-3x for small owner-tech business; do not pay strategic multiples at Dan’s scale.
