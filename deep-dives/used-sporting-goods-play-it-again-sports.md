# Used Sporting Goods / Play It Again Sports Deep Dive
*Generated: 2026-05-25 | Confidence: Medium (public FDD summaries; verify current FDD before LOI)*

## 1. Executive Verdict — **CONDITIONAL GO**
This is one of the cleaner “Dan-fit” retail concepts: understandable customer behavior, trade-in supply creates local moat, and Winmark’s resale playbook is proven. But the single-unit economics are not exciting enough unless Dan buys right or opens in a dense youth-sports market with a strong operator. **Best path:** buy an existing Play It Again Sports or independent used sporting goods store at ≤2.5x verified SDE, not a de novo build unless the specific territory is exceptional.

## 2. Market Sizing
- Broad U.S. sporting goods retail is large: IBISWorld’s Sporting Goods Stores category is cited around **$117.5B in 2025** with ~84k businesses; this includes new goods and is not a pure used-goods number. Source: IBISWorld search result summary, NAICS 45111, 2026 update.
- Used sporting goods is a niche inside used-goods retail and sporting goods retail. Public sources do **not** provide a clean U.S. used-sporting-goods-only market size. Treat the TAM as **local/category-specific**, driven by youth hockey, lacrosse, baseball, golf, skiing, fitness, and team sports participation.
- Winmark gives a better bottom-up read: Play It Again Sports had about **302 units** and **$331.9M systemwide global sales**, implying roughly **$1.1M average system sales per store** before mix/geography differences (public FDD summary/search result).

## 4. Competitive Landscape
- **Play It Again Sports / Winmark** — dominant used sporting goods franchise. Public FDD summaries cite $25K initial franchise fee, ~5% royalty, ~$343K-$457K total investment, and 100% franchised system.
- **SidelineSwap** — online peer-to-peer used sports marketplace; strongest in hockey/lacrosse/baseball gear.
- **Facebook Marketplace / Craigslist / eBay** — liquidity competitors; lower trust/convenience but huge supply.
- **Dick’s Sporting Goods / Academy / local pro shops** — new-equipment competitors, especially when clearance pricing narrows the used/new gap.
- **Local ski/bike/golf consignment stores** — niche specialists with better credibility in high-ticket categories.

## 6. Operator Economics
A $1.0M store at 11% EBITDA produces ~$110K pre-owner EBITDA. Pay a real GM $65K + 10% bonus + payroll burden and Dan’s passive/board-level cash flow can fall to **$35K-$55K**. That is not enough unless there is a credible path to multi-unit or a very low purchase price. Dan should underwrite this as an operator-partner business, not truly absentee.

## 7. Acquisition Targets
Look for:
- 8+ years operating history, high repeat customer base, clean POS by category/season.
- High used mix and disciplined buy policy; weak inventory controls are the silent killer.
- Youth sports / affluent suburb exposure: Boston suburbs, southern NH, RI, CT shoreline, Portland ME.
- Seller retiring, underdeveloped online resale, poor local marketing.
Avoid:
- Stores dependent on owner’s personal sports knowledge.
- Bloated stale inventory marked at unrealistic cost.
- Lease with poor visibility or limited storage.

## 8. Dan Fit — **4.0 / 5**
Good: consumer/retail diligence, franchisor pattern recognition, unit economics discipline. Bad: small single-unit cash flow and inventory-heavy retail. Better as a platform if Dan can buy 2-3 local stores or pair a strong operator with online resale execution.
