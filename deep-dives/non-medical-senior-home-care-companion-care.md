# Non-medical Senior Home Care / Companion Care — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Demand is structurally excellent, but labor quality and scheduling intensity make this a people business, not passive recurring revenue. Attractive only with an operator partner, disciplined caregiver recruiting engine and no fantasy about margins.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- Non-medical home care provides companion care, personal care, meal prep, transportation, respite and light housekeeping; it is not skilled nursing.
- Customers are mostly B2C families/private-pay seniors, with B2B referral sources from hospitals, rehab centers, elder-law attorneys and senior communities.
- Demand drivers: Census/ACL aging data show the 65+ population growing rapidly; preference to age in place; caregiver shortages create high willingness to pay for reliability.
- Geographic relevance: strong in NE/NY/SFL, especially affluent suburbs and retiree markets.
- Market size: Home Care Association and market-research sources describe home care as a large and growing U.S. market; exact local SAM should be built by county age/income data.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue range: $1.5M-$4.0M, private-pay heavy, diversified referral channels.
- SDE range: $250K-$600K after paid administrator/scheduler.
- Years in business: 5+ years with state compliance history and caregiver tenure data.
- Route density / customer count: 60+ active clients, no client >10%-15% revenue, caregivers clustered by county.
- Key assets to verify: caregiver roster, payroll taxes, EVV/scheduling system, client contracts, referral sources, licenses, complaint history.
- Red flags to avoid: Medicaid-heavy low-margin payer mix, 1099 caregiver misclassification, high churn, wage/hour issues, seller as primary referral source.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Home Instead, Visiting Angels, Right at Home, Comfort Keepers, Senior Helpers, Homewatch CareGivers.
- Franchise diligence: FDD Item 19, royalty/brand fund, territory demographics, caregiver recruiting support, local licensing help.
- Franchise fees: many senior-care franchises cite initial fees roughly $45K-$60K and royalties often 5%-6%+; verify FDD.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Competitors: Home Instead, Visiting Angels, Right at Home, Comfort Keepers, Senior Helpers, local independent agencies, live-in care agencies.
- PE activity: active but selective; platforms like Honor/Home Instead and Addus illustrate scale interest, though Dan's scale remains lower-middle-market/local.
- Fragmentation: high locally despite major brands.
- Moat: caregiver supply, referral relationships, compliance, 24/7 scheduling, reputation with adult children.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Typical deal: asset purchase, seller note/earnout tied to revenue retention, non-solicit/non-compete, compliance reps.
- SBA eligibility: generally yes for private-pay service agency; lender will scrutinize working capital and licensure transfer.
- Down payment required: ~10%-15% plus payroll float reserve.
- Payback period at base case: 3-5 years; faster only if churn stays low.
- Honest multiple arbitrage: 3.0-4.0x SDE in; maybe 4x out if revenue quality, compliance and manager layer are proven.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map affluent senior ZIPs in NE/NY/SFL; request FDDs; identify 15 independent targets.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Deep-dive payroll, churn, referral sources and scheduler workload; interview operating partner candidates.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): LOI only if paid-admin model works and compliance diligence is clean.

## 8. Kill Criteria
- Caregiver misclassification or wage/hour exposure.
- Medicaid/waiver reimbursement dominates economics.
- No reproducible recruiting channel.
- Revenue concentration in a few clients or one referral source.
- Owner is the scheduler, recruiter and salesperson with no bench.
