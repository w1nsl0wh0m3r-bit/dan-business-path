# Roll-off Dumpster Rental — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Attractive asset-backed local logistics business if Dan buys density, permits, disposal relationships, and contractor repeat accounts. Avoid commodity one-truck lead-gen businesses with landfill cost exposure and no dispatch discipline. Best as a multi-truck acquisition with documented contractor cohorts.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? A local haulage company that delivers open-top roll-off containers (10-40 yard) to construction sites, renovation projects, roofing jobs, estate cleanouts, and commercial properties, then transports filled containers to landfills or transfer stations. Revenue comes from container rental fees, haul charges, tonnage/disposal pass-throughs, and sometimes recycling/sorting premiums.
- Who are the customers? Contractors (roofing, remodeling, general construction), property managers, real estate investors, homeowners doing renovations, commercial tenants, and government/municipal projects. B2B contractor accounts are the underwriteable core — they recur with every job. B2C homeowners are higher-margin but episodic.
- Demand drivers: construction activity (residential remodel, roofing replacement cycles, new builds), disaster/storm debris, estate cleanouts, commercial tenant turnover. NAICS 562111 (solid waste collection) shows steady demand with construction-cycle sensitivity. Container utilization tracks housing starts and renovation spending.
- Geographic relevance: All three target markets work — Boston/NE has strong remodeling/roofing demand; NY/CT/NJ has massive construction volume and strict disposal regulations; SFL has hurricane debris, condo renovation, and construction growth. Local disposal costs and transfer-station access are the geographic differentiator.
- Market size: US waste management services ~$120B (IBISWorld); roll-off dumpster rental is a small subset. Back of Napkin estimates a single truck/5-container startup can generate $150K-$300K revenue; established small fleets (3-8 trucks, 50-250 containers) generate $750K-$3M. Local trade-area demand matters more than national TAM.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue: $1M-$3M, SDE: $250K-$700K
- 3-8 roll-off trucks, 50-250+ containers (various sizes)
- Documented contractor accounts (not just lead-gen calls)
- Transfer-station or landfill contracts with known tipping fees
- Dispatch/routing software in use (not paper-based)
- Clean vehicle titles and maintenance records
- Required permits and local licenses in good standing
- Yard/depot lease with room for container storage
- Red flags: aging truck fleet (replacement cliff), verbal-only disposal arrangements, top 5 customers >40% of revenue, yard lease expiring soon, environmental violations

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- Franchise options: redbox+ Dumpsters (waste+portable toilet combo), Bin There Dump That (residential-focused), DumpStor. FDD diligence should focus on: Item 7 total investment, territory size and exclusivity, royalty + ad fund drag on margin, container/truck requirements, Item 19 mature-unit economics if available, and whether brand adds meaningful customer acquisition in a relationships-driven market.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Major players: Waste Management (WM), Republic Services, Waste Connections, GFL Environmental — but these focus on large commercial/municipal contracts and often under-serve small contractor roll-off demand.
- Regional/local: hundreds of small independent roll-off operators in every metro; many are 1-3 truck owner-operators.
- Franchises: redbox+, Bin There Dump That, DumpStor, HaulAway — growing but still small share.
- Junk removal crossover: 1-800-GOT-JUNK, College HUNKS, Junk King offer dumpster-adjacent services but are priced as premium consumer services, not contractor-focused.
- PE activity: Consolidation at the mid-market level (Regional waste platforms), but small roll-off fleets remain fragmented and under-professionalized.
- Moat: disposal cost advantage (transfer-station proximity and volume pricing), container availability and fast delivery (contractors hire the first available), route density lowering fuel/time per pull, and contractor relationship stickiness.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Deal structure: Asset purchase preferred — trucks, containers, customer list, permits, phone number/website, yard lease assignment. Exclude environmental liabilities with strong reps/warranties. Seller note (15-25%) tied to account retention.
- SBA eligibility: Strong — heavy equipment (trucks/containers) serves as collateral. Lender will inspect truck age, condition, and title status. Expect lender to require vehicle appraisal and environmental questionnaire on yard/depot.
- Down payment at base case ($1.5M price): ~$150K at 10% down.
- Payback period: ~1.0-1.3 years at base case if contractor retention holds.
- Multiple arbitrage: Buy at 3.5x from a retiring owner-operator, professionalize dispatch and marketing, add container inventory, sell at 4.0-4.5x to a regional platform. Modest but achievable.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map all roll-off operators in target metros (Boston, NY tri-state, SFL). Scrape BizBuySell, LoopNet, and broker listings for roll-off businesses for sale. Identify disposal costs, transfer-station locations, and permit requirements in each market. Contact 5-10 brokers specializing in waste/logistics businesses.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Build three-case acquisition model with local tipping-fee assumptions. Diligence 3-5 targets: verify tax returns, container inventory and condition, truck maintenance logs, contractor account list, and disposal contracts. Speak with 2-3 SBA lenders. Interview dispatcher/ops-manager candidates.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on top target with seller note/retention earnout. Include diligence gates: vehicle appraisal, environmental review of yard, permit transfer verification, and customer-reference calls with top 10 contractor accounts. If no quality target, kill or park.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Truck fleet is at end of useful life — replacement capex would consume 2+ years of cash flow
- Disposal/tipping fees cannot be passed through to customers
- Top 5 customers exceed 40% of revenue with no contracts
- Yard/depot lease is lost or untransferable
- Environmental violations or pending enforcement on yard or operations
- Revenue is primarily lead-gen (HomeAdvisor/Angi) with no contractor relationships
- Local market is oversaturated with aggressive pricing (race to bottom on per-pull rates)
- Base-case DSCR fails after adding dispatcher and ops-manager salary
