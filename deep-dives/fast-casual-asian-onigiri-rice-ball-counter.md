# Fast-Casual Asian / Onigiri / Rice-Ball Counter — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL / WATCHLIST
Interesting white-space concept, but it is more venture/build than acquisition unless Dan finds an existing profitable shop with a repeatable commissary model. This is not a first acquisition unless paired with a strong food operator and a low-risk kiosk/food-hall launch.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- What is this business? A compact fast-casual counter selling onigiri/rice balls, musubi, kimbap-adjacent handhelds, bento sides, miso soup, tea/coffee, and grab-and-go Asian snacks. Revenue is lunch, commute, catering, and delivery.
- Who are the customers? Primarily B2C office workers, students, commuters, Asian-food enthusiasts, gym/health customers seeking portable meals; B2B includes office catering and wholesale grab-and-go to cafes/corporate pantries.
- Demand drivers: fast-casual Asian growth, demand for portable affordable meals, social-media novelty, lunch value, and rising familiarity with Japanese/Korean convenience foods.
- Geographic relevance: Best in NYC, Boston/Cambridge, college towns, and dense SFL urban nodes. Needs high pedestrian density or institutional demand; weak in car-dependent suburbs.
- Market size: No clean U.S. onigiri-counter TAM. Use proxy markets: U.S. limited-service restaurants are hundreds of billions (National Restaurant Association), Asian limited-service and sushi/poke segments are large and growing. Onigiri is a niche within that; label TAM as estimate.

**Key assumptions:**
- Entry multiples: 3-4.5x SDE at Dan's scale ($200-500K SDE). NOT 8-10x.
- SBA 7(a): 10-year term, ~11% rate (2026), 10% down.
- Cash-on-cash = (SDE - debt service) / down payment. Approx annual debt service per $100K financed at 11%/10yr is ~$16.5K.
- Operator partner: required. Food safety, rice holding, throughput, prep, and labor scheduling are not passive-owner skills.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue range: $700K-$1.5M, ideally with lunch/daypart density and catering.
- SDE range: $100K-$250K after adding a paid GM.
- Years in business: 2+ with stable inspection history and repeat customer data.
- Route density / customer count: located near offices, subway/transit, hospitals, universities; 500+ daily footfall immediately accessible or signed institutional wholesale accounts.
- Key assets to verify: lease/food-hall agreement, permits, POS by SKU/daypart, commissary/prep workflow, shelf-life and food-safety controls, delivery app economics, brand/social traction.
- Red flags to avoid: low throughput, high delivery-app dependence, rice/seaweed quality issues, no standard recipes, rent/revenue share traps, concept novelty without repeat purchase.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- There are few pure-play U.S. onigiri franchises. Treat franchise path cautiously.
- Comparable brands to diligence: Onigiri Bongo-style independents, Sunny Blue, Omusubi Gonbei (global/Japan-origin), Musubi Cafe Iyasume, and broader Asian fast-casual systems like Teriyaki Madness or Pokeworks as operating comps.
- If a franchise emerges, require Item 19, food cost by SKU, labor model, commissary requirements, territory exclusivity, and closure history.
- For Dan, a licensing/operating partnership with an experienced chef-operator is more realistic than buying a franchise.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- Competitors: local sushi grab-and-go, poke shops, Korean kimbap counters, Japanese bakeries, convenience stores, Sweetgreen/Cava/Just Salad for lunch dollars, H Mart/Japanese markets, food halls.
- PE activity: broader Asian fast-casual has sponsor-backed chains, but pure onigiri is too niche/early in the U.S.; consolidation is not mature.
- Fragmentation level: Very high; few scaled brands. This is white space, which is both opportunity and risk.
- Moat: throughput, lease/location, commissary process, product consistency, brand/community, and wholesale/catering relationships.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- Typical deal structure: likely asset purchase of existing restaurant/counter or startup/kiosk build. Seller note valuable because transferability is uncertain.
- SBA eligibility: possible for profitable existing restaurant with tax returns; startup underwriting requires experienced operator, collateral, and realistic working capital.
- Down payment required: base $420K acquisition implies ~$42K down plus working capital; startup/kiosk may require $150K-$400K cash need depending buildout.
- Payback period at base case: 2-4 years if $120K SDE is real and revenue is not delivery-app dependent.
- Honest multiple arbitrage: this only works if one prep kitchen can support multiple counters/wholesale accounts; a single cute shop has limited exit multiple.

## 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)
- Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map NYC/Boston/Cambridge onigiri/kimbap/musubi operators; visit during lunch rush; collect menus/prices; interview 3 food operators.
- Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Build SKU-level model including rice, nori, protein, packaging, waste, labor minutes/unit; test office catering demand; identify an operator partner.
- Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Decide whether to pass, run a pop-up/wholesale MVP, or pursue one existing profitable counter with clean books.

## 8. Kill Criteria
- No operator partner with fast-casual or sushi/Asian prep experience.
- Unit cannot produce $1,500+ lunch-hour sales with acceptable wait time.
- Gross margin below 60% before labor or food waste above 8%-10%.
- Delivery apps exceed 30% of sales and erase margin.
- No commissary/multi-site path; only a single owner-operated storefront.
- Lease/food-hall rent share makes base case cash flow fragile.
