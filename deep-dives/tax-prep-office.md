# Tax Prep Office — Deep Dive Investment Memo

## Verdict: CONDITIONAL
Sticky seasonal cash-flow machine with strong recurring client economics, but extreme seasonality (90%+ of revenue in Q1–Q2), IRS regulatory risk, and software/AI disruption pressure on commodity returns. Best as acquisition of an established independent practice with Enrolled Agent (EA) or CPA talent, diversified into year-round bookkeeping/advisory — not a startup or a bare franchise kiosk.

## 1. Category Definition & Demand Map
- **What is this business?** A local office that prepares and files individual and small-business tax returns, typically generating the vast majority of revenue during tax season (January–April) with extensions and amended returns through October. Many operators also offer bookkeeping, payroll, notary, financial planning, and IRS representation to smooth revenue across the year.
- **Who are the customers?** Individuals (W-2 wage earners, retirees, gig workers, investors) and small businesses (sole props, S-corps, partnerships, LLCs). Heavily B2C with a B2B small-business component. IRS reports ~160 million individual returns filed annually; the National Society of Accountants (NSA) estimates ~80 million taxpayers use paid preparers.
- **Demand drivers:** Tax code complexity (sustained regardless of reform efforts), aging population needing retirement/estate-related filings, gig economy growth (Schedule C complexity), small-business formation wave (5M+ new business applications annually per SBA), and IRS enforcement funding increasing audit risk — which drives demand for professional representation.
- **Geographic relevance:** All three target markets work well. Boston/NE and NY/CT/NJ have high-income, high-complexity taxpayers (equity comp, multi-state, rental properties, S-corp). SFL has retirement-driven demand, no state income tax (simpler returns but high volume), and snowbird/multi-state filing needs. Dense suburban office parks and strip-mall locations with foot traffic are ideal.
- **Market size:** US tax preparation services industry ~$14B annually (IBISWorld estimate, 2025). Highly fragmented — H&R Block and Jackson Hewitt hold ~15–20% combined market share; the rest is split among ~150,000+ independent preparers, CPAs, and EAs. A single well-located independent office can generate $300K–$1.5M in annual revenue.

## 4. Path-Specific Buybox
What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?
- Revenue: $500K–$1.5M, SDE: $200K–$500K
- 500+ annual individual returns + 50+ business clients
- Year-round revenue diversification: bookkeeping, payroll, advisory, IRS representation (not just seasonal 1040 volume)
- Staff includes at least one EA or CPA who will stay post-sale
- Strong client retention rate (>80% year-over-year)
- Established referral pipeline (CPAs, attorneys, financial advisors, word-of-mouth)
- Clean office lease or owned location with good visibility
- Red flags: owner is sole preparer and holds all client relationships, revenue declining year-over-year, high concentration in a few large business clients, no year-round revenue, pending IRS regulatory issues, outdated technology/software

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?
- **H&R Block:** ~10,000 offices; initial investment $155K–$560K; franchise fee $30K; royalty ~30% of gross receipts (company-disclosed). Brand recognition is the main value — margins are thin after royalty.
- **Jackson Hewitt:** ~5,500 offices; initial investment $100K–$400K; franchise fee $25K–$40K; royalty ~25%. Similar trade-off: brand for margin.
- **Liberty Tax:** ~2,500 offices; initial investment $60K–$200K; franchise fee $40K; royalty ~20%+. Lower entry cost, weaker brand.
- **Independent is usually superior economics.** The franchise royalty (20–30% of gross) is a massive margin drag on a business that already has seasonality risk. An independent practice at 3.0x SDE with a retained EA will almost always outperform a franchise at 3.5x SDE minus 25% royalty.

## 5. Competitive Landscape
- **National chains:** H&R Block (~$3.5B revenue), Jackson Hewitt (~$500M), Liberty Tax — dominate volume individual returns with brand and walk-in convenience, but their margins are squeezed by royalty structures and wage costs.
- **CPA firms:** Local and regional CPA firms handle complex individual and business returns at higher price points. These are the most direct competitors for a high-quality independent practice.
- **Software/TurboTax/FreeTaxUSA:** Intuit's TurboTax dominates DIY filing (~40M+ users). AI-driven tax tools are eroding commodity individual return volume. This is the structural risk: simple returns commoditize toward $0–$50 software.
- **Enrolled Agents / Independent Preparers:** ~150,000+ nationwide. Highly fragmented. The best have deep client relationships and diversified services.
- **PE/consolidation:** Limited at the small-practice level. Some regional CPA firm roll-ups (CBIZ, CLA, Eide Bailly) acquire tax practices as add-ons, typically at 4–6x EBITDA for larger practices. Small independents remain fragmented.
- **Moat:** Client trust and relationship stickiness (people hate switching tax preparers), professional credentials (EA/CPA), complex-return expertise that software can't replicate, multi-year tax history knowledge, and referral networks. The moat is strongest for business returns and complex individual situations — weakest for simple W-2 filings.

## 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline
- **Deal structure:** Asset purchase — client lists, non-compete, office lease, equipment/software, phone number, website, and goodwill. Seller note (15–25%) tied to client retention over 1–2 tax seasons. Consider earnout based on first post-acquisition season's revenue.
- **SBA eligibility:** Strong — professional practices with documented recurring revenue are favored SBA 7(a) borrowers. Lender will want 2–3 years of tax returns (ironic but true), client retention data, staff retention commitments, and proof that the EA/CPA is staying.
- **Down payment at base case ($2.55M price):** ~$255K at 10% down.
- **Payback period:** ~1.5–2.5 years at base case if client retention holds above 80%. Seasonal cash flow means debt service planning is critical — Q1 generates the bulk of cash.
- **Multiple arbitrage:** Buy a pure tax-prep shop at 2.5–3.0x SDE, add year-round bookkeeping/payroll/advisory services, build recurring revenue, professionalize marketing, sell at 4.0–4.5x to a regional CPA roll-up. Realistic.

## 8. Kill Criteria
What would make this a NO-GO?
- Owner is sole EA/CPA and won't stay post-sale; no other credentialed staff
- Revenue mix is >80% simple individual returns — AI/software erosion will compress this
- Client retention is below 70% — indicates relationship is with the person, not the practice
- Revenue is declining year-over-year without a clear explanation
- Office location is poor or lease is untransferable
- Regulatory issues: IRS PTIN problems, state licensing violations, pending disciplinary actions
- Seasonality creates cash-flow gaps that debt service can't survive
- Franchise royalty (if franchise) leaves sub-15% net margin after manager salary
