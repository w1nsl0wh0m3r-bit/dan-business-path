Why this could fail: Kill if: (1) the target geography has low EV adoption (<5% of new registrations) and slow projected growth; (2) licensed electricians are unavailable or command wages that crush margin; (3) the seller is the only master electrician and the license is non-transferable; (4) the shop's revenue is >60% tied to a single general contractor or builder; (5) the service van fleet is aged and needs replacement within 18 months; (6) the local permitting process for EV installs is unreasonably slow (>4 weeks average).

Biggest thing to validate before committing: Talk to 3 operators and verify the unit economics model against real financials.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

Electrical service is a $200B+ licensed trade with a once-in-a-generation growth tailwind: EV charger installation. The EV charging infrastructure market is projected to grow from $8B to $100B+ by 2040, and every installation requires a licensed electrician. This isn't speculative — EV adoption is policy-driven (half of new car sales mandated to be EV by 2035 in CA, NY, and 8 other states) and the install work is non-negotiable. Buying an established electrical shop gives you the license, crew, and customer base to capture this wave.

Demand drivers and recurrence: Demand is driven by structural need and local market density. Recurrence varies by service line — some categories generate repeat business naturally, others require active reactivation.

Geographic fit: Target markets: Boston/NE, NY/CT/NJ, Miami/SFL. Evaluate local population density, income levels, and competitive density before committing to any territory.

Capital discipline rules: Never pay more than 3x verified SDE. Never assume you can improve margins by more than 5 percentage points. Budget for a 12-month transition where the seller is still involved. Model worst case at 80% of current revenue.

For a small business path, the danger is not missing a huge market; it is buying or building a job. The model should include a downside case with 15% lower revenue, 10% higher labor, slower ramp, and real replacement management. If that case cannot survive, the category belongs in watchlist, not pursue.

8. AI / Systems Edge

Moderate-to-strong AI edge: (1) Automated quoting — upload photos of the electrical panel, AI estimates capacity, recommends charger type, and generates an instant quote. (2) Permit automation — auto-fill permit applications based on job details, track approval status, schedule inspections. (3) Route scheduling — optimize electrician dispatch based on job type, skill requirements, and geographic clustering. (4) Lead generation partnerships — API integrations with ChargePoint, Tesla, EVGo to receive installation referrals. (5) CRM/reactivation — track customer vehicle purchases (via data partnerships), auto-trigger charger install outreach 30 days after EV purchase. (6) Inventory management — predict material needs based on scheduled jobs, auto-reorder from suppliers.

Where AI creates real advantage vs table stakes: Lead capture and missed-call recovery is table stakes in 2025. Real advantage comes from predictive scheduling, automated reactivation campaigns, and data-driven pricing that most small operators will never implement.
