HOA / Association Management — Deep Dive Investment Memo

Full investment analysis: unit economics, competitive landscape, buybox criteria, and 90-day roadmap.

CONDITIONAL Acquisition Confidence: Medium-High

Executive Verdict

Attractive on recurrence and fragmentation, but only if Dan buys a manager-run book of associations with clean retention.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A community-association management company handles day-to-day operations for homeowner and condominium associations — collecting dues, paying vendors, enforcing rules, managing reserves, running board meetings, and coordinating maintenance. Revenue comes from per-door monthly management fees plus à la carte project and compliance services.
  • Who are the customers? B2B — HOA and condo association boards. Each board is a recurring client that typically renews annually. The end consumer is the homeowner, but the buyer is the elected board.
  • Demand drivers: US has ~370,000 community associations housing ~75M residents (Community Associations Institute 2024 estimate). New construction continues to be HOA-governed. Aging associations need more professional management as volunteer burnout rises. Recurrence is near-automatic unless the board fires you.
  • Geographic relevance: NE/Boston suburbs, CT/NJ, and South Florida are all dense with HOA/condo communities. Florida alone has ~49,000 associations. Massachusetts and Connecticut have significant condo-stock concentrations. National TAM is large; Dan should focus on one metro cluster for density.
  • Market size: IBISWorld estimates Property Management (NAICS 53131) at ~$115B revenue in the US, of which HOA/community-association management is a meaningful but not separately reported subset. Estimate HOA-specific management revenue at $15B-$25B nationally based on ~370K associations × average fees.

Key assumptions:

  • Entry multiples: 3-4.5x SDE at Dan's scale ($200K-$500K SDE). Not sponsor-platform multiples (which run 6-10x EBITDA for FirstService-scale).
  • SBA 7(a): 10-year term, ~11% rate (2026), 10% down.
  • Cash-on-cash = (SDE - debt service) / down payment.
  • Operator partner: Dan can hire a licensed CAM (Community Association Manager) as operator partner. Many states require CAM licensing; Florida requires state licensure (DBPR Chapter 718/720).

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Revenue range: $750K-$2.0M with diversified portfolio of 15-40+ associations.
  • SDE range: $175K-$450K before manager adjustment; target $200K+ net of a qualified CAM hire.
  • Years in business: 7+ years with long-tenured association contracts.
  • Route density: managed communities clustered in one county or metro area; no 90-minute drives between board meetings.
  • Key assets to verify: association contracts (term, cancellation clause), management software (AppFolio, TOPS, Caliber), CAM licenses, trust/escrow accounts, D&O and E&O insurance, employee non-competes.
  • Red flags to avoid: one board-member relationship drives 30%+ revenue; owner is the only licensed CAM; reserve-study liabilities; pending HOA litigation; deferred maintenance complaints.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Top franchise/system brands: Real Property Management (franchise), PMI (Property Management Inc., franchise), Associa (not franchise but acquires operators), FirstService Residential (public acquirer), Community Management Associates.
  • Typical franchise fee: $40K-$70K; royalties 5%-8% of managed revenue. Verify current FDDs.
  • FDD highlight: Item 19 for average unit revenue; Item 20 for franchisee turnover; check whether national accounts or lead-generation actually benefits local franchisees.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Top competitors / consolidators: FirstService Residential (FSRV, public, ~$3.5B revenue), Associa (~$2B+ revenue, PE-backed), RealManage, Keystone Pacific, The Management Trust, Seacrest Services (SFL), local New England operators (MCM, Ward Group, Crossbridge).
  • PE activity: Significant at scale. FirstService and Associa buy platforms in the $5M-$50M+ range. Below that, the market is deeply fragmented with thousands of local CAM shops.
  • Fragmentation level: Very high below the top-20 national operators. Most metro areas have 10-30 local competitors with 5-25 associations each.
  • Moat: board relationships and contract renewals, CAM licensing barriers, software/operations platform, trust-account compliance, and local reputation among developer/attorney referral networks.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Typical deal structure: asset purchase with seller note (10%-20%) tied to contract retention over 12 months; working-capital adjustment for trust accounts; transition services agreement for 6-12 months.
  • SBA eligibility: strong candidate for SBA 7(a) — recurring revenue, transferable contracts, service business. DSCR must support ~11% debt service.
  • Down payment required: ~10% of purchase price plus closing costs and operating reserve. On a $960K deal, roughly $96K-$140K cash.
  • Payback period at base case: 2.5-4 years if retention holds above 85% and manager-run SDE is validated.
  • Honest multiple arbitrage: buy at 3.5-4.0x SDE, professionalize operations/add density, potential exit at 4.0-5.0x to a FirstService or Associa if you reach $2M+ EBITDA.

7. 90-Day Roadmap

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map top 30 local CAM operators in target metro (Boston, CT, or SFL). Scrape reviews, check licensing databases, call 3-5 brokers for listing flow. Request FDDs from Real Property Management and PMI.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Interview 5 CAM operators (buy lunch). Identify potential operator-partner (licensed CAM). Collect 3 sample P&Ls from brokers. Build valuation model. Visit 2-3 targets.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on best-fit target if retention, manager-adjusted SDE, and density clear the model. Otherwise kill or park.

8. Kill Criteria

What would make this a NO-GO?

  • Manager-adjusted SDE below $200K after paying a qualified CAM $70K-$90K.
  • Seller cannot produce association-level revenue, renewal history, and trust-account audits.
  • Top 3 associations exceed 40% of revenue without long-term contracts.
  • Business requires owner's personal board relationships or CAM license with no transferable bench.
  • State licensing requirements (e.g., FL DBPR) create barriers Dan cannot meet without hiring a licensed CAM pre-close.
  • Pending litigation from associations, reserve-study deficiencies, or D&O claims.
