Medical Equipment / Mobility Retail / 101 Mobility — Deep Dive

Full franchise analysis on medical equipment / mobility retail (101 mobility). Operator economics, competitive landscape, diligence framework, and 90-day roadmap.

Franchise Conditional Franchise UW Score: 4.00 Confidence: Medium-High

Executive Verdict

Conditional GO — attractive only if focused on private-pay mobility/accessibility, not reimbursement-heavy DME.

101 Mobility has real scale, disclosed FDD data, and aging-demographic tailwinds. The risk is technician availability, local referral generation, and margin compression from equipment suppliers/online competitors.

Recommended action: Get the current FDD, call 12 franchisees, and compare a 101 Mobility resale/open territory against an independent accessibility/DME acquisition.

Why this could work: Needs-based aging-in-place demand, private-pay stairlifts/ramps/lifts, recurring service/rental/used equipment opportunities, and a fragmented local competitor base.

Why this could fail: One territory may not support a paid GM and debt; referral development is slow; install tech hiring can bottleneck growth; reimbursement-heavy DME adds compliance and collection risk.

Biggest thing to validate before committing: Whether mature territories can clear at least $150K manager-adjusted EBITDA and whether franchisees say owner sales involvement is mandatory.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

Medical equipment/mobility retail should be underwritten around private-pay accessibility: stairlifts, ramps, vertical platform lifts, bathroom safety, patient lifts, rentals, used/refurbished equipment, and installation service.

Demand drivers and recurrence: Aging population, aging-in-place preference, hospital discharge needs, VA/family caregiver demand, maintenance contracts, rentals, and escalating accessibility needs over time.

Geographic fit: Strong in senior-dense markets and hospital/VA-rich territories. Boston/NE, RI/CT/NH, and Florida are logical screens, but territory senior density and hospital relationships matter more than MSA size.

Core buyer
Adult children (45-65) buying for aging parents; discharge planners arranging home modifications post-hospitalization; VA coordinators for veterans; OTs prescribing specific equipment after assessment
Revenue engine
Equipment sale + install leads to annual maintenance contract which feeds referral flywheel from OT/PT/CM network which generates repeat/expand revenue as patient needs escalate over time
Hard part
Building referral relationships takes 12-18 months. OTs and case managers are loyal to existing suppliers. You earn trust one successful install at a time. There is no shortcut — it is relationship farming, not relationship hunting.

Why this path (Franchise) for this category: Franchise is the default here because the product knowledge (stair lift brands, Medicare billing codes, ADA specs) and referral network take years to build independently. The franchise provides training, supplier relationships, and a recognized brand that OTs trust. However, an experienced DME dealer could go independent and save the 9% royalty/marketing drag. The comparison is close enough that both paths deserve modeling.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

Primary path: Franchise.

  • Territory must have 100K+ seniors (65+) within 30-mile radius
  • At least 3 hospitals with active discharge planning departments
  • Existing DME competition is fragmented (no dominant player with more than 15% share)
  • VA medical center within territory for VA contract eligibility
  • Warehouse/showroom lease available under $18/sqft
  • Owner can commit to 12-month referral-building ramp before meaningful revenue
  • Franchisee satisfaction: confirm with at least 5 current franchisees before signing

What disqualifies a deal in this category: Territory with fewer than 50K seniors, only 1 hospital, and no VA center. Existing franchisee with pending litigation against franchisor. Territory where 2+ mobility dealers already have strong OT relationships. Medicare billing compliance issues.

Underwriteable cash flow = recurring/repeat gross profit - paid manager cost - normalized marketing - maintenance capex - owner heroics.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

Valuation frame
Independent mobility/DME: 2.0x-3.5x SDE. 101 Mobility resale: 2.5x-4.0x if clean and growing.
Financing frame
SBA 7(a), seller financing on resales, and equipment/inventory financing where appropriate.

Capital discipline rules: Require $150K+ manager-adjusted EBITDA, verify private-pay mix, avoid reimbursement complexity, and do not underwrite referrals that do not transfer.

For a small business path, the danger is not missing a huge market; it is buying or building a job. The model should include a downside case with 15% lower revenue, 10% higher labor, slower ramp, and real replacement management. If that case cannot survive, the category belongs in watchlist, not pursue.

7. Diligence Checklist

Before spending real time or money, every item below needs a clear answer. Blank boxes are loose ends. Loose ends kill deals.

Customer diligence
  • Call 5 OTs/discharge planners: who do they refer mobility equipment to and why?
  • Ask referral sources: what would make you switch suppliers?
  • Review Google reviews (target: 4.5+ stars, 50+ reviews)
  • Check Medicare/Medicaid billing compliance history for any red flags
  • Verify insurance referral pipeline volume (monthly referral count by source)
  • Interview adult children buyers: what was their decision process?
Operator diligence
  • Installer training: how long, what certifications are needed?
  • Warehouse and inventory management system quality
  • Emergency service capability (ramp or lift breakdown means patient is stuck)
  • Staffing model: how many installers per $500K revenue?
  • Seasonality patterns (spike after hospital discharge seasons, flu season)
  • Product liability insurance adequacy
Financial diligence
  • Request 3 years of tax returns from any resale territory
  • Verify maintenance contract renewal rate (above 70% is good)
  • Check inventory aging report (older than 90 days is a problem)
  • Separate owner salary and perks from true SDE
  • Confirm all referral sources transfer with sale (get it in writing)
  • Review accounts receivable aging — Medicare payments can lag 60-90 days
Growth diligence
  • Adjacent product lines: bathroom modifications, vehicle lifts, ceiling lifts, vertical platform lifts
  • Commercial revenue opportunity: ADA compliance work for small businesses
  • VA contract expansion potential within territory
  • Geographic expansion within territory boundaries
  • Partnership with home health agencies for bundled service offering
  • Smart-home aging-in-place technology integration (fall detection, medication reminders)

8. AI / Systems Edge

AI adds value in three places: lead capture (never miss a referral call), routing optimization (installer scheduling across territory), and CRM-driven reactivation (maintenance contract renewals). The referral-driven model means every missed call is a lost $3K-$7K job. An AI receptionist that captures after-hours referral calls could pay for itself in the first week.

Where AI creates real advantage vs table stakes: Lead capture and missed-call recovery is table stakes. Real advantage comes from predictive scheduling (estimating install time based on equipment type and home configuration) and automated reactivation campaigns triggered by warranty expiration dates. Most mobility dealers have zero CRM infrastructure — any system is a competitive advantage.

9. Risk Register & Kill Criteria

Every risk below has a corresponding kill criterion. If any kill criterion is triggered during diligence, walk away. No deal is worth overriding a kill criterion.

  • Referral dependency: 12-18 month ramp with minimal revenue while building OT/CM relationships — this is the number one risk
  • Insurance and Medicare billing complexity: claim denials, changing reimbursement codes, compliance audits
  • Product liability: improper stair lift or ramp installation can cause serious injury or death
  • Inventory carrying cost: stair lifts ($3-7K each) and modular ramps require warehouse space and working capital
  • Territory saturation: 194 locations means some markets have multiple franchisees competing
  • Succession risk: if franchisee exits, referral relationships may not transfer to buyer
  • Technology disruption: smart home and aging-in-place tech (stair-assist chairs, home elevators) could reduce traditional equipment demand
  • Franchisor risk: royalty increases, territory disputes, or franchisor financial problems

Explicit kill criteria: Unable to confirm at least 3 franchisees who would do it again. Revenue declining year over year. Medicare compliance issues that cannot be resolved. Territory with fewer than 50K seniors. No viable path to positive cash flow within 18 months in the base case model.

11. Operator Reality

These are not hypothetical. They come from operator interviews, Reddit threads, YouTube walkthroughs, and industry forums. Take them seriously.

Where operators win

  • Building deep OT/CM relationships creates a referral moat that is extremely hard to displace
  • Maintenance contracts create predictable recurring revenue on the installed base (target 60%+ attach rate)
  • VA contracts provide reliable government revenue stream with predictable payment cycles
  • Emergency and urgent installs (post-surgery, post-discharge) command premium pricing and build loyalty
  • Cross-selling naturally: stair lift customer becomes ramp customer becomes grab bar customer becomes bathroom mod customer
  • Referral sources compound over time: one happy OT refers to their entire department and to other departments
  • Low overhead model: warehouse + small showroom + 1-2 installers + owner-operator = lean cost structure

Where owners lose money

  • Underestimating the 12-18 month referral ramp and burning through cash reserves while building relationships
  • Carrying too much inventory in slow-moving stair lift SKUs while running out of fast-moving ramp components
  • Hiring installers who lack patience and precision for elderly customer interactions — one bad experience kills the referral source
  • Ignoring maintenance contracts and leaving $50-100K/year of recurring revenue on the table
  • Competing on price against Amazon for commodity items instead of selling installation quality and trust
  • Not tracking referral source attribution and being unable to double down on what works
  • Taking on too much territory too fast before the referral network is solid in the core area

The one thing operators consistently say: The number one thing I hear from mobility equipment dealers: 'I wish I had started treating this like a real business from day one instead of winging it for the first two years.' The ones who invest in CRM, inventory management, and referral tracking from the start consistently outperform those who try to keep it all in their head.

12. Competitive Landscape & Whitespace

Whitespace analysis looks at what competitors are NOT doing, not what they are. The best opportunities are service gaps, geographic gaps, and customer segments that incumbents ignore.

  • Geographic whitespace: many mid-size metros (200-500K population) have no dedicated mobility equipment dealer — just general DME suppliers
  • Service gap: most DME companies are order-takers, not consultants. There is room for a concierge mobility advisor model that does home assessments and recommends holistic solutions
  • Commercial whitespace: ADA compliance work for small businesses (restaurants, retail, medical offices) is underserved by mobility-focused dealers
  • Bundling whitespace: combining mobility equipment with smart-home aging-in-place tech (fall detection sensors, medication reminders, voice-activated home controls)
  • VA whitespace: many territories under-serve the VA channel, which is a reliable payer with predictable volume
  • After-hours and weekend emergency service is almost never offered and could be a meaningful differentiator
  • Multi-family and senior living community bulk installations are an untapped channel for most dealers

13. Dashboard Score Reality Check

The 101 Mobility score mostly holds because the demand is needs-based, local, and tied to aging-in-place decisions that families cannot defer forever. The score is not a clean "franchise win," though — it is really a local relationship-sales and installation business with a franchise wrapper.

What holds up

  • The demographic score holds because stair lifts, ramps, scooters, and bathroom safety are triggered by falls, surgeries, VA benefits, and discharge events — not by consumer whim.
  • The resilience score holds because the customer often has an urgent need and a third-party influencer: OT, discharge planner, VA case manager, adult child, or home health agency.
  • Installation and service are underrated; the best operators are not just selling equipment, they own measurement, install quality, maintenance, rentals, and repairs.
  • Territory economics can work if referral density is high; top territories prove there is a path above $1M revenue without needing a huge fixed asset base.
  • The franchise value is real if it lowers vendor, training, quoting, and local SEO friction fast enough to offset the royalty.

What is overstated

  • This is overrated if the model assumes demographic growth automatically converts into leads. Referral trust can take 12-24 months and weak operators will starve before the market catches up.
  • The downside risk is territory dispersion: a bottom-quartile territory can look like a job with warehouse rent, inventory, vans, and 9% system fees attached.
  • The score should move down if Medicare/Medicaid/VA billing is required for meaningful volume and the franchisee lacks reimbursement discipline.
  • The score should move up if calls prove repeat service, rentals, and referral accounts create >25% recurring or repeat revenue within three years.
  • The honest risk is that the business rewards patient local networking more than Dan's analytical edge; the moat is relationships and execution, not data.
  • Inventory mistakes are painful: slow-moving scooters, lifts, and parts tie up cash while urgent jobs still require fast availability.

16. 30 / 60 / 90 Roadmap

Days 1 to 30

Discovery and FDD Review

  • Request 101 Mobility FDD and review Item 19 (financials), Item 6 (fees), Item 12 (territory) in detail
  • Call 5 franchisees (mix of quartiles) focusing on ramp timeline, support quality, and economics reality
  • Research territory availability in target markets (Boston/NE, NY/CT/NJ, Miami/SFL)
  • Talk to SBA lender about pre-qualification for franchise loan and understand terms
  • Visit a local 101 Mobility or Amramp operation to see the warehouse setup and meet the team
  • Start relationship with 2-3 OTs to understand referral dynamics from their perspective

Kill criteria: If franchisees universally report dissatisfaction, or if no attractive territories are available in target markets, pause and reassess.

Days 31 to 60

Validation and Comparison

  • Build independent vs franchise side-by-side financial model with three-case scenarios
  • Map referral sources in target territory: hospitals, VA centers, rehab centers, home health agencies
  • Evaluate territory demographics: senior population density, competitor landscape, median income, hospital count
  • Negotiate territory if proceeding with franchise; confirm boundaries, exclusivity, and protection radius
  • If considering independent path: source stair lift and ramp suppliers, compare wholesale pricing to franchise GPO rates
  • Secure SBA pre-approval and identify warehouse/showroom lease options in target territory

Kill criteria: If financial model shows negative cash flow in base case for more than 18 months, or independent path is clearly superior to franchise, pivot accordingly.

Days 61 to 90

Decision and Launch Planning

  • Make franchise vs independent decision with full financial conviction — no remaining loose ends
  • If franchise: sign agreement, begin training program, secure territory and start OT outreach
  • If independent: file business formation, secure supplier agreements, begin building OT referral relationships
  • Hire first installer (experienced, patient, excellent elderly-customer-service skills, clean background)
  • Set up CRM with referral source tracking, call handling, and maintenance contract management from day one
  • Launch referral outreach program targeting OTs, discharge planners, VA coordinators, and home health agencies

Kill criteria: If unable to identify a concrete path to positive cash flow within 18 months, shelve the category and move to the next dashboard item.

Final Recommendation

101 Mobility is one of the stronger franchise plays in the dashboard. The demographic tailwind is undeniable, the SBA default rate is low, and the referral-driven moat is real once established. The key risk is the 12-18 month referral ramp — Dan needs to be comfortable funding operations during that period. If he can secure a territory with high senior density, multiple hospitals, and a VA center, this is a Pursue. Compare carefully with the independent path — the 9% royalty needs to buy real lead flow and purchasing power that justifies the drag. This is a long-term compounding play, not a quick flip.
