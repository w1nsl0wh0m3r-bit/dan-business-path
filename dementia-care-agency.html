1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A dementia care agency provides non-medical home care, companionship, ADL support, respite care, and memory-care trained caregivers for clients with Alzheimer’s or related dementia. Medical skilled home health is different; this thesis is private-pay/non-medical unless licenses allow more.
  • Who are the customers? Customers are adult children, spouses, care managers, elder-law attorneys, hospitals/discharge planners, senior communities, and Medicaid waiver/private-pay clients depending on state. B2C decision-maker, but referral networks drive demand.
  • Demand drivers: aging population, desire to age at home, caregiver burnout, and Alzheimer’s prevalence. The Alzheimer’s Association estimates nearly 7 million Americans 65+ live with Alzheimer’s in 2024; Census projects older-adult growth through 2030.
  • Geographic relevance: NE/NY/CT/NJ and SFL are both excellent; SFL has dense retiree demand but labor competition.
  • Market size: home care is large nationally, but dementia-specific revenue should be modeled from local 75+ population, income, referral sources, and caregiver availability.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Buy $1.5M-$5M revenue, $250K-$750K SDE, 8+ years, licensed where required, strong scheduler/recruiter, low caregiver turnover, diversified referral sources, clean payroll/classification, and meaningful private-pay mix. Avoid Medicaid-only margin traps, live-in labor compliance risk, seller as only rainmaker, and poor caregiver files.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Franchise candidates: Home Instead, Right at Home, Visiting Angels, Comfort Keepers, Senior Helpers, Home Helpers, ComForCare. Current FDDs needed for fees/royalties; Item 19 often reports gross revenue ranges, but local caregiver recruiting and referral execution dominate.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Home Instead/Honor, Right at Home, Visiting Angels, Comfort Keepers, Senior Helpers, BrightStar Care, Griswold, Home Helpers, ComForCare, local agencies. PE and strategic activity has been steady in home care/franchise platforms. Fragmentation remains high. Moat is caregiver recruiting/retention, referral relationships, care coordination, scheduling utilization, and compliance.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Usually asset purchase with license-transfer analysis, employee/payroll diligence, seller note, and retention payments for scheduler/recruiter. SBA can work if DSCR survives caregiver wage pressure. A $1.4M base deal implies ~$140K down; payback can be 2.5-4 years. Multiple arbitrage is buying a local agency at 3.25-3.75x and exiting as a systematized multi-branch agency around 4x+

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map local competitors, licenses/regulatory requirements, broker listings, franchise FDD requests, and 20-30 possible operators/partners.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Build acquisition model, diligence 3-5 targets, verify customer/revenue retention, speak with lenders, and test operator partner fit.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on first target or reject category; include seller transition, retention/earnout mechanics, and diligence gates.

8. Kill Criteria

What would make this a NO-GO?

  • Caregiver turnover above market with no recruiting engine
  • Material wage/hour or misclassification exposure
  • Referral sources concentrated in one owner relationship
  • License transfer uncertain
  • Medicaid reimbursement dependence with thin margin
  • No scheduler/recruiter bench
