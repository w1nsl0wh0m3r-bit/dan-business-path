1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? Junk removal hauls unwanted furniture, appliances, construction debris, estate cleanouts, eviction debris, and light commercial waste using box trucks and crews. It can extend into dumpster rental, moving labor, donation/recycling, and property cleanouts.
  • Who are the customers? Customers are homeowners, renters, realtors, estate attorneys, landlords, property managers, contractors, storage facilities, and commercial offices. B2C is lead-gen heavy; B2B repeat accounts are the underwriteable core.
  • Demand drivers: moving, downsizing, renovation, estate turnover, storage cleanouts, eviction cycles, and consumer convenience. The U.S. EPA reports hundreds of millions of tons of municipal solid waste annually, which anchors the waste backdrop even though junk removal is a subset.
  • Geographic relevance: NY/CT/NJ and Boston dense suburbs are strong; SFL is strong for retirees, cleanouts, and hurricane-related debris but disposal costs/competition matter.
  • Market size: use franchise disclosures and local call volume; public junk-removal TAM estimates vary, so treat them as directional only.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Buy $700K-$2M revenue, $200K-$450K SDE, 2-6 trucks, CRM with repeat accounts, trained crew leaders, disposal/recycling relationships, strong reviews, and diversified lead sources. Avoid lead-gen arbitrage businesses, unsafe crews, high workers comp issues, undocumented cash, and old trucks.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Franchise candidates: 1-800-GOT-JUNK, Junk King, College HUNKS Hauling Junk & Moving, The Junkluggers, redbox+ Dumpsters. FDD diligence should focus on mature-unit revenue, call-center/marketing fees, truck ramp curve, and whether royalties erase the advantage versus buying an independent route.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • 1-800-GOT-JUNK, Junk King/Neighborly, College HUNKS, The Junkluggers, LoadUp, local haulers, dumpster rental operators, and waste companies. Neighborly acquired Junk King, and platform activity exists around home-service franchisors, but the base market is still fragmented. Moat is brand/reviews, dispatch density, B2B referral accounts, labor culture, and disposal cost advantage.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Asset purchase with fleet inspection, seller note, retention of crew leaders, and normalized marketing spend. SBA can finance, but lenders will haircut cash-heavy books and owner addbacks. At a $840K base price, 10% down is ~$84K and modeled year-one cash-on-cash can be high if labor/disposal costs hold. Do not pay more than 4x unless B2B repeat revenue is proven.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map local competitors, licenses/regulatory requirements, broker listings, franchise FDD requests, and 20-30 possible operators/partners.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Build acquisition model, diligence 3-5 targets, verify customer/revenue retention, speak with lenders, and test operator partner fit.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit LOI on first target or reject category; include seller transition, retention/earnout mechanics, and diligence gates.

8. Kill Criteria

What would make this a NO-GO?

  • Revenue mostly paid search with no repeat accounts
  • Truck fleet replacement cliff
  • Disposal fees rising faster than pricing
  • Crew turnover/safety problems
  • No CRM/job-level gross margin
  • Franchise royalties leave too little owner cash flow
