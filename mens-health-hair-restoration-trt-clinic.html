1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • Clinics sell testosterone evaluation/therapy, ED care, hair restoration/transplants, PRP, prescription hair-loss protocols and body-composition services.
  • Customers are adult men; B2C cash-pay dominates with some insurance or HSA/FSA usage depending on service.
  • Demand drivers: aging male population, aesthetics destigmatization, telehealth normalization and GLP-1/body composition adjacency.
  • Geographic relevance: strong in affluent NE/NY/SFL metros; physician-supervision and corporate-practice rules vary by state.
  • Market size: Grand View Research estimates global men’s health and hair restoration services as multi-billion-dollar markets; local clinic SAM is an estimate.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Revenue range: $1.2M-$3.0M with diversified service mix.
  • SDE range: $250K-$600K after clinician/medical director and paid operator costs.
  • Years in business: 3+ years with EMR, ad-spend history, patient cohorts and compliance documentation.
  • Route density / customer count: 500+ active patients or strong membership base; acquisition CAC tracked by channel.
  • Key assets to verify: physician/MSO contracts, licenses, EMR retention cohorts, prescription protocols, malpractice coverage, ad account data.
  • Red flags to avoid: aggressive prescribing, unclear CPOM structure, single influencer/seller brand, no medical governance, ad CAC rising.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Gameday Men’s Health, Low T Center-style clinics, Bosley/HairClub, Restore Hair and regional medspa/MSO concepts.
  • Top diligence: FDD Item 19, medical-director structure, royalty/marketing drag, lead-gen source and state-by-state legal model.
  • Franchise fees/royalties vary widely; portals commonly cite ~$49K initial fees and mid-single-digit royalties, but FDD governs.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Competitors: Hims & Hers, Ro, Bosley, HairClub, Gameday Men’s Health, Low T Center, local urology/derm practices, medspas.
  • PE activity: active in dermatology, medspa and urology MSOs; strategic interest exists but compliance quality separates winners.
  • Fragmentation: still fragmented locally; online prescription platforms pressure simple medication revenue.
  • Moat: compliant care model, outcomes, reviews, patient retention, CAC discipline, physician trust.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Typical deal: asset purchase/MSO interest purchase with physician transition, seller note, compliance reps and patient-retention earnout.
  • SBA eligibility: possible, but legal structure must be lender-comfortable; CPOM and healthcare ownership rules are central.
  • Down payment required: ~10%-20% plus legal/compliance reserve.
  • Payback period at base case: 3-5 years.
  • Honest multiple arbitrage: 3.5-4.0x in for small clinics; only assume >4x exit with clean cohorts and scalable compliance.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map state CPOM rules, request FDDs, identify physician/operator partner requirements.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Review EMR cohorts, prescriptions, CAC, refund/chargeback data and clinical governance.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): LOI only after healthcare counsel validates structure and paid-operator model works.

8. Kill Criteria

  • Regulatory structure is ambiguous or aggressive.
  • SDE relies on underpaid physician/seller labor.
  • Weak retention or CAC rising faster than LTV.
  • Any sign of unsafe prescribing or marketing claims.
  • Revenue is mostly one-time hair procedures without pipeline visibility.
