Home Accessibility / Aging-in-Place — Franchise Deep Dive

Decision-grade franchise diligence on home accessibility and aging-in-place modifications: 101 Mobility, TruBlue, Next Day Access, Mobility Plus, unit economics, SBA default signals, kill criteria, expert-call plan, and 90-day roadmap.

Executive Verdict

Franchise, but only with 101 Mobility as the lead candidate; keep TruBlue as a lower-capex hedge and kill Mobility Plus / Next Day Access unless fresh franchisee calls explain their SBA risk.

Best franchisor candidate: 101 Mobility — strongest combination of category purity, Item 19 disclosure, clear product-market fit, mature unit base, and acceptable SBA default signal.

Why now: aging demographics, caregiver shortages, fall-risk awareness, and the preference to age in place are pushing families toward stairlifts, ramps, grab bars, bathroom safety, and home safety assessments.

Why Dan: this is a diligence-first, territory-quality, referral-network, manager-economics problem — exactly where FDD discipline and sober underwriting matter.

Biggest risk: the best Item 19 numbers may come from mature territories and operator-led sales intensity that Dan cannot replicate in an available territory.

1. Category Definition & Franchise Landscape

Home accessibility businesses help seniors, disabled people, veterans, and temporarily injured customers stay safe and mobile at home. Core lines include stairlifts, wheelchair ramps, vertical platform lifts, patient lifts, grab bars, accessible showers, walk-in tubs, door widening, threshold ramps, railings, safety assessments, equipment rentals, repairs, and maintenance.

Pure-play mobility
101 Mobility, Next Day Access, Mobility Plus, Liftology
Senior handyman
TruBlue; aging-in-place angle plus maintenance plans
Adjacent bath remodel
ReBath, Bath Fitter, Five Star Bath; relevant but not pure thesis

The market is still fragmented. Franchises have national footprints, but most local supply is independent stairlift installers, medical equipment dealers, remodelers, handyman operators, and accessibility specialists. That fragmentation makes franchise plausible — but only if the franchisor’s training, vendor economics, brand trust, and referral support justify 8–10% of gross revenue.

3. Top 3 Franchisor Deep Dives

101 Mobility — lead candidate

FDDIQ profile: $181,850–$258,600 investment; $74,000 franchise fee; 7% royalty; 2% ad fund; Item 19 present; latest net unit change -4; SBA default 6.67% on 25 loans; FDDIQ distress corpus marks healthy.

Evaluation: the franchisor likely adds real value through product/vendor relationships, installation training, equipment category knowledge, referral positioning, and trust-heavy brand credibility. But the 9% gross fee burden is acceptable only if franchisee calls prove faster ramp, better vendor economics, and better referral conversion than an independent operator.

Recon read: established brand, franchising since 2010, national consumer/franchise infrastructure. The -4 unit-change datapoint must be explained before discovery day.

TruBlue Home Service Ally — low-capex hedge

FDDIQ profile: $70,050–$96,400 investment; $49,900 fee; 6% royalty; 2% ad; Item 19 present; FDDIQ median revenue $314,484; SBA default 0.0% on 13 loans; healthy distress signal.

Evaluation: lower-capex and attractive as a senior safety / home maintenance concept, but less pure than mobility equipment. It can collapse into a generic handyman labor business unless referral channels and recurring maintenance plans are real.

Next Day Access — high-revenue / high-risk

FDDIQ profile: $176,225–$352,050 investment; 8% royalty; 2% ad; Item 19 present; FDDIQ median revenue $702,876; +22 latest net unit change; SBA default 50.0% on 18 loans.

Evaluation: category-pure and product mix is attractive, but the SBA signal violates the default-rate doctrine. It is watch-only unless franchisor and operators can explain the performance.

4. Franchisee Voice & Operator Reality

Public franchisee voice is thin and mostly franchisor-curated. YouTube has 101 Mobility owner-experience interviews; useful but promotional. Reddit surfaced general small-franchise warnings rather than strong brand-specific complaints. That makes direct operator calls non-negotiable.

Positive themes to verify
  • Mission-driven work and demographic tailwind
  • Vendor and product training reduces mistakes
  • Healthcare/senior-care referrals can compound
  • Brand trust matters for vulnerable customers
Negative themes to test
  • Franchisor lead gen weaker than pitch
  • Owner must be the salesperson
  • Callbacks/warranty eat margins
  • Item 19 reflects mature outlier territories

7. Financing & Capital Structure

101 Mobility

Suggested structure: $160K–$180K SBA, $40K–$60K buyer equity, and $50K minimum working-capital reserve beyond the FDD estimate if manager-run.

TruBlue

Suggested structure: $40K–$50K buyer equity, $35K–$50K SBA/small business loan, and $25K–$40K working-capital reserve.

Financing verdict: 101 Mobility is financeable. TruBlue is financeable but may buy an owner job. Next Day Access and Mobility Plus require lender-level explanation before more time goes in.

8. Risks & Kill Criteria

Primary risks
  • Item 19 selection bias
  • Available territory weaker than published averages
  • Royalty drag destroys manager-run economics
  • Vendor/product margin compression
  • Install liability and warranty callbacks
  • Referral channels are relationship-driven
  • Permitting/licensed-sub requirements
Kill the deal if
  • Current FDD does not substantiate revenue claims
  • 3 franchisee calls do not support $1M+ year 3–5 potential
  • Paid-manager EBITDA cannot clear 12–15% by year 3
  • Franchisor lead gen is weak and all sales are self-generated
  • Dan must be daily salesperson/operator for 12+ months
  • Next Day Access / Mobility Plus cannot explain SBA defaults

9. Expert Call Plan

Franchisees: current and former

  1. What was your all-in startup cost including working capital?
  2. What revenue did you hit in years 1, 2, 3, and 5?
  3. Gross margin by line: stairlifts, ramps, platform lifts, grab bars, bathroom mods, service?
  4. What percent of leads are franchisor/national vs self-generated local referrals?
  5. Which channels work: OTs, hospitals, senior care, VA, Google, contractors, property managers?
  6. Can this be manager-run? At what revenue?
  7. Would you buy again?

Conviction-raising answer: operators crossed $1M by year 3–4, franchisor support shortened ramp, referrals are repeatable, and paid manager economics work.

Kill answer: brand gives little lead flow, owner must sell everything, margins are worse than FDD implies, callbacks are painful.

Franchise broker / resale specialist

Ask about resales, multiples, lender support, territory availability, and whether buyers pay a premium for the brand.

Franchise attorney

Review territory carveouts, national accounts, non-compete, vendor mandates, transfer fees, forced arbitration, renewal terms, and personal guarantees.

10. 30 / 60 / 90 Day Franchise Roadmap

Days 1–30: FDD + territory screen

Build the fact base

  • Request current FDDs for 101 Mobility, TruBlue, Next Day Access, Mobility Plus, Liftology
  • Build territory matrix for Boston/NE, NY/CT/NJ, Miami/SFL: 65+ population, income, homeownership, hospitals, rehab, VA, senior living, competitor count
  • Pull Item 19 tables into a model and separate average vs median vs top quartile
  • Call 3 current 101 Mobility franchisees and 1 TruBlue franchisee
  • Map independent competitors: stairlift installers, ramp companies, medical equipment dealers, aging-in-place contractors
Days 31–60: Validate

Franchisee calls + legal/economic diligence

  • Complete 8 franchisee/former franchisee calls
  • Interview an occupational therapist/discharge planner, senior-care agency, elder-law/referral source, and VA/veterans contact if possible
  • Have franchise attorney review the 101 Mobility FDD
  • Run lender prequalification with SBA lender
  • Stress test paid-manager P&L and working capital
  • Compare franchise startup to buying an independent accessibility dealer/installer
Days 61–90: Decide

Discovery day or kill

  • Attend 101 Mobility discovery day only if first 60 days validate economics
  • Negotiate territory clarity and written answers to support/lead-generation questions
  • Decide: sign 101 Mobility, pivot to independent acquisition search, or kill category
  • Build pre-opening plan only after final investment memo
  • Sign nothing until franchisee calls and territory math work.

11. Final Recommendation

Proceed to diligence on 101 Mobility as the primary franchise candidate. Do not commit capital yet.

Backup: TruBlue if Dan wants a lower-capex senior-home-services company and accepts that it may be closer to a handyman labor business than a pure accessibility equipment platform.

Pass/watch: Next Day Access is watch-only until SBA default is explained. Mobility Plus is pass until default is explained. Liftology is watchlist only. Bath remodelers are adjacent but not the right thesis.

Decision Dan needs to make: does he want to underwrite a ~$200K, territory-dependent, referral-heavy home accessibility franchise where upside is a manager-run platform and downside is buying a sales-and-install job? If yes, 101 Mobility is the best next target. If no, pursue an independent accessibility dealer/installer acquisition.

12. Winslow Augmentation — Avoid Double-Counting the Mobility Thesis

My upgrade: this overlaps heavily with the medical equipment / mobility retail page. Treat them as one broader thesis, not two separate bets. The attractive version is aging-in-place installation + service + referral network; the unattractive version is retail DME inventory with franchise fees layered on top.

Best formulation
  • Stairlifts, ramps, grab bars, bathroom safety, rentals, repairs, and assessments.
  • Private-pay customer base; reimbursement is incidental, not the model.
  • Referral engine from OTs/PTs, discharge planners, senior care agencies, elder-law attorneys, VA/veterans networks, and affluent adult children.
  • Available territory with old housing stock, high 70+ density, and high homeownership.
Key diligence upgrades
  • Separate product gross margin from install labor and callback/warranty burden.
  • Underwrite paid salesperson/GM by year 2; otherwise Dan bought himself a sales job.
  • Call former franchisees, not just current happy references.
  • Compare against acquiring an independent stairlift/accessibility installer at 2–3x SDE.

Relative ranking: interesting but not as clean as compliance-route services. It can be a good mission-aligned business, but I would not rank it above backflow, fire/life-safety, or a clean grease-trap route unless franchisee calls prove manager-run economics.
