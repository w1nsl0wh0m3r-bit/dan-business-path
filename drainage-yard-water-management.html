Drainage / Yard Water Management — Deep Dive

Full diy analysis on drainage / yard water management. Operator economics, competitive landscape, diligence framework, and 90-day roadmap.

DIY Conditional DIY UW Score: 3.60 Confidence: Medium-High

Executive Verdict

Screen-high — project-based water problem with storm tailwinds

This is a conditional GO and probably better as an add-on thesis to landscaping, basement waterproofing, or hardscaping than as a standalone first acquisition. Demand is real in New England: older homes, heavy rain events, grading issues, basements, sump systems, French drains, dry wells, catch basins, downspout routing, and erosion control.

Recommended action: Screen as an acquisition/add-on, not a cold-start DIY build.

Why this could work: Pain-driven, high-ticket residential leads with $3K-$15K projects and cross-sell into landscaping, grading, gutters, sump, crawlspace, and hardscape.

Why this could fail: Market data is fragmented, diagnosis quality matters, callbacks/warranty risk can eat margin, and many operators are really landscapers doing occasional drains.

Biggest thing to validate before committing: Confirm a technical estimator/crew leader exists and job-level gross margins/callbacks support 35%+ gross margin.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

Yard drainage is not a clean NAICS market; it sits inside landscaping, stormwater management, basement waterproofing, excavation, and hardscaping. The investable wedge is a local contractor that can diagnose water problems, sell high-ticket projects, and execute cleanly without warranty blowups.

Demand drivers and recurrence: Older housing stock, heavier rain events, grading failures, wet basements, sump discharge, downspout routing, erosion, and storm-triggered homeowner urgency. Recurrence is lower than maintenance services, so repeat/referred demand and adjacent cross-sell matter.

Geographic fit: Strongest in New England and older Northeast suburbs with basements, slopes, clay/poor soils, and high home values. Target Boston/NE first; screen NY/CT/NJ selectively.

Core buyer
Homeowners and property managers with visible flooding, basement seepage, erosion, soggy yards, or downspout/sump discharge problems.
Revenue engine
$3K-$15K French drain, regrading, dry well, catch basin, downspout, and sump-discharge projects; larger waterproofing/drainage jobs can exceed $25K.
Hard part
Technical diagnosis, excavation coordination, callback control, seasonality after storms, and making the business more than a rain-event lead spike.

Why this path (Acquisition/Add-on) for this category: Best version is buying a small drainage/waterproofing contractor with documented leads and bolting it onto landscaping, hardscape, or waterproofing capabilities.

2. Economics to Underwrite

Standalone local contractor estimate: $800K-$3M revenue, $3K-$15K average ticket, 35%-55% gross margin if self-performing, 12%-22% SDE/EBITDA for a well-run operator, 2.0x-3.2x SDE entry multiple, and 3-4.5 year payback.

Key insight: Drainage only works if the technical operator/estimator and crew execution transfer. If diagnosis lives in the seller's head, Dan is buying warranty risk and a job.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

Valuation frame
2.0x-3.2x SDE. Example: $270K SDE at 2.7x = $729K EV; do not pay for unproven storm-spike revenue.
Financing frame
SBA 7(a) plus seller note; require technical seller transition and equipment/callback reserves.

Capital discipline rules: Never pay above the manager-adjusted cash-flow case. Reserve for equipment replacement and warranties. Model field/operator GM at $85K-$120K plus bonus.

For a small business path, the danger is not missing a huge market; it is buying or building a job. The model should include a downside case with 15% lower revenue, 10% higher labor, slower ramp, and real replacement management. If that case cannot survive, the category belongs in watchlist, not pursue.

Gold-Tier Diligence Addendum

Why this made the top-100 cut: Drainage / Yard Water Management deserves a real diligence memo because the category has at least one structural feature worth underwriting: storm-driven pain, project tickets, and quote/process discipline. The dashboard score is only the entry point. The investment question is whether a local operator can convert that structural demand into durable, manager-run cash flow after replacing the seller, normalizing labor, and paying for real customer acquisition.

The attractive version of the business: The investable version is narrow. It has documented demand, clean books, repeat or recurring customer behavior, and a clear operating cadence that does not require the founder to personally save every job. It has a review moat, a defensible source of leads, and enough gross margin to pay a capable service manager without destroying SDE. It also has a simple customer promise: respond faster, quote cleaner, show up reliably, and make the buyer's problem disappear.

The bad version: The bad version looks similar from a broker memo but collapses under diligence. The seller is the lead generator, estimator, dispatcher, technician, and customer relationship. Revenue is lumpy, pricing is undocumented, employees are friends/family under market wages, and the P&L excludes real marketing, insurance, callbacks, vehicle/equipment replacement, software, or management labor. That is not an acquisition platform; it is buying someone's job with debt.

What must be true

  • Demand is local, visible, and not dependent on one channel or one personality.
  • Gross margin remains attractive after realistic labor, callbacks, warranty, waste, and travel time.
  • There is a repeat/reorder/referral loop — not a pure one-time marketing treadmill.
  • The workflow can be documented into checklists, scripts, pricing rules, and manager KPIs.
  • A buyer can improve the business with systems rather than heroic hustle.

What breaks the thesis

  • Owner is personally responsible for more than half of sales, estimating, or delivery quality.
  • Financials require aggressive add-backs to look attractive.
  • Customer acquisition cost is unknown or rising faster than ticket size.
  • Labor market is too thin to replace the seller or add a manager.
  • Revenue quality is worse than the headline — one-off, seasonal, concentrated, or discount-driven.

Dan-Specific Investment View

Dan should treat Drainage / Yard Water Management as a candidate for a very specific type of small-business platform: boring enough to have real demand, fragmented enough to professionalize, and operationally simple enough to understand quickly. The best version is not necessarily the biggest TAM. It is the category where local execution gaps are obvious and the improvement playbook is repeatable: better phones, better pricing, better follow-up, tighter scheduling, clearer reporting, cleaner upsells, and disciplined financial controls.

The category should be downgraded immediately if the path to manager-run ownership is fuzzy. A good small business can be bought at 2–4x SDE and improved. A founder-dependent job should not be bought at any multiple unless Dan wants to operate it personally. The diligence standard is therefore simple: prove that the customer demand survives without the seller, prove that job economics survive with real labor and management cost, and prove that growth can come from systems rather than vibes.

Bottom line: keep this in the top-100 only if the first 3–5 expert calls come back with concrete numbers and no obvious manager-run blocker. If the calls are hand-wavy, if the seller cannot produce clean data, or if the category requires hidden craft expertise that cannot be hired, move it below the line and focus on cleaner recurring-route or counter-distribution concepts.
