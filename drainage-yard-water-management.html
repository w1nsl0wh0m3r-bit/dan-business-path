For Drainage / yard water management, Dan should prove willingness to pay from homeowners with standing water, landscapers, builders, property managers, sell the first cohort/route/menu before committing to a lease, and kill it if repeat demand is weak.

Recommended action: Run a 30-day proof sprint with landing page, calls, presales, and a tiny operating pilot before signing a lease or hiring fixed staff.

Why this could work: The category has identifiable buyers — homeowners with standing water, landscapers, builders, property managers — and monetizable service lines: French drains, grading, downspout extensions, dry wells, maintenance plans. Why this could fail: The main traps are diagnostic skill, weather cycles, permitting, project gross margin. If those cannot be measured quickly, the dashboard score is not enough reason to proceed.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

Drainage / yard water management should be evaluated as a local operating system, not an abstract market. The investable question is whether a small operator can repeatedly acquire customers, deliver the service without founder heroics, and retain enough demand to support a paid manager. The customer set is primarily homeowners with standing water, landscapers, builders, property managers. The revenue pool comes from French drains, grading, downspout extensions, dry wells, maintenance plans, with upside from better scheduling, reminders, reviews, reactivation, and local SEO.

3. Path-Specific Buybox

Primary path: DIY / De Novo.

  • Customer proof: show real demand from homeowners with standing water, landscapers, builders, property managers through calls, local search, competitor density, and actual purchase behavior.
  • Service proof: confirm French drains, grading, downspout extensions, dry wells, maintenance plans can be delivered repeatedly with documented SOPs, margin, and quality control.
  • Manager-run proof: define who diagnoses jobs, sells scopes, schedules crews, calls 811/utility locates, manages permits, and handles callbacks; model crew lead wages and supervision before committing fixed costs.
  • Local moat: diagnostic trust, documented scopes, before/after proof, stormwater know-how, referral partnerships with landscapers/gutter firms, and low callback rates must matter more than generic marketing.
  • Kill discipline: kill if callback rate, utility-strike exposure, permitting delays, crew availability, weather dependency, or gross margin by job type remain unmeasured after 30 days.
Underwriteable cash flow = recurring/repeat gross profit − paid manager cost − normalized marketing − maintenance capex − owner heroics.

5. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

Valuation frame
Stage capital: <$10K proof sprint, then controlled pilot, then fixed-cost commitment only after repeatable CAC and fulfillment quality.
Financing frame
Avoid borrowing into unproven demand. Debt is acceptable only after booked recurring revenue or signed contracts cover fixed costs.

For a small business path, the danger is not missing a huge market; it is buying or building a job. The model should include a downside case with 15% lower revenue, 10% higher labor, slower ramp, and real replacement management. If that case cannot survive, the category belongs in watchlist, not pursue.

6. Diligence Checklist

Customer diligence
  • Interview 10 target customers and 5 referral/channel partners.
  • Pull competitor review themes: complaints, delight drivers, price objections.
  • Verify whether buyers repeat because they value the service or because they are locked into convenience/compliance.
  • Estimate local demand by neighborhood, not MSA market-size reports.
Operator diligence
  • Speak with 3 current operators and 2 former operators/franchisees.
  • Ask what breaks at 2x volume: labor, scheduling, quality, capex, or sales.
  • Get wage benchmarks and employee turnover reality.
  • Understand licensing, insurance, child/pet/health/safety, and premises obligations.
Financial diligence
  • Reconcile bank deposits, POS, booking system, tax returns, and customer export.
  • Separate one-time jobs from recurring cohorts/contracts/memberships.
  • Normalize owner compensation, personal expenses, one-off repairs, and underpaid family labor.
  • Confirm lease assignability, required capex, deposits, insurance, and working capital.
Growth diligence
  • Test if local SEO can move lead volume without wrecking CAC.
  • Quantify referral partnerships and review velocity.
  • Model second-unit or second-route economics only after first-unit utilization is real.
  • Identify automation opportunities in scheduling, reminders, review capture, quoting, and win-back.

7. AI / Systems Edge

AI is not the thesis; operational discipline is. The practical edge is using lightweight systems to remove leakage: missed calls, poor follow-up, no-show gaps, weak reviews, slow quotes, messy scheduling, and no customer reactivation. In a local category like Drainage / yard water management, the first automation layer should be CRM + call tracking + quoting templates + review requests + reactivation sequences. Only after the core workflow is stable should Dan add dynamic routing, demand forecasting, or automated content.

8. Risk Register & Kill Criteria

  • Callback trap: kill if warranty callbacks exceed modeled margin or diagnostics depend on one rainmaker.
  • Utility-strike trap: kill if crews cannot reliably manage 811 locates, buried utilities, excavation safety, and documentation.
  • Permitting trap: kill if municipal stormwater rules, HOA constraints, or drainage discharge limits delay projects or create liability.
  • Crew-availability trap: kill if skilled labor, equipment scheduling, or subcontractor reliability bottlenecks spring/fall demand.
  • Weather-dependency trap: kill if revenue spikes after storms but cannot be scheduled profitably through dry periods.

10. Operator Reality

Where operators win

  • Water problems create urgent, high-intent demand.
  • Tickets are meaningful enough to support paid acquisition if close rates are strong.
  • Many landscapers under-specialize in drainage diagnostics.
  • Before/after photos and video inspections create trust.
  • Can cross-sell waterproofing, gutters, grading, hardscape repair, and lawn restoration.

Where owners lose money

  • Bad diagnostics create expensive callbacks.
  • Permits, utilities, and stormwater rules vary locally.
  • Labor and excavation equipment coordination can be messy.
  • Weather creates demand spikes and scheduling chaos.
  • Founder expertise may be needed early to sell complex jobs.

11. Competitive Landscape & Whitespace

  • Competes with landscapers, basement waterproofers, gutter companies, hardscapers, and handyman crews.
  • Whitespace is a specialist water-management brand with clear diagnostics, photos, and packaged solutions.
  • DIY launch works through landing pages, storm-event campaigns, and a subcontractor bench.
  • Systems edge: inspection templates, quote calculators, job photos, weather-triggered campaigns.
  • Acquisition can come later by buying small drainage/landscape crews after demand proof.

15. 30 / 60 / 90 Roadmap

Days 1–30

Validate the local wedge

  • Build a 100-row local competitor and target map across Dan's preferred geographies.
  • Run 15 customer/referral/operator calls focused on repeat behavior, pricing, and service failures.
  • Draft a unit model with paid manager labor, conservative utilization, and downside CAC.
  • For acquisition: source 25 owner-led targets. For franchise: request FDD and resale list. For DIY: launch landing page/call campaign/presale test.
Days 31–60

Prove the path

  • Compare franchise, acquisition, and DIY economics side by side using the same manager-run assumptions.
  • Collect seller/franchisor/operator evidence: customer exports, Item 19 where relevant, booking data, wage data, review patterns.
  • Pressure-test labor hiring with real job posts or recruiter/operator conversations.
  • Identify 3 concrete ways systems could increase revenue or reduce leakage within 90 days post-close/post-launch.
Days 61–90

Commit, negotiate, or kill

  • Proceed only if demand, labor, and manager-run cash flow are proven with source-level evidence.
  • For acquisitions, submit IOIs only with working capital, transition, retention, and seller-note protections.
  • For franchises, proceed only after current/former franchisee validation supports the FDD economics.
  • For DIY, commit fixed costs only after paid demand and repeat intent are visible.
