Grease Trap Pumping — Acquisition Deep Dive

Full acquisition underwriting on grease trap pumping / FOG compliance businesses: route economics, deal comps, diligence traps, financing, target search, digital value-add, and 90-day execution roadmap.

Screen-high Acquisition UW Score: 4.05 Overall: 4.00 Confidence: Medium-High Date: May 24, 2026

Executive Verdict

Acquire / Screen-high — attractive category, but only if the target is a real recurring route book with disposal access, not a messy truck-and-owner job.

Recommended action: Build a 100-target off-market map in MA/RI/CT/NJ/NY/South Florida using municipal FOG/hauler lists, Google Maps, restaurant grease-trap keywords, and septic/wastewater businesses that list grease-trap service.

Why now: Grease trap pumping sits at the intersection of recurring restaurant compliance, wastewater regulation, route density, and ugly-work labor avoidance. Municipal FOG programs require regular cleaning, and SBA-derived data shows real change-of-ownership activity.

Why Dan: This is a compliance database + route-density + technician utilization + disposal logistics problem. The edge is diligence, systems, and acquisition discipline — not brand-building.

Biggest risk: The business can be materially nastier and more capital-intensive than lighter route services. Trucks, disposal fees, permits, odors, spills, emergency work, and driver retention can erase the recurring-revenue appeal.

1. Category Definition & Market

Grease trap pumping businesses remove fats, oils, grease, and food solids from commercial kitchen traps and interceptors. Customers include restaurants, QSRs, hotels, schools, hospitals, commissaries, grocery prepared-food departments, and property managers.

Core service
Scheduled grease trap/interceptor pumping, manifests, compliance records, emergency backups.
Add-ons
Used cooking oil, hydro-jetting, drain cleaning, septic pumping, interceptor repair/replacement.
Demand driver
FOG rules, sewer authority enforcement, backup avoidance, and recurring restaurant operations.

Geographic fit

Boston / New England
Dense restaurant stock, municipal FOG rules, local hauler lists, older buildings. Winter/disposal logistics matter.
NY / CT / NJ
High route density and enforcement, but traffic, labor, and permit complexity increase execution risk.
Miami / South Florida
Year-round service, restaurant growth, strong septic/wastewater market; hurricane/flood logistics require diligence.

6. Quality of Earnings & Diligence Framework

Must-have data
  • Route list: customer, trap size, frequency, price, last/next service
  • Manifests and disposal invoices
  • 3 years tax returns/P&L
  • Truck maintenance and DOT records
  • Permits, insurance, spill/fine history
  • AR aging and top-customer concentration
QoE traps
  • Emergency/one-time work sold as recurring
  • Unpaid spouse/admin labor
  • Deferred truck capex
  • Disposal agreement not transferable
  • Customer list includes closed restaurants
  • Cash leakage or weak invoice discipline

100-day post-close plan

  1. Ride every route and meet top 30 customers.
  2. Install KPI dashboard: stops/day, revenue/stop, disposal cost/gallon, drive time, AR days, emergency calls.
  3. Migrate route data into real software and automate reminders.
  4. Reprice lowest-margin accounts using trap size/access/frequency data.
  5. Add adjacent restaurants only after core service continuity is stable.

8. Competitive Landscape & Whitespace

Competitors include local septic pumpers, dedicated grease specialists, plumbing/drain companies, used cooking oil collectors, hood-cleaning providers, and regional liquid-waste platforms. White space exists in compliance reminders, digital manifests, multi-location restaurant reporting, missed-call capture, and route-density tuck-ins.

SEO wedge
Local “grease trap cleaning [city]” pages still look under-optimized in many markets.
Ops wedge
Price by trap size, hose distance, access, frequency, solids level, and disposal cost.
Sales wedge
Property managers and restaurant groups want one reliable vendor and clean paperwork.

9. Operator Reality

Operators and adjacent septic/pumping sources consistently point to the same hard truths: disposal access is make-or-break, grease waste can cost more to dispose than septic waste, pump trucks are expensive, route density matters more than gross ticket, and dirty-work driver retention is the bottleneck.

Where operators win
  • Dense scheduled routes
  • Strong disposal relationships
  • Digital manifests and reminders
  • Emergency premium pricing
  • Hydro-jet/drain/septic add-ons
Where owners lose money
  • Buying trucks before route density
  • Underpricing long-drive stops
  • Ignoring disposal fees
  • Letting restaurant AR age
  • Deferred truck maintenance

11. Financing & Capital Structure

SBA is plausible but the deal must support DSCR after replacing owner labor. Do not count seller labor as free cash flow unless Dan intends to operate directly. Underwrite manager salary, admin replacement, maintenance capex reserve, truck replacement reserve, working capital, insurance, and disposal fees.

Example: A $900K purchase with $275K SDE should not be valued on seller-operated SDE alone. If manager/admin normalization removes $70K and annual debt service is ~$115K, the remaining cushion is thin. Better targets need $250K+ normalized EBITDA/SDE.

12. Risks & Kill Criteria

Kill the deal if:

  • No route-level database with customer, trap size, frequency, price, last service, and next due date.
  • Less than 60% of revenue is scheduled recurring service or contracted route work.
  • Seller is the only driver/operator and will not provide 6+ month transition.
  • Permits or disposal agreements are not transferable or cannot be replaced before close.
  • Truck replacement/repair capex exceeds 15% of purchase price and is not priced in.
  • Top 10 customers exceed 40% of revenue without contracts and relationship transfer.
  • Purchase price is above 3.0x SDE for a one-truck owner-operated route.
  • Environmental/spill/fine history is unresolved.
  • Normalized DSCR after manager/admin replacement is below 1.35x.
  • AR over 60 days is large and not reserved.

14. 30 / 60 / 90 Day Acquisition Roadmap

Days 1–30: Map & Verify

Build the target universe

  • Build 100-target database across MA/RI/CT/NJ/NY/SFL from municipal hauler/FOG lists.
  • Call 10 operators, 3 brokers, 2 disposal facilities, and 2 SBA lenders.
  • Model local disposal fees, stops/day, truck capex, and permit transfer rules.
Days 31–60: Source & Underwrite

Move from category to real targets

  • Prioritize 25 owner-operated targets with succession signals.
  • Send 10–15 off-market letters/emails.
  • Request CIMs/financials for active septic/grease/liquid-waste listings.
  • Underwrite 3–5 targets using normalized manager-run EBITDA.
Days 61–90: LOI or Kill

Do not force a deal

  • Submit LOI only if route data, manifests, disposal, permits, and seller transition are clean.
  • Structure 10%–20% seller note/retention holdback.
  • Conduct ride-alongs and top-customer calls before QoE spend.
  • Close only if normalized DSCR >1.35x. Otherwise kill and keep the sourcing list.

15. Final Recommendation

Proceed with acquisition screening, not immediate pursuit. Grease trap pumping deserves a serious off-market search because it has recurring compliance demand, real SBA acquisition activity, strategic roll-up interest, and clear AI/ops value-add. But it is not as clean as backflow testing: asset intensity, disposal logistics, environmental risk, and labor make diligence harder.

Ideal deal: $1M–$2M revenue, $250K–$500K SDE/EBITDA, 2–4 trucks, 500+ recurring commercial accounts, dense routes, transferable permits/disposal, retained non-owner team, and seller note tied to route retention.

Backup: If no clean target appears, screen adjacent septic/wastewater businesses where grease trap service is a line item, or examine lower-capex hood cleaning / restaurant compliance route businesses.
