Quick-Lube / Oil Change Center — Deep Dive Investment Memo

Verdict: CONDITIONAL

Attractive high-throughput consumer service, but only at the right rent and with disciplined upsell ethics. Best path is acquisition of a small multi-bay operator or franchise resale, not overpaying for a trophy corner.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A local automotive service operator that monetizes vehicle maintenance, repair, replacement, insurance-driven work, and/or mobile service. The category is not a software wedge; the value is buying local demand, technician capacity, dispatch/counter process, and reputation.
  • Who are the customers? Mostly B2C households, with the best operators adding B2B fleet, dealer, insurer, rideshare, property-manager, and small-business accounts.
  • Demand drivers: aging vehicles, miles driven, complex sensors/ADAS, longer ownership periods, insurance claims, mandated safety expectations, and the fact that most consumers cannot defer mission-critical vehicle work for long.
  • Geographic relevance: NE/NY/SFL all work. Boston/NE and NY/CT/NJ have dense car parc plus high labor rates; SFL adds sun/heat, fleet, rideshare, and storm-driven repair/replacement demand. Site-level traffic, rent, and technician supply decide the market more than state-level macro.
  • Market size: the U.S. auto aftermarket is a large national market tracked by the Auto Care Association Factbook; exact category cuts should be pulled from paid Factbook/IBISWorld reports before an LOI. Treat public market-size snippets as directional, not underwriting-grade.

    **Key assumptions:**

  • Entry multiples: 3-4.5x SDE at Dan's scale ($200-500K SDE), with 3.5-4.0x reserved for clean books, manager layer, and transferable demand.
  • SBA 7(a): 10-year term, ~11% rate, 10% down; model uses annual amortizing debt service, before taxes and growth capex.
  • Cash-on-cash = (SDE - debt service) / down payment. High percentages are leverage math, not a promise; verify working capital, capex, and manager replacement cost.
  • Operator partner: Dan can hire/partner with an operator, but the model must subtract a real GM/service-manager cost if seller labor is embedded in SDE.

    • 4. Path-Specific Buybox

    What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Revenue range: $900K-$4M for most single-site targets; collision can be larger if production manager is in place.
  • SDE range: $200K-$500K, with owner replacement explicitly modeled.
  • Years in business: 7+ years, 4.6+ Google rating, stable technician base, clean tax returns, and no hidden cash normalization.
  • Route density / customer count: for mobile categories, compact dispatch radius; for bay businesses, strong local radius, traffic access, and utilization.
  • Key assets to verify: technician roster, service writer/GM, equipment condition, lifts/alignment/calibration tools, environmental compliance, insurance/vendor relationships, CRM/POS history, reviews, call tracking, and fleet/insurer/customer concentration.
  • Red flags to avoid: owner is the only rainmaker or master tech, undocumented cash, old lifts or spray booths needing capex, landlord risk, low review count, paid-lead addiction, DRP/fleet concentration above 35%, and wage rates below market.

    • What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Top brands to diligence: Valvoline Instant Oil Change, Jiffy Lube, Take 5, Grease Monkey, Strickland Brothers, Express Oil Change, Oil Changers, and local quick-lube operators.
  • Typical franchise costs vary widely; use current FDDs for franchise fee, royalty, marketing fund, required equipment, territory terms, and Item 19.
  • FDD highlight: demand Item 19 by outlet age/cohort, local ad spend, closure/transfer history, required vendor rebates, and whether franchisees are actually manager-run.
  • Unit economics from franchise disclosure: do not accept gross sales alone; normalize for rent, labor, royalty, local marketing, working capital, and owner salary.

    • 5. Competitive Landscape

  • Top competitors / roll-ups active in this space: Valvoline Instant Oil Change, Jiffy Lube, Take 5, Grease Monkey, Strickland Brothers, Express Oil Change, Oil Changers, and local quick-lube operators.
  • PE activity: auto aftermarket services remain active consolidator territory; Caliber/Crash Champions in collision, Mavis/Monro/Tire Discounters in tires, Valvoline/Take 5 in quick-lube, Safelite/Belron in glass, and platform-backed regional MSOs in repair.
  • Fragmentation level: still meaningfully fragmented below national chains; many founder-owned shops lack succession plans, modern marketing, or KPI discipline.
  • Moat: local trust, technician retention, speed, warranties, fleet/insurer/vendor relationships, certifications, calibrated equipment, Google reputation, and disciplined service writing.

    • 6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Typical deal structure: asset purchase preferred; 10%-20% seller note where possible; working-capital peg; escrow/holdback for warranty, environmental, payroll tax, and undisclosed liability issues.
  • SBA eligibility and terms: generally SBA-financeable if books are clean, collateral/equipment is documented, lease assignment is available, and no regulatory/environmental red flags are unresolved.
  • Down payment required at Dan's scale: roughly $60K-$225K for a $600K-$2.25M purchase at 10% down, before closing costs and working capital.
  • Payback period at base case: leverage can show 1-3 year cash-on-cash payback, but unlevered economic payback is closer to 3.5-5.5 years.
  • Honest multiple arbitrage: assume ~3x in to ~4x out only if Dan professionalizes reporting, adds a manager layer, improves retention/marketing, and creates a buyer-ready asset.

    • 7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map 50 targets across Boston/NE, NY/CT/NJ, and SFL; pull Google reviews, bay count, estimated tech count, rent/site constraints, and chain density; request current FDDs for relevant brands.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Recruit/identify an operator partner or advisor; speak with 5 owners, 3 technicians/service writers, 2 SBA lenders, and 2 equipment vendors; build a model with owner replacement, capex, and working capital.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): Submit one indication of interest only if books support SDE, lease is assignable, technician retention is credible, and the operator partner is willing to underwrite the day-to-day plan.

    • 8. Kill Criteria

    What would make this a NO-GO?

  • Seller cannot provide tax returns, POS reports, payroll records, and customer/source mix that tie to stated SDE.
  • Owner replacement wipes out more than 50% of SDE.
  • Technician turnover, wage catch-up, or certification gaps imply a broken labor model.
  • Major equipment/environmental/capex issue is discovered after price is set.
  • Customer/channel concentration above 35% without a contractual reason it will transfer.
  • Purchase price exceeds 4.5x SDE for a single-location small business without manager-in-place and clean recurring demand.
