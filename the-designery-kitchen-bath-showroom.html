Verdict: GO

Kitchen and bath showrooms are an underappreciated small business category with high average tickets ($15-50K), strong referral dynamics, and genuine moats (trade relationships, designer expertise, physical showroom). The Designery-type model — curated showroom + design consultation + contractor coordination — has real recurring revenue from the renovation cycle and referrals. Best played as an acquisition of an established local showroom with 10+ years of builder/designer relationships. Dan's PE background in retail/consumer is directly relevant.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • **What is this business?** A kitchen and bath showroom displays and sells cabinetry, countertops, fixtures, tile, and appliances for residential renovation projects. The Designery model adds design consultation, project management, and contractor coordination — becoming a one-stop shop for kitchen/bath renovations rather than just a product showroom.
  • **Who are the customers?** Mix of B2C (homeowners doing renovations, 60-70%) and B2B (builders, contractors, interior designers, 30-40%). B2B referrals are the lifeblood — a single builder relationship can generate $500K+ in annual revenue.
  • **Demand drivers:** Aging housing stock (kitchens/baths need updating every 15-25 years), home equity extraction (renovations spike when home values rise), aging-in-place modifications, kitchen/bath as top ROI renovation categories. The renovation market is cyclical but not volatile — people remodel through most economic environments.
  • **Geographic relevance:** Affluent suburbs with older housing stock are ideal. NE (Boston Metrowest, North Shore, South Shore) and NY suburbs (Westchester, Fairfield County, Bergen County) have 1950s-1980s housing stock overdue for updates. SFL (Palm Beach, Boca, Fort Lauderdale) has strong renovation demand from aging condos and luxury upgrades.
  • **Market size:** US kitchen and bath market ~$170B (estimate, NKBA + Census Bureau remodeling data). Kitchen/bath is ~40% of the total home improvement market. Showroom/retail channel is ~$25-35B of that. Highly fragmented — thousands of local showrooms.

**Key insight:** Margin comes from the design service layer, not the product alone. Cabinetry and fixtures are commoditized — the markup is 25-40%. But design consultation + project management can add 15-25% margin on top. A pure product showroom is a race to the bottom; a design-build showroom has a real moat.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target:

  • Revenue range: $1.5M-$4M
  • SDE range: $200K-$600K
  • Years in business: 10+ (relationships compound)
  • Active builder/designer referral network: 10+ consistent referral sources
  • Showroom: well-maintained, 2,000-5,000 sq ft, in visible location
  • Key assets: designer/consultant staff, builder relationships, supplier agreements (Cabinets To Go, CliqStudios, regional distributors), client portfolio, real estate
  • Red flags: owner IS the lead designer (knowledge transfer problem), showroom in declining retail center, single builder generates >30% of revenue, heavy inventory (>60 days), deferred showroom maintenance

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate:

  • **Kitchen Solvers** — ~80 units, kitchen/bath remodeling franchise, $40K franchise fee, 6% royalty
  • **DreamMaker Bath & Kitchen** — ~80 units, franchise model, ~$50K fee, full-service remodel
  • **Re-Bath** — ~150 units, bath-only franchise, owned by HB Global (PE-backed)
  • **Bath Fitter** — ~200 units, bath liner/install model, corporate + franchise
  • **Bella Bath & Kitchen** — emerging franchise model
  • Takeaway: Franchise route is available but limits product flexibility. Independent showroom with established relationships is more attractive for Dan's profile.

5. Competitive Landscape

**Fragmentation level:** Very high. Thousands of independent kitchen/bath showrooms across the US. Regional chains exist but rarely dominate markets.

Key players:

  • **Home Depot / Lowe's** — big-box kitchen/bath departments. Compete on price, not service. Their customers are typically lower-budget DIY projects.
  • **Re-Bath / Bath Fitter** — national franchise networks, bath-focused. Compete on convenience and speed.
  • **CliqStudios / Cabinets To Go** — online + showroom hybrid, cabinetry-focused. Price-competitive.
  • **Regional chains:** Koichi Kitchen (NE), Poggenpohl showrooms, local high-end dealers
  • **Independent showrooms** — the vast majority. Best acquisition targets.

**PE activity:** Moderate. HB Global (Re-Bath parent) is PE-backed. Private equity has invested in larger remodeling platforms ($50M+ revenue) but rarely touches single-location showrooms. This is a whitespace opportunity — aggregating showrooms regionally could be compelling.

**Moat:** Trade relationships (builders trust specific showrooms), designer expertise (hard to replicate), physical showroom experience (can't replace online), and geographic convenience. The kitchen/bath purchase is high-involvement — customers want to see and touch materials.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • **Typical deal structure:** Asset sale (inventory, fixtures, client lists, trade name, goodwill). Seller consulting agreement common (6-12 months). Real estate separate or included.
  • **SBA eligibility:** Yes, retail showrooms are SBA 7(a) eligible. Inventory and fixtures provide collateral.
  • **Down payment at Dan's scale:** $50K-$100K on a $500K-$1M acquisition.
  • **Payback period at base case:** ~2.5-3 years.
  • **Multiple arbitrage:** Buy at 2-3x → professionalize operations → add product lines/services → sell to aggregator at 4-5x. Or expand to 2-3 locations and sell as a platform.

Capital discipline rules:

  • Never pay >3.5x SDE for a single-location showroom
  • Verify referral sources — talk to 3+ builders who send business
  • Walk away if owner is the sole designer and has no successor
  • Budget $30-50K for showroom refresh in first 6 months
  • Verify inventory is current/saleable (not dead stock)

8. Kill Criteria

  • Owner is the sole designer with no replacement candidate
  • Revenue is >30% dependent on a single builder/contractor relationship
  • Showroom is in a declining retail location with dropping foot traffic
  • Inventory is >90 days and heavily weighted to discontinued lines
  • Active project pipeline has declined >20% year-over-year
  • The business is really a contractor business disguised as a showroom (different risk profile)
  • Lease renewal is imminent and landlord wants 30%+ increase
  • Local market has 5+ competing showrooms all fighting on price
