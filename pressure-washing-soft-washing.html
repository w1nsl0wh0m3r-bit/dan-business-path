Verdict: GO

Simple, fragmented, local-service category with attractive cash-on-cash if bought cheaply and professionalized; biggest risk is buying a seasonal owner-operator job rather than a routeable exterior-cleaning platform.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? Pressure washing and soft washing businesses clean home exteriors, roofs, siding, decks, driveways, storefronts, fleet equipment, dumpsters and commercial flatwork using pressure, low-pressure chemical wash systems and water-recovery practices where required. The better version sells annual/semiannual maintenance to HOAs, property managers and affluent homeowners.
  • Who are the customers? B2C homeowners and seasonal/second-home owners; B2B property managers, HOAs, restaurants, retail centers, municipalities and fleet operators.
  • Demand drivers: Aging housing stock, curb-appeal spending, HOA/commercial maintenance standards, mold/algae in humid climates, roof-life extension and low-ticket home-service recurrence.
  • Geographic relevance: Excellent in South Florida due to humidity/algae and year-round work; good in NY/NJ/CT and Boston suburbs with seasonal peaks and affluent housing. NE winter cyclicality needs gutter/window/holiday-light add-ons or commercial work.
  • Market size: Market size estimates for pressure washing vary across research firms and are not consistently defined; use local bottoms-up TAM by homes/HOAs/commercial properties. Franchise systems such as Rolling Suds and Window Hero validate national expansion, but mom-and-pop fragmentation remains high.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target in this category?

  • Revenue range: $500K-$1.5M with at least two crews or clear crew-build path.
  • SDE range: $125K-$400K after GM/dispatcher adjustment.
  • 4+ years, 500+ customer records, 150+ repeat annual customers or 20+ commercial/HOA accounts.
  • Dense ZIP/HOA clusters; job photos, CRM history and route data available.
  • Verify equipment condition, chemical handling, insurance, reviews, recurring accounts, crew productivity, seasonality and lead sources.
  • Avoid owner-only labor, no CRM, uninsured roof work, overreliance on paid leads, winter revenue cliff, environmental wash-water violations.

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate?

  • Brands: Rolling Suds, Window Hero, Shack Shine, Sparkle Squad, Fish Window Cleaning adjacency, Squeegee Squad, local soft-wash specialists.
  • FDD focus: Item 19 revenue by outlet age, royalty/ad fees, required equipment, call-center/lead-gen quality and territory size.
  • Franchise can help brand/lead-gen, but royalty only works if local route density and crew utilization are strong.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Rolling Suds, Window Hero, Shack Shine, Squeegee Squad, Fish Window Cleaning, local roof-cleaning and exterior-cleaning independents. PE activity is emerging through home-services roll-ups but most targets are small owner-operators. Moat is reviews, recurring property-manager relationships, crew SOPs, before/after proof, safety and local SEO.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Asset purchase with seller note tied to repeat-customer handoff; equipment valuation should be conservative. SBA is straightforward for clean books; base $630K deal needs ~$63K down plus truck/equipment reserves. Buy 3-3.5x, add CRM/maintenance plans/commercial sales, exit around 4x if manager-run and recurring.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

  • Phase 1 (Days 1-30): Map 50 local operators in SFL and affluent NE suburbs; scrape reviews, service mix, local SEO and commercial accounts.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31-60): Interview franchisees/independents; model crews/day, average ticket, seasonality and route density; identify operator/GM candidate.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61-90): LOI on a two-crew target with clean CRM/repeat customers, or launch small via operator if acquisition multiples are inflated.

8. Kill Criteria

  • No repeat customers or CRM history.
  • Owner is one of one field laborer.
  • Revenue collapses outside spring/summer with no add-ons.
  • Unsafe roof work or chemical/environmental issues.
  • Paid-lead CAC erases margins.
