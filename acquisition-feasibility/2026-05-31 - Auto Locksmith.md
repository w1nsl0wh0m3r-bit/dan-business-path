# Auto Locksmith — Acquisition Feasibility Memo

## Category #18 from Definitive 100 List

---

## 1. Category Overview

**What it is:** Auto locksmith businesses provide vehicle lockout services, key cutting, key programming (transponder/smart keys), ignition repair/replacement, and lock rekeying for cars, trucks, and motorcycles. Operations range from solo mobile operators to multi-van fleet operations with commercial contracts.

**Revenue model:** Per-job pricing with three segments:
- **Automotive lockout/emergency:** $75-$200 per call. Dispatched through roadside assistance networks or direct calls. High margin, irregular volume.
- **Key cutting/programming:** $50-$400 per key. Transponder and smart key programming commands premium pricing. Dealership alternative positioning.
- **Commercial/auto fleet contracts:** Recurring service agreements with car dealerships, rental car companies, auto auctions, and fleet operators. Volume pricing but consistent revenue.

**Market size:** IBISWorld tracks the broader locksmith industry at $2-3B annually. Auto locksmith segment estimated at $500M-$1B. BLS tracks ~25,000 locksmiths nationwide. Median revenue per BizBuySell locksmith listings: $478K. Median SDE: $150K.

**Demand drivers:**
- Key complexity increasing: Modern vehicles require specialized programming equipment for transponder/smart keys. Dealership alternative at lower cost.
- Vehicle aging: Older vehicles need ignition and lock cylinder repair.
- Fleet turnover: Rental companies and dealerships need bulk key/lock services.
- Emergency demand: Lockouts are non-discretionary — people pay whatever it costs to get back in their car.

## 4. Regulatory and Compliance Moat

**Moderate moat — varies by state.**

- **State licensing:** 15 states require locksmith licensure with background checks (including CA, TX, NC, TN, IL). NY, CT, MA, FL are among the majority that do NOT require state locksmith licensure.
- **ALOA certifications:** Associated Locksmiths of America offers Certified Registered Locksmith (CRL), Certified Professional Locksmith (CPL), and Certified Master Locksmith (CML) credentials. Not legally required in most states but signal professional credibility.
- **Key programming equipment:** Modern vehicle key programming requires expensive proprietary equipment ($2K-$15K per manufacturer system) and ongoing software subscriptions. This creates a modest capital and knowledge barrier.
- **Automotive-specific:** Unlike residential locksmiths, auto locksmiths need vehicle-specific key code databases, transponder programming tools, and smart key/fob programming capability. The equipment and knowledge investment is meaningful but not insurmountable.
- **Compliance moat verdict: WEAK to MODERATE.** In unlicensed states (NY, CT, MA, FL), the barrier is just equipment and skill. The programming equipment cost is real but available to anyone willing to invest.

## 5. Competitive Dynamics

**Fragmentation:** Very high. ~25,000 locksmiths nationwide, mostly solo or small operations. No dominant national auto locksmith brand.

**PE roll-up activity:** Limited at the automotive locksmith level. Commercial access-control consolidation is active (Convergint, Pye-Barker, ADT Commercial), but these buyers target commercial security integrators, not mobile auto locksmiths. CT Acquisitions notes: "PE rollup activity in residential locksmith is limited."

**Franchise presence:** Minimal. Some franchise concepts exist (Pop-A-Lock, The Key Guy) but market penetration is low in the auto locksmith segment.

**Competitive pressure:**
- Dealerships: Offer key programming as a premium service but at 2-3x the price
- Hardware stores: Basic key cutting but can't handle transponder/smart keys
- Mobile apps/on-demand: Emerging but not yet significant competitors
- Roadside assistance networks: AAA, Allstate Motor Club dispatch to network providers — being in-network is a revenue source but not a moat

## 8. Risk Factors

- **Low multiples:** 1.5-2.5x SDE reflects shallow buyer pool and limited exit options. Dan would be buying into a category with poor exit liquidity.
- **Technology disruption:** As vehicles move to phone-as-key and biometric entry, traditional auto locksmith services decline. Tesla and other EVs use app-based access that bypasses physical keys entirely.
- **Equipment obsolescence:** Key programming equipment needs constant updates as manufacturers change protocols. $2K-$15K per manufacturer system = ongoing capital drag.
- **Labor dependency:** Solo tech operations are owner-dependent. Multi-van operations need trained techs who can operate independently — and who could leave to compete.
- **Emergency hours:** Auto lockout work often requires 24/7 availability. Without a dispatch system and multiple techs, the owner is on call constantly.
- **Insurance/liability:** Working on customer vehicles = damage risk. Garage keepers liability needed.
- **Low recurring revenue:** Unlike inspection stations (annual mandate), auto locksmith work is transactional. No natural return cycle.

---

## 9. Verdict: SKIP (Confidence: MEDIUM)

**Why:** Auto locksmith is a legitimate business but doesn't match Dan's buy box. The recurring revenue is weak, the regulatory moat is thin in target states (NY, CT, MA, FL don't require locksmith licensure), and the multiples are compressed by a shallow buyer pool. The technology trend (phone-as-key, biometric entry) is a structural headwind that reduces long-term demand for physical key services.

**The one angle worth watching:** If Dan finds a commercial locksmith operation with 40%+ revenue from recurring fleet/dealership contracts AND strong key programming capability, the recurring revenue profile improves. But these are rare at Dan's price point and still face technology disruption risk.

**Kill criteria:**
- Low multiples (1.5-2.5x SDE) = poor exit optionality
- Technology disruption (phone-as-key trend)
- No regulatory moat in target states
- Low recurring revenue without fleet contracts
- Owner-dependency at solo operator level
