## 1. Category Overview

Carpet cleaning businesses provide deep cleaning of residential and commercial carpets, area rugs, upholstery, and other soft surfaces using hot water extraction (truckmount or portable), bonnet cleaning, dry cleaning, or low-moisture encapsulation methods. The business ranges from solo owner-operators in a van to multi-truck franchise operations with 10+ technicians.

**Revenue Model:**
- Residential carpet cleaning: **$120–$400 per room** ($0.25–$0.50 per sq ft)
- Whole-house special (3–5 rooms): **$300–$800**
- Upholstery cleaning: **$75–$250 per piece** (sofa, loveseat, sectional, dining chairs)
- Area rug cleaning (in-plant): **$2–$6 per sq ft** (pick-up and delivery premium)
- Commercial carpet cleaning: **$0.15–$0.40 per sq ft** ($200–$5,000+ per job)
- **Recurring maintenance contracts**: Commercial accounts (offices, hotels, restaurants) on quarterly to annual cycles
- Protectant application (Scotchgard, Teflon): **$25–$75 per room** (high-margin add-on)
- Deodorization/sanitization: **$30–$100 per room** (pet, smoke, mold)

**Market Size:**
- US carpet and rug cleaning market: **$5.5B+** (2025)
- Part of the broader $112B commercial/residential cleaning market
- ~35,000–50,000 carpet cleaning businesses in the US (highly fragmented)
- Growth drivers: housing turnover (move-in/move-out cleaning), pet ownership (stain/odor removal), commercial facility maintenance cycles, post-COVID sanitation awareness
- Residential carpet has been losing share to hard-surface flooring (LVP, tile) — a long-term headwind

**The recurring angle — commercial side:** Like tile/grout cleaning, the value for Dan-fit is in commercial contracts. Offices, hotels, restaurants, and retail spaces need carpet cleaning on scheduled cycles (quarterly to annually). Residential is mostly one-time/event-driven.

## 4. Regulatory & Compliance Moat

**Low moat — minimal regulatory barriers:**

- **No contractor license required** — Carpet cleaning is unregulated in most states. No specialized trade license needed. This is a LOW barrier to entry.
- **No mandated inspection or compliance cycle** — Nobody is required to clean their carpets.
- **IICRC certification** — Institute of Inspection Cleaning and Restoration Certification (S100 Carpet Cleaning standard). Not legally required but commercial accounts and quality-conscious consumers look for it.
- **Chemical safety / OSHA** — Cleaning solutions (some acidic, some alkaline, some solvent-based) require SDS compliance and PPE. Training burden, not a moat.
- **EPA wastewater** — Truckmount extraction generates wastewater that must be disposed of properly (not into storm drains). Local regulations vary.
- **Bonding and insurance** — Commercial accounts require bonds and liability insurance. Creates minor barrier for smaller operators entering commercial market.

**The compliance gap:** Carpet cleaning has no regulatory tailwind. The recurring demand comes from commercial relationships and consumer habits — not from any code or mandate. This is the same fundamental limitation as tile/grout cleaning and surface refinishing.

## 5. Competitive Dynamics

**Fragmentation:** Extremely high. ~35,000–50,000 carpet cleaning businesses nationally, most are one-truck owner-operators. Very low barriers to entry ($25K–$65K startup).

**Franchise Presence — Strong:**
- **Stanley Steemer** — the dominant national brand, franchise + corporate locations. Offers carpet, upholstery, tile/grout, air duct, and water damage services.
- **Chem-Dry** — franchise system, part of the ServiceMaster/HomeFair ecosystem (Roark Capital)
- **Zerorez** — premium franchise with proprietary cleaning technology, expanding rapidly
- **Heirloom Rug Cleaning** — niche area rug specialist franchise
- **Oxi Fresh** — eco-friendly franchise carpet cleaning

**PE Roll-Up Activity — Active but mostly through broader platforms:**
- ServiceMaster Brands (Roark Capital, $5.5B platform) includes Chem-Dry and ServiceMaster Clean — carpet cleaning is a core service line
- ABM, ISS, and commercial janitorial platforms acquire carpet/floor care businesses as bolt-ons to their janitorial contracts
- CT Acquisitions notes: "How to sell a carpet cleaning or floor care company" is an active advisory vertical — carpet cleaning businesses are frequently acquired by commercial cleaning platforms as service-line add-ons

**The consolidation thesis:** Carpet cleaning alone is too small and fragmented for standalone PE platform interest. But as part of a broader floor care or commercial janitorial platform, carpet cleaning is a valuable bolt-on because:
1. Same customer base as commercial janitorial
2. Same equipment and crew skills as tile/grout cleaning
3. Natural cross-sell to existing commercial accounts
4. Franchise brand recognition (Stanley Steemer) drives consumer demand

## 8. Risk Factors

- **Low barriers to entry** — $25K–$65K startup means constant new competitors. Residential markets see heavy price competition.
- **Long-term demand headwind** — Hard-surface flooring (LVP, tile, laminate) has been gaining share vs carpet in both new construction and renovations. Fewer carpeted square feet = fewer cleaning jobs over time. This is a structural multi-decade trend.
- **No recurring compliance mandate** — The fundamental model risk for residential-heavy operators.
- **Labor-intensive** — Physical work; technician turnover is a constant challenge.
- **Equipment dependency** — Truckmount extractors ($15K–$40K) require maintenance and occasional replacement. Downtime = lost revenue.
- **Seasonality** — Residential demand peaks in spring/fall (moving season, pre-holiday cleaning). Dips in winter (NE) and mid-summer.
- **Weather dependency** — Snow/ice days in NE can cancel 20–30% of winter appointments.
- **Quality liability** — Over-wetting, shrinkage, color bleeding, or damage to delicate fibers creates warranty claims. Area rug cleaning has especially high liability (expensive rugs, natural dyes).
- **Franchise competition** — Stanley Steemer and Chem-Dry dominate consumer search and brand recognition. Independent operators without brand recognition struggle to compete for residential jobs.
- **Key-person risk** — Solo owner-operators who are the primary technician have high key-person risk. Buyers discount heavily.
