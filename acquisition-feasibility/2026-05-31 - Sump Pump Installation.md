## 1. Category Overview

Sump pump installation and basement waterproofing businesses solve one of homeowners' most visceral fears: water in the basement. The core service is installing interior French drain systems, sump pump basins, and pumps to manage groundwater infiltration. Most operators also offer crawl space encapsulation, foundation crack repair, dehumidifier installation, and exterior waterproofing.

**Revenue Model:**
- **Full basement waterproofing system install:** $5,000–$20,000+ per job (interior perimeter drain + sump basin + pump + backup)
- **Sump pump replacement (standalone):** $500–$1,500
- **Crawl space encapsulation:** $3,000–$10,000
- **Foundation crack repair:** $800–$3,000
- **Annual maintenance contracts:** $150–$300/year (pump inspection, battery backup check, system flush)
- **Emergency water event response:** $1,500–$5,000

**Market Size:**
- North America sump pump market: **$181M in 2025 → projected $261M by 2032** (5.34% CAGR) — Fortune Business Insights
- Broader waterproofing services market: ~$2–3B in the US
- North America pump services market: **$6.4B in 2025 → $12.8B by 2035** (GMInsights)
- Basement waterproofing demand concentrated in: Northeast (old housing stock, basements), Midwest, and Mid-Atlantic
- Florida relevance is low (slab construction, no basements) — this is primarily a NE/NY play

**Labor Model:**
- 2–3 person installation crews (lead installer + laborers)
- No specialized license required in most states (general contractor license suffices)
- Seasonal labor pressure — install demand peaks in spring/summer and after major rain events
- Owner can run as sales/estimator while crews execute installs

**Recurring Revenue:**
- Annual maintenance contracts are the recurring hook: pump inspection, battery backup testing, drain cleaning
- CT Acquisitions notes that sump pump/water heater maintenance contracts in plumbing businesses typically run **10–20% recurring**
- Lifetime transferable warranties create a built-in customer relationship — new homeowners inherit the warranty and become service customers
- This is **weaker** recurring revenue than salt delivery or septic pumping

## 4. Regulatory & Compliance Moat

- **Low barrier** — no specialized professional license required beyond general contractor registration
- **Plumbing license** may be required in some states for pump discharge pipe connection
- **Mold remediation licensing** if offering mold services (varies by state — FL and TX require it, most NE states don't)
- **OSHA** confined space entry for crawl space work
- **Building permits** required for waterproofing system installation in most municipalities
- **Lead paint (RRP)** certification required if working in pre-1978 homes
- The moat is primarily **brand reputation and warranty system**, not regulation. This is weaker than septic or water softener.

## 8. Risk Factors

- **Seasonality** — heavy spring/summer demand; winter installation drops in NE (frozen ground limits excavation). Revenue is weather-dependent.
- **Weather event dependence** — revenue spikes after major rain/flood events; calm years compress revenue. This is not predictable recurring revenue.
- **Low barrier to entry** — no specialized license means competitors can enter easily. Differentiation is brand/warranty, which takes time to build.
- **Insurance/liability** — if a waterproofing system fails and floods a finished basement, liability exposure is significant. Warranty claims can be expensive.
- **Labor model** — installation is physically demanding (jackhammers, concrete, crawl spaces). High turnover among laborers.
- **Florida mismatch** — Florida's slab construction means almost no basement waterproofing market. This is a NE/NY-only play.
- **Recession sensitivity** — waterproofing is deferrable. Homeowners put it off in downturns. Unlike septic pumping (you MUST pump), you can live with a damp basement.

---

## 9. Verdict: WATCH (Medium Confidence)

**Why not PURSUE:** The project-based revenue model and thin recurring revenue don't match Dan's buy box. The business is fundamentally a contractor — sales-driven, weather-dependent, and cyclical. Florida is a dead market for this category. No meaningful regulatory moat.

**Why not SKIP:** The NE/NY housing stock is old (lots of 50–100+ year old basements with water issues). Climate change is increasing extreme precipitation events in the Northeast, driving waterproofing demand. An established operator with a large warranty customer base and service contract program could generate meaningful recurring revenue — but you'd have to find one that already has it, not build it.

**The right angle:** This category works better as a **bolt-on to a plumbing or waterproofing platform** than as a standalone acquisition. If Dan acquired a plumbing business, adding sump pump maintenance contracts to the route would be high-margin incremental revenue.

**Kill criteria:** Skip if recurring revenue <15% of total, or if purchase price >3x SDE, or if the business has >30% revenue concentration in foundation repair (high liability, cyclical).
