## 1. Category Overview

Concrete lifting (also called mudjacking, slab jacking, polyjacking, or concrete leveling) is a specialty repair trade that raises sunken or uneven concrete slabs by injecting material underneath. **Traditional mudjacking** pumps a cement/sand/water slurry through drilled holes. **Modern polyjacking** injects expanding polyurethane foam that lifts the slab with less weight, faster cure time, and smaller drill holes.

The result: a **$500–$3,000 repair job** that saves a homeowner or property manager from a $5,000–$15,000 concrete replacement.

**Revenue Model:**
- Residential driveway/sidewalk/patio lifting: **$500–$2,500 per job** (1–2 hours on site)
- Garage floor/pool deck lifting: **$1,000–$4,000**
- Commercial slab lifting (warehouse floors, parking lots): **$3,000–$25,000+**
- Municipal work (sidewalks, school grounds): **$5,000–$50,000 contracts**
- Soil stabilization/void filling: **$2,000–$20,000**
- Joint sealing and concrete sealing (bolt-on services): **$300–$1,500**

**The unit economics are exceptional.** A single residential job bills $1,200–$1,500 average with $200–$300 in material costs. A single crew can do **3–5 jobs per day** at $4,800/day gross revenue. With ~$480/day in operating costs, net revenue per day is **~$3,400** ([HMI Company profitability analysis](https://hmicompany.com/cost-to-start-concrete-lifting-business/)).

**Market Size:**
- Niche specialty trade — smaller than paving or general concrete
- Aging US infrastructure: ASCE gave US roads a near-failing grade; $3.7T infrastructure gap
- Aging housing stock (median 42 years) — settled concrete is ubiquitous
- Growing awareness: property managers and homeowners increasingly choose lifting over replacement
- Municipal demand: ADA trip hazard compliance on public sidewalks creates government contract work

**The market opportunity is real but fragmented.** No dominant national brand. Mostly small operators with 1–3 crews. Some franchise presence (PolyLevel, A-1 Concrete Leveling) but most operators are independent.

**These are some of the best unit economics in the trades.** The combination of high gross margins (65–80%), low material costs ($200–$300/job), fast job completion (1–2 hours), and low equipment investment ($50K–$80K/rig) creates exceptional cash flow.

**Recurring Revenue Profile:**

Concrete lifting sits in an interesting middle ground:
- **Not annually recurring** — once a slab is lifted, it shouldn't need re-lifting for 10–20 years (if done correctly with proper void filling and soil stabilization)
- **But relationship-driven repeat** — property managers, HOAs, municipalities, and commercial property owners with multiple sites generate repeat business across different locations
- **Bolt-on recurring services**: joint sealing (every 2–3 years), concrete sealing (every 2–5 years), void monitoring for known problem areas
- **Municipal contracts**: some cities have ongoing sidewalk leveling programs funded by ADA compliance budgets — multi-year contracts
- **Trip hazard remediation**: ADA requires property owners to address trip hazards (height differentials >¼"). This creates demand for leveling on commercial properties, especially when cited by inspectors or after insurance claims.

## 4. Regulatory & Compliance Moat

**Moderate-to-strong moat — the ADA trip hazard angle is genuine:**

- **ADA compliance**: Trip hazards (uneven concrete, height differentials >¼ inch) violate ADA accessibility requirements on commercial and public properties. Property owners face lawsuits, fines, and insurance liability if they don't address them. **This creates a quasi-compliance-driven demand** — not an annual mandate, but a "fix it when cited" trigger.
- **Contractor licensing** — varies by state. Some require general contractor license; others have specific concrete specialty licensing. FL, CA, NV have stricter requirements. CT, MA, NY are home improvement contractor license jurisdictions.
- **Environmental permits** — polyurethane injection involves chemical handling. Pollution/environmental insurance riders required. OSHA chemical safety standards apply. Some jurisdictions require specific permits for injecting material into the ground.
- **Municipal contracts** — government work requires bonding capacity, prevailing wage (Davis-Bacon) compliance, and competitive bidding. Barrier to entry for the municipal segment.
- **Specialized equipment knowledge** — while not a formal license, the skill of properly diagnosing settlement causes, selecting injection points, controlling lift, and avoiding over-lifting is learned through training (HMI, PolyLevel, etc.). Operators without training cause damage and earn bad reputations.
- **No hard annual inspection mandate** — unlike backflow or fire suppression, no code requires annual concrete inspection. BUT: ADA compliance and insurance/liability frameworks create de facto recurring demand on commercial properties.

**The ADA trip hazard compliance angle** is the closest thing to a regulatory tailwind. When a commercial property gets cited (or an insurance inspector flags it), the owner must fix trip hazards. Concrete lifting is the cheapest fix (vs. replacement). This creates inbound demand driven by compliance events.

## 5. Competitive Dynamics

**Fragmentation:** Very high. Mostly 1–3 rig independent operators. No dominant national brand. The trade is still in early awareness phase — many property owners don't know lifting is an option until they search.

**PE Roll-Up Activity:** Minimal in pure-play concrete lifting. But adjacent platforms are active:
- Infrastructure services platforms (soil stabilization, foundation repair) include lifting
- Foundation repair companies (Olshan, Ram Jack, Basement Systems) offer polyjacking as a service line
- Concrete/paving platforms add lifting as a complementary service
- No dedicated PE platform has formed specifically around concrete lifting — **this is both a risk (no clear exit path) and an opportunity (first-mover consolidation potential)**

**Franchise Presence:**
- **PolyLevel** (Supportworks network) — franchise/dealer model; premium positioning; strong training and brand
- **A-1 Concrete Leveling** — franchise system; long-established (since 1982); multi-location
- **HMI Company** — equipment manufacturer that also provides training, leads, and support (not franchise, but ecosystem)
- Franchise models provide brand, training, lead generation, and supplier discounts

**The consolidation thesis:** Concrete lifting is at an earlier consolidation stage than sealcoating or fencing. No PE platform has formed specifically around it. This means:
1. No clear bolt-on exit to a platform acquirer
2. BUT: opportunity to build a multi-location operator and sell to an adjacent platform (foundation repair, infrastructure services, property maintenance)
3. The low barrier to entry ($50K–$80K startup) means organic competition is high — but acquiring existing accounts, reputation, and customer relationships avoids the startup phase

## 8. Risk Factors

- **Not annually recurring** — once lifted, a slab stays level for 10–20 years. Revenue depends on finding new jobs, not returning to existing customers (unless multi-site property managers).
- **Startup competition** — $50K–$80K startup cost is low enough that new entrants proliferate. Differentiation requires reputation, Google reviews, and commercial relationships.
- **Seasonality (NE)** — concrete lifting requires temperatures above 40°F. Frozen ground and cold weather limit the NE season to April–October (~7 months). FL is year-round.
- **Weather dependency** — can't inject foam in heavy rain (material washout) or when ground is frozen.
- **Skill risk** — improper lifting can crack slabs, damage underground utilities, or over-lift. One bad job can generate a lawsuit. Insurance is essential.
- **Chemical handling** — polyurethane isocyanate and resin components require safe handling, proper PPE, and environmental compliance. OSHA exposure.
- **Material cost volatility** — polyurethane chemical costs track petroleum prices (~$2.30/lb). Must manage pricing.
- **Municipal contract complexity** — government work requires bonding, prevailing wage compliance, and competitive bidding. Higher barrier but also higher moat.
- **Exit path uncertainty** — no dedicated PE platform for concrete lifting yet. Exit would likely be to an adjacent platform (foundation repair, infrastructure, property maintenance) rather than a category-specific acquirer.
