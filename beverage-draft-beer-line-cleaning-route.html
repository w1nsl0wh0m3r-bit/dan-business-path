Beverage / Draft Beer Line Cleaning Route

Compliance-like recurring route with strong density in bar-heavy metros, but small-ticket economics require commercial account density and a non-owner route supervisor.

CONDITIONALDan Business PathConfidence: Medium

Verdict: CONDITIONAL

Compliance-like recurring route with strong density in bar-heavy metros, but small-ticket economics require commercial account density and a non-owner route supervisor. Best as acquisition of a beer-line cleaning, draft-system install, and preventive maintenance route — not a startup.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A route-based service company that cleans and maintains draft beer lines, faucets, couplers, and cooling systems for bars, restaurants, breweries, taprooms, and sports venues on a biweekly or weekly cadence. Many operators also sell draft-system installation, repair, and equipment — which is where margin improves.
  • Who are the customers? Bars, restaurants, breweries, hotel F&B, sports/entertainment venues, and taprooms. Virtually 100% B2B. Key buyers are bar managers, beverage directors, and brewery distribution teams who need clean lines for product quality and health-code compliance.
  • Demand drivers: Brewers Association guidelines recommend cleaning every two weeks; health departments increasingly inspect draft systems; craft beer growth (8,000+ US craft breweries) creates more tap systems; distributor incentive programs sometimes subsidize or require line cleaning. Recurring by nature — every draft account needs it continuously.
  • Geographic relevance: NY/CT/NJ and Boston/NE are exceptionally strong — high bar/restaurant density, craft beer culture, and strict health departments. SFL hospitality corridor (Miami, Tampa, Orlando) is also strong but seasonal tourist bars can churn.
  • Market size: No clean public TAM for line-cleaning specifically; it sits within the broader $4B+ draft beer dispense equipment/service market (Micro Matic estimates). A single metro can support $500K–$2M in cleaning/service revenue.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

Good Acquisition Target

  • Revenue: $500K–$1.5M, SDE: $150K–$400K
  • 200–600+ recurring accounts on biweekly or weekly cadence
  • Trained technicians (not just the owner) with chemical-handling certifications
  • Service agreements/contracts (not verbal-only relationships)
  • Draft-system install/repair capability (higher-margin upsell)
  • Clean vehicle fleet and chemical inventory
  • Brewer/distributor referral relationships

Red Flags

  • Owner is sole technician, accounts are verbal-only
  • Poor chemical safety records
  • Customer concentration in one restaurant group
  • No install/repair revenue

This category is overwhelmingly independent — few franchise systems exist. Comparable service-route franchises for diligence: Micro Matic-trained service providers (training/brand ecosystem, not franchise). If evaluating any franchise, focus on: territory exclusivity, training quality, equipment requirements, royalty vs. independent margin.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Competitors: Local independent draft-line cleaning services, brewery in-house cleaning teams, beverage distributors who offer line cleaning as a value-add, restaurant equipment service companies, and Micro Matic-trained technicians.
  • PE/consolidation: Minimal at the small-route level. Distributors (e.g., Reyes Holdings, Southern Glazer's) sometimes acquire cleaning operations as vertical integration.
  • Fragmentation: Extremely fragmented — mostly 1-5 person operators in each metro. Few regional platforms.
  • Moat: Account relationships and trust (bars don't switch cleaners casually), route density and scheduling discipline, technical skill in draft-system repair, distributor/brewery referral networks, and health-code compliance reputation.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Deal structure: Asset purchase — customer lists, service agreements, vehicles, tools, chemical inventory. Seller note tied to account retention (e.g., 20% holdback released after 12 months if 90%+ of accounts remain).
  • SBA eligibility: Strong candidate — recurring B2B revenue, tangible assets (vehicles/equipment), service contracts transferable.
  • Down payment at base case ($735K): ~$73.5K at 10% down.
  • Payback period: ~1.0–1.3 years at base case if account retention holds above 85%.
  • Multiple arbitrage: Buy a sleepy 3.0–3.5x SDE independent, professionalize dispatch/scheduling, add install/repair upsell, build to 4.0–4.5x on exit. Cash-flow play.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

Phase 1 · Days 1–30

Market Mapping

Map all draft-line cleaning operators in Boston, NY, and SFL target markets. Contact 10–15 brokers specializing in service-route businesses. Request any available FDD or franchise materials. Identify potential operator-partner candidates with draft-system or beverage-service backgrounds.

Phase 2 · Days 31–60

Diligence & Modeling

Build three-case acquisition model. Diligence 3–5 targets: verify tax returns, account lists, service agreements, retention rates, technician qualifications, and vehicle/equipment condition. Speak with 2–3 SBA lenders about route-business financing. Interview operator-partner candidates.

Phase 3 · Days 61–90

LOI or Kill

Submit LOI on top target with seller note/retention mechanics. Include diligence gates: account verification calls, chemical-safety inspection, vehicle appraisal, and transition-period terms. If no quality target emerges, kill or park the category.

8. Kill Criteria

  • Accounts are handshake-only with no service agreements — retention unprovable
  • Owner is sole technician and relationship holder; no transferable goodwill
  • Bar/restaurant churn in target market is extreme (e.g., >30% annual account loss)
  • Chemical-handling compliance is poor (OSHA/health-code liability)
  • Technicians cannot be hired or retained at wages the business can support
  • Install/repair revenue is minimal — pure cleaning is too low-margin for a paid manager layer
  • SDE disappears after replacing owner labor with route manager salary
