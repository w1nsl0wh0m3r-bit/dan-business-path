Roll-off Dumpster Rental

Attractive asset-backed local logistics business if Dan buys density, permits, disposal relationships, and contractor repeat accounts. Avoid commodity one-truck lead-gen businesses.

CONDITIONALDan Business PathConfidence: Medium

Verdict: CONDITIONAL

Attractive asset-backed local logistics business if Dan buys density, permits, disposal relationships, and contractor repeat accounts. Avoid commodity one-truck lead-gen businesses with landfill cost exposure and no dispatch discipline. Best as a multi-truck acquisition with documented contractor cohorts.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A local haulage company that delivers open-top roll-off containers (10–40 yard) to construction sites, renovation projects, roofing jobs, estate cleanouts, and commercial properties, then transports filled containers to landfills or transfer stations. Revenue from container rental fees, haul charges, tonnage/disposal pass-throughs, and sometimes recycling/sorting premiums.
  • Who are the customers? Contractors (roofing, remodeling, general construction), property managers, real estate investors, homeowners doing renovations, commercial tenants, and government/municipal projects. B2B contractor accounts are the underwriteable core — they recur with every job.
  • Demand drivers: Construction activity (residential remodel, roofing replacement cycles, new builds), disaster/storm debris, estate cleanouts, commercial tenant turnover. Container utilization tracks housing starts and renovation spending.
  • Geographic relevance: All three target markets work — Boston/NE has strong remodeling/roofing demand; NY/CT/NJ has massive construction volume and strict disposal regulations; SFL has hurricane debris, condo renovation, and construction growth.
  • Market size: US waste management services ~$120B (IBISWorld); roll-off dumpster rental is a small subset. A single truck/5-container startup can generate $150K–$300K revenue; established small fleets (3–8 trucks, 50–250 containers) generate $750K–$3M.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

Good Acquisition Target

  • Revenue: $1M–$3M, SDE: $250K–$700K
  • 3–8 roll-off trucks, 50–250+ containers (various sizes)
  • Documented contractor accounts (not just lead-gen calls)
  • Transfer-station or landfill contracts with known tipping fees
  • Dispatch/routing software in use (not paper-based)
  • Clean vehicle titles and maintenance records
  • Required permits and local licenses in good standing
  • Yard/depot lease with room for container storage

Red Flags

  • Aging truck fleet (replacement cliff)
  • Verbal-only disposal arrangements
  • Top 5 customers >40% of revenue
  • Yard lease expiring soon
  • Environmental violations

Franchise candidates: redbox+ Dumpsters (waste+portable toilet combo), Bin There Dump That (residential-focused), DumpStor. FDD diligence: Item 7 total investment, territory size and exclusivity, royalty + ad fund drag on margin, container/truck requirements, Item 19 mature-unit economics if available.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • Major players: Waste Management (WM), Republic Services, Waste Connections, GFL Environmental — but these focus on large commercial/municipal contracts and often under-serve small contractor roll-off demand.
  • Regional/local: Hundreds of small independent roll-off operators in every metro; many are 1–3 truck owner-operators.
  • Franchises: redbox+, Bin There Dump That, DumpStor, HaulAway — growing but still small share.
  • Junk removal crossover: 1-800-GOT-JUNK, College HUNKS, Junk King offer dumpster-adjacent services but are priced as premium consumer services.
  • PE activity: Consolidation at the mid-market level (regional waste platforms), but small roll-off fleets remain fragmented and under-professionalized.
  • Moat: Disposal cost advantage (transfer-station proximity and volume pricing), container availability and fast delivery, route density lowering fuel/time per pull, and contractor relationship stickiness.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Deal structure: Asset purchase preferred — trucks, containers, customer list, permits, phone number/website, yard lease assignment. Exclude environmental liabilities with strong reps/warranties. Seller note (15–25%) tied to account retention.
  • SBA eligibility: Strong — heavy equipment (trucks/containers) serves as collateral. Lender will inspect truck age, condition, and title status.
  • Down payment at base case ($1.5M): ~$150K at 10% down.
  • Payback period: ~1.0–1.3 years at base case if contractor retention holds.
  • Multiple arbitrage: Buy at 3.5x from a retiring owner-operator, professionalize dispatch and marketing, add container inventory, sell at 4.0–4.5x to a regional platform. Modest but achievable.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

Phase 1 · Days 1–30

Market Mapping

Map all roll-off operators in target metros (Boston, NY tri-state, SFL). Scrape BizBuySell, LoopNet, and broker listings. Identify disposal costs, transfer-station locations, and permit requirements. Contact 5–10 brokers specializing in waste/logistics businesses.

Phase 2 · Days 31–60

Diligence & Modeling

Build three-case acquisition model with local tipping-fee assumptions. Diligence 3–5 targets: verify tax returns, container inventory and condition, truck maintenance logs, contractor account list, and disposal contracts. Speak with 2–3 SBA lenders. Interview dispatcher/ops-manager candidates.

Phase 3 · Days 61–90

LOI or Kill

Submit LOI on top target with seller note/retention earnout. Include diligence gates: vehicle appraisal, environmental review of yard, permit transfer verification, and customer-reference calls with top 10 contractor accounts.

8. Kill Criteria

  • Truck fleet is at end of useful life — replacement capex would consume 2+ years of cash flow
  • Disposal/tipping fees cannot be passed through to customers
  • Top 5 customers exceed 40% of revenue with no contracts
  • Yard/depot lease is lost or untransferable
  • Environmental violations or pending enforcement on yard or operations
  • Revenue is primarily lead-gen (HomeAdvisor/Angi) with no contractor relationships
  • Local market is oversaturated with aggressive pricing (race to bottom on per-pull rates)
  • Base-case DSCR fails after adding dispatcher and ops-manager salary
