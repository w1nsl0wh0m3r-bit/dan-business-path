## First Call Underwriting: What It Must Prove

The first call cannot be a generic "tell me about your company" conversation. It has to prove whether there is a transferable compliance-route asset.

Minimum proof points:

- **Active devices/customer sites:** number of active devices, customer sites, and unique accounts; split by county/city, device type, domestic/fire/irrigation/commercial/HOA.
- **Renewal cadence:** annual, semiannual, quarterly; due-date distribution; seasonal concentration.
- **Database ownership:** whether records sit in software, Excel, QuickBooks, BSI/municipal portals, paper files, or technician memory.
- **Renewal evidence:** prior-year test reports, notices, customer list, device make/model/serial, pass/fail history, repair/retest history.
- **Revenue split:** testing vs repair/replacement/install vs unrelated plumbing/fire work.
- **Route productivity:** tests per tech per day, drive time, first-time pass rate, retest rate, average price/test, average repair ticket.
- **Certified tester retention:** number of certified testers, tenure, pay model, subcontractor dependence, renewal/recertification exposure.
- **Reporting portal workflow:** BSI, Miami-Dade WASD, PBCWUD, BWSC, CT eLicense/DPH/public water systems, NJDEP, NYC DEP; who files and how errors are handled.
- **Owner role:** sales, scheduling, filing, testing, repair, customer relationships, license holder.
- **Seller expectation:** whether the owner has ever considered succession, partnership, minority recap, asset sale, or route-book carveout; do not lead with price.

## Recommended First-Call Script Outline

Use only if Dan later approves outreach.

1. **Context opener:** "We are studying small backflow compliance-route businesses in [county/state], especially firms with recurring device records and annual testing workflow. We are not a broker and not trying to sell anything."
2. **Respect the operator:** "The interesting part to us is the boring admin: notices, due dates, filings, retests, and customer continuity."
3. **Route-book probe:** "Roughly how many active devices or sites do you track each year, and are those organized by due date?"
4. **Workflow probe:** "Which portals/utilities do you submit through, and who handles the filing?"
5. **Labor probe:** "How many certified testers can handle a normal week without you personally doing every test?"
6. **Mix probe:** "Is the business mostly annual testing, or do repairs/replacements/other plumbing drive the economics?"
7. **Succession-safe close:** "If there were ever a path to take admin/renewal burden off your plate while preserving the customer relationships and team, would that be worth a short follow-up?"

## Exact Kill Criteria

Kill a target immediately if any of these are true:

- Fewer than **500 active devices** and no credible path to tuck-in density.
- No device-level records with due dates; records are only invoices, memory, or customer names.
- Less than **50% of revenue** from recurring testing/certification for a pure-play acquisition thesis.
- Backflow is just a plumbing/fire add-on and cannot be carved out cleanly.
- Owner is the only certified tester and there is no hireable/subcontractor replacement path.
- Certification, plumbing, fire, or municipal-list status is personally tied to the seller and non-transferable without a new licensed operator.
- Average realized price per test is below **$75** with low repair attach and long drive times.
- Repair/replacement work is the majority of EBITDA but not backed by transferable labor/licenses.
- Customer concentration: top 10 customers above **35%** of revenue without contracts or historical retention.
- Seller wants a plumbing-company multiple for a thin backflow route book.
- Portal/reporting burden is high but records are disorganized enough that migration would require rebuilding the book from scratch.
