## PART 1: WHERE WORKING CAPITAL MISPRICING CREATES BUYER EDGES

This is the strategic key to the whole thesis. Working capital mispricing happens when **the seller's stated EBITDA includes working capital that isn't actually "owned" by the business — it's fleeting, seasonal, or already committed.** A sophisticated buyer can negotiate a lower effective purchase price by pointing this out.

### The Five Working Capital Traps (Ranked by Buyer Edge)

| Trap | How It Works | Categories Affected | Buyer Edge |
|------|-------------|---------------------|------------|
| **1. Seasonal Inventory Build** | Seller carries 60-90 days of seasonal inventory at close. "EBITDA" looks fine, but $500K+ of cash is trapped in inventory that won't sell for months. Effective price is higher than headline. | Nurseries, building materials, HVAC, propane, marine | 🔴 HIGH — seller often doesn't model this |
| **2. Accounts Receivable Aging** | "EBITDA" includes revenue booked but not collected. At close, 45-60 day AR means $400-800K of the purchase price is literally uncollected bills. | B2B contractors, distributors, commercial HVAC | 🔴 HIGH — sellers rarely discount for AR risk |
| **3. Equipment Capex Cycle** | Business "throws off cash" because equipment is old and fully depreciated. Buyer inherits $500K-2M of deferred capex. | Print shops, machine shops, laundromats, car washes | 🟡 MEDIUM — visible but often under-negotiated |
| **4. Supplier Deposits / Prepaid Inventory** | Seller has $200-500K tied up in supplier deposits or prepaid inventory (common in import-heavy businesses). These show as "assets" but aren't really liquid. | Jewelry, specialty retail, importers | 🟡 MEDIUM — buried in balance sheet |
| **5. Customer Deposits (Liability)** | Seller has collected $300K-1M in customer deposits for future work. This shows as a liability but seller wants credit for "revenue." | Contractors, marinas, custom manufacturers | 🔴 HIGH — creates direct price negotiation lever |

### Categories Ranked by Working Capital Mispricing Opportunity

**Tier 1 — Highest Mispricing Opportunity (buyers win):**

1. **Wholesale Nurseries / Greenhouse Growers** (FL, NE)
   - Massive seasonal inventory build (spring shipping season)
   - Seller typically values living inventory at cost — but unsold stock depreciates
   - Land + greenhouses + inventory = 3 borrowable asset classes
   - **WC edge: 10-20% of asking price negotiable**

2. **HVAC / Plumbing / Electrical Contractors** (all markets)
   - Underperforming AR is rampant (commercial contractors especially)
   - Equipment is rolling stock (trucks) — depreciating, not appreciating
   - **WC edge: 5-15% of asking price negotiable on AR quality alone**

3. **Marinas / Boatyards** (FL, NE coastal)
   - Seasonal revenue (NE) + customer deposits (summer slips prepaid)
   - Deferred dredging/bulkhead capex is the hidden trap
   - Real estate (waterfront) is the non-reproducible moat
   - **WC edge: 10-15% on deposit true-ups + deferred capex exposure**

4. **Building Materials / Lumber Yards** (NE, FL, DFW)
   - Commodity inventory with price volatility (lumber)
   - Seller often values inventory at recent (high) prices
   - **WC edge: 5-10% on inventory mark-to-market**

5. **Propane / Fuel Oil Distributors** (NE — heating season)
   - Massive seasonal inventory build (winter)
   - Seller wants credit for full-tank inventory at close
   - **WC edge: 10-15% on seasonal inventory timing**

**Tier 2 — Moderate Mispricing:**

6. Commercial printers (capex cycle trap)
7. Independent jewelers (inventory valuation)
8. Equipment rental yards (depreciated fleet)
9. Auto dealers / powersports (floorplan complexity)
10. Laundromats (equipment capex cycle)

**Result:** ~$6.5-7.5M effective price for a $9M ask. That's the edge.

| Source | Amount | Rate | Term | Secured By |

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Purchase price | $10M |
