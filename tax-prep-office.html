Tax Prep Office

Sticky seasonal cash-flow machine with strong recurring client economics, but extreme seasonality (90%+ of revenue in Q1–Q2), IRS regulatory risk, and AI disruption pressure on commodity returns.

Verdict: CONDITIONAL

Sticky seasonal cash-flow machine with strong recurring client economics, but extreme seasonality (90%+ of revenue in Q1–Q2), IRS regulatory risk, and software/AI disruption pressure on commodity returns. Best as acquisition of an established independent practice with EA/CPA talent, diversified into year-round bookkeeping/advisory.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • What is this business? A local office that prepares and files individual and small-business tax returns, typically generating the vast majority of revenue during tax season (January–April) with extensions through October. Many operators also offer bookkeeping, payroll, notary, financial planning, and IRS representation to smooth revenue year-round.
  • Who are the customers? Individuals (W-2 wage earners, retirees, gig workers, investors) and small businesses (sole props, S-corps, partnerships, LLCs). Heavily B2C with a B2B small-business component. ~80 million taxpayers use paid preparers (NSA estimate).
  • Demand drivers: Tax code complexity, aging population, gig economy growth (Schedule C complexity), 5M+ new business applications annually per SBA, and IRS enforcement funding increasing audit risk.
  • Geographic relevance: All three markets work. Boston/NE and NY/CT/NJ have high-income, high-complexity taxpayers. SFL has retirement-driven demand and snowbird/multi-state filing needs.
  • Market size: US tax preparation services ~$14B annually (IBISWorld). ~150,000+ independent preparers, CPAs, and EAs. A single well-located office can generate $300K–$1.5M.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

Good Acquisition Target

  • Revenue: $500K–$1.5M, SDE: $200K–$500K
  • 500+ annual individual returns + 50+ business clients
  • Year-round revenue diversification: bookkeeping, payroll, advisory, IRS representation
  • Staff includes at least one EA or CPA who will stay post-sale
  • Client retention rate >80% year-over-year
  • Established referral pipeline (CPAs, attorneys, financial advisors)

Red Flags

  • Owner is sole preparer and holds all client relationships
  • Revenue declining year-over-year
  • High concentration in a few large business clients
  • No year-round revenue, pending IRS regulatory issues

Franchise candidates: H&R Block (~30% royalty drag), Jackson Hewitt (~25%), Liberty Tax (~20%+). Independent is almost always superior economics — the franchise royalty is a massive margin drag.

5. Competitive Landscape

  • National chains: H&R Block (~$3.5B revenue), Jackson Hewitt (~$500M), Liberty Tax — dominate volume individual returns with brand and walk-in convenience, but margins squeezed by royalty structures.
  • CPA firms: Local and regional CPA firms handle complex returns at higher price points. Most direct competitors for a high-quality independent.
  • Software/TurboTax: Intuit's TurboTax dominates DIY (~40M+ users). AI-driven tools eroding commodity individual return volume — structural risk.
  • Independent preparers: ~150,000+ nationwide. Highly fragmented. Best have deep client relationships and diversified services.
  • PE/consolidation: Regional CPA roll-ups (CBIZ, CLA, Eide Bailly) acquire tax practices as add-ons at 4–6x EBITDA for larger practices.
  • Moat: Client trust and stickiness (people hate switching preparers), professional credentials, complex-return expertise, multi-year tax history knowledge, and referral networks. Strongest for business returns; weakest for simple W-2 filings.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • Deal structure: Asset purchase — client lists, non-compete, office lease, equipment/software, phone number, website. Seller note (15–25%) tied to client retention over 1–2 tax seasons. Earnout based on first post-acquisition season's revenue.
  • SBA eligibility: Strong — professional practices with documented recurring revenue are favored SBA 7(a) borrowers.
  • Down payment at base case ($2.55M): ~$255K at 10% down.
  • Payback period: ~1.5–2.5 years at base case if client retention holds above 80%. Seasonal cash flow means debt service planning is critical.
  • Multiple arbitrage: Buy pure tax-prep at 2.5–3.0x SDE, add year-round bookkeeping/payroll/advisory, build recurring revenue, sell at 4.0–4.5x to a regional CPA roll-up.

7. 90-Day Roadmap (If Dan Pursues This)

Phase 1 · Days 1–30

Market Mapping

Map all independent tax practices and EA/CPA offices in target markets. Scrape BizBuySell, Accounting Practice Sales, ProHorizons, and local listings. Contact 10+ brokers specializing in accounting/tax practice sales. Research EA/CPA hiring market and compensation.

Phase 2 · Days 31–60

Diligence & Modeling

Build three-case acquisition model with seasonal cash-flow timing. Diligence 3–5 targets: verify returns, client count and retention, return-type mix, staff credentials, software stack, and office lease terms. Speak with 2–3 SBA lenders. Interview EA/CPA operator candidates.

Phase 3 · Days 61–90

LOI or Kill

Submit LOI on top target with seller note/retention earnout tied to client retention through first post-acquisition season. Include diligence gates: EA/CPA employment agreement, client verification, software/license transfer, and transition consulting period.

8. Kill Criteria

  • Owner is sole EA/CPA and won't stay post-sale; no other credentialed staff
  • Revenue mix is >80% simple individual returns — AI/software erosion will compress
  • Client retention is below 70% — relationship is with the person, not the practice
  • Revenue declining year-over-year without a clear explanation
  • Office location is poor or lease is untransferable
  • Regulatory issues: IRS PTIN problems, state licensing violations, pending disciplinary actions
  • Seasonality creates cash-flow gaps that debt service can't survive
  • Franchise royalty (if franchise) leaves sub-15% net margin after manager salary
