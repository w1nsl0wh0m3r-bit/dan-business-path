Gold-Tier Diligence Addendum

Why this made the top-100 cut: Grease Trap Pumping / Compliance Route deserves a real diligence memo because the category has at least one structural feature worth underwriting: regulated kitchen compliance, route density, and disposal logistics. The dashboard score is only the entry point. The investment question is whether a local operator can convert that structural demand into durable, manager-run cash flow after replacing the seller, normalizing labor, and paying for real customer acquisition.

The attractive version of the business: The investable version is narrow. It has documented demand, clean books, repeat or recurring customer behavior, and a clear operating cadence that does not require the founder to personally save every job. It has a review moat, a defensible source of leads, and enough gross margin to pay a capable service manager without destroying SDE. It also has a simple customer promise: respond faster, quote cleaner, show up reliably, and make the buyer's problem disappear.

The bad version: The bad version looks similar from a broker memo but collapses under diligence. The seller is the lead generator, estimator, dispatcher, technician, and customer relationship. Revenue is lumpy, pricing is undocumented, employees are friends/family under market wages, and the P&L excludes real marketing, insurance, callbacks, vehicle/equipment replacement, software, or management labor. That is not an acquisition platform; it is buying someone's job with debt.

What must be true

  • Demand is local, visible, and not dependent on one channel or one personality.
  • Gross margin remains attractive after realistic labor, callbacks, warranty, waste, and travel time.
  • There is a repeat/reorder/referral loop — not a pure one-time marketing treadmill.
  • The workflow can be documented into checklists, scripts, pricing rules, and manager KPIs.
  • A buyer can improve the business with systems rather than heroic hustle.

What breaks the thesis

  • Owner is personally responsible for more than half of sales, estimating, or delivery quality.
  • Financials require aggressive add-backs to look attractive.
  • Customer acquisition cost is unknown or rising faster than ticket size.
  • Labor market is too thin to replace the seller or add a manager.
  • Revenue quality is worse than the headline — one-off, seasonal, concentrated, or discount-driven.

Dan-Specific Investment View

Dan should treat Grease Trap Pumping / Compliance Route as a candidate for a very specific type of small-business platform: boring enough to have real demand, fragmented enough to professionalize, and operationally simple enough to understand quickly. The best version is not necessarily the biggest TAM. It is the category where local execution gaps are obvious and the improvement playbook is repeatable: better phones, better pricing, better follow-up, tighter scheduling, clearer reporting, cleaner upsells, and disciplined financial controls.

The category should be downgraded immediately if the path to manager-run ownership is fuzzy. A good small business can be bought at 2–4x SDE and improved. A founder-dependent job should not be bought at any multiple unless Dan wants to operate it personally. The diligence standard is therefore simple: prove that the customer demand survives without the seller, prove that job economics survive with real labor and management cost, and prove that growth can come from systems rather than vibes.

Bottom line: keep this in the top-100 only if the first 3–5 expert calls come back with concrete numbers and no obvious manager-run blocker. If the calls are hand-wavy, if the seller cannot produce clean data, or if the category requires hidden craft expertise that cannot be hired, move it below the line and focus on cleaner recurring-route or counter-distribution concepts.
