Verdict: CONDITIONAL GO

Highly fragmented, recession-resilient category with real recurring revenue via repeat visits. The catch: heavy owner-operator dependency and thin margins if you're just cutting hair. The thesis works only with a booth-rent or commission model that decouples revenue from the owner's chair time, or a multi-unit franchise (Great Clips, Sport Clips, Supercuts) with a manager in place.

1. Category Definition & Demand Map

  • **What is this business?** A hair salon or haircut chain provides hair cutting, coloring, styling, and treatment services. Chains range from value-oriented strip-mall walk-ins (Great Clips, Supercuts) to upscale full-service salons. The key distinction for acquisition purposes: booth-rental vs. commission-based stylist compensation.
  • **Who are the customers?** Overwhelmingly B2C. Women visit every 4-8 weeks; men every 3-6 weeks. The industry skews ~70% female clients by revenue due to higher average tickets (color, treatments). Recurring visits are near-universal — hair doesn't stop growing.
  • **Demand drivers:** Recession-resilient (people don't stop cutting hair), aging population (color services grow), men's grooming trend (more frequent visits, higher tickets). Not discretionary in the traditional sense — it's a recurring necessity.
  • **Geographic relevance:** National category. Strong everywhere. Dense suburban areas with middle-income demographics are the sweet spot for value chains. Upscale salons thrive in affluent suburbs (Westchester, Greenwich, Boca Raton). NE/NY/SFL all viable.
  • **Market size:** US hair care services market ~$50B (estimate, IBISWorld reports US hair salons at ~$48-53B annually). ~1.3M licensed cosmetologists in the US. Highly fragmented — no single operator has >2% market share.

**Key insight:** The compensation model determines everything. Booth-rental salons (landlord model) generate 60-75% gross margin with minimal variable cost. Commission salons (50/50 split typical) have lower margin but can attract better stylists. Value chains (Great Clips) pay hourly + commission.

4. Path-Specific Buybox

What makes a GOOD acquisition target:

  • Revenue range: $350K-$700K
  • SDE range: $50K-$120K
  • Years in business: 5+ (established book of business)
  • Active stylist count: 6+ (revenue diversification)
  • Key assets: cosmetology licenses (transferable), lease terms, client database, equipment condition
  • Lease: 3+ years remaining, favorable terms, salon-use permitted
  • Red flags: single stylist generates >40% of revenue; owner is top producer; equipment >10 years old; Yelp/Google reviews <4 stars

What makes a good FRANCHISE candidate:

  • **Great Clips** — ~4,400 units, $25-35K franchise fee, 6% royalty, ~$150-250K total investment. Item 19: average unit revenue ~$300K. Semi-absentee ownership possible with manager.
  • **Sport Clips** — ~1,850 units, $25K franchise fee, 6% royalty, men's sports theme. Average revenue ~$500K.
  • **Supercuts** — ~2,500 units (Regis Corp), $25-40K franchise fee, 6% royalty. Established brand, walk-in model.
  • **Cost Cutters** — ~500 units, lower investment, value positioning.
  • **Lemon Tree** — ~50 units, NE presence, family-oriented, franchise fee ~$25K.

Franchise economics: thinner margins (royalty + marketing fund = ~8-10% of revenue off the top) but brand, systems, and training. Better for multi-unit scale.

5. Competitive Landscape

**Fragmentation level:** Extremely high. 80,000+ hair salons in the US. Top 10 chains combined have <10% market share.

Key players:

  • **Regis Corp** (NYSE: RGS) — largest salon company, ~5,000 salons (Supercuts, SmartStyle, Cost Cutters). Publicly traded, struggling with same-store sales.
  • **Great Clips** — privately held, largest franchise system, ~4,400 units.
  • **Fantastic Sams** — ~1,200 units, franchise model, NE presence.
  • **Sport Clips** — ~1,850 units, backed by PE (Roark Capital invested in parent).
  • **Drybar** — blowout-only chain, acquired by WellBiz Brands (PE-backed).
  • **Independent salons** — vast majority of market. This is where acquisitions come from.

**PE activity:** Limited at small scale. Roark Capital (Sport Clips parent), WellBiz Brands (Drybar, Elements Massage), and Riverside Partners have rolled up beauty/personal care concepts. Most PE activity is at the $10M+ EBITDA level — well above Dan's target.

**Moat:** Local reputation, stylist retention, location/lease. No meaningful technology moat. Brand matters at franchise level but not for independents.

6. Financing, Valuation & Capital Discipline

  • **Typical deal structure:** Asset sale (equipment, client lists, lease assignment, goodwill). Stock sales rare. Seller notes common (10-30% of price).
  • **SBA eligibility:** Yes, cosmetology salons are SBA 7(a) eligible. License must transfer. SBA requires personal guarantee.
  • **Down payment at Dan's scale:** $25K-$55K on a $250K-$550K acquisition.
  • **Payback period at base case:** ~3-4 years (SDE / total investment).
  • **Multiple arbitrage:** Buy at 3-3.5x single unit → build to 3+ units → sell portfolio at 4-4.5x or higher if franchise-backed.

Capital discipline rules:

  • Never pay >4x SDE for a single-unit salon
  • Verify last 3 years tax returns — cash businesses inflate revenue
  • Walk away if owner is top producer and no stylist will stay post-sale
  • Model for 10-15% revenue decline in Year 1 (transition risk)

8. Kill Criteria

  • Single stylist generates >40% of revenue and won't sign a non-compete
  • Owner is the #1 producer and there's no clear replacement
  • Revenue has declined >15% in last 2 years (structural decline, not COVID blip)
  • Yelp/Google rating <3.5 stars with consistent complaints about quality/cleanliness
  • Lease expiring within 12 months with no renewal option
  • Location is in a declining strip center with dropping foot traffic
  • Can't find a licensed operator partner willing to run day-to-day
  • Booth-rent model where >50% of chairs are vacant at time of acquisition
